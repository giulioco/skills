# Negotiation Prep — One-Sheet Template

Adapted from Chris Voss's *Never Split the Difference* (Appendix). The single tool you should fill out before any meaningful negotiation — pricing call, renewal, contract terms, escalation, salary.

> "You don't rise to the occasion — you fall to your highest level of preparation."

Use this as a worksheet. Print it; bring the printed version into the room. Hand-marked notes and crossed-out lines are evidence to your counterpart that you took this seriously.

---

## The 5-section template

### Section I — The Goal

Write down only the **best-case, optimistic-but-reasonable** target. Specific, written, brought into the room.

**Do NOT anchor on BATNA** — knowing your "walk-away alternative" caps your aim. Goal first; BATNA is your floor, not your aim.

```
What I want (best-case, but defensible):
___________________________________________

Specific number, term, or outcome:
___________________________________________

Why this is reasonable (the case I'd make if challenged):
___________________________________________

What I will NOT accept (BATNA / walk-away):
___________________________________________
```

Discuss the goal with a colleague before the negotiation — once it's spoken, it's harder to wimp out.

---

### Section II — Summary

1–2 sentences capturing the known facts that led to this negotiation. Must be a summary the counterpart would respond to with **"That's right."**

```
Why are we here?
___________________________________________

What do they want? (their goal, in their words)
___________________________________________

What do I want? (my goal, in plain language)
___________________________________________

What's the situation that brings us together?
___________________________________________
```

The summary is what you'll deliver in the first 30–60 seconds of the conversation. If you nail it, the counterpart says "that's right" early — and you're in influence territory before you've made a single ask.

---

### Section III — Labels / Accusation Audit

Anticipate everything they could throw at you — fair or unfair. Distill to **3–5 sharpest possible labels** that pre-empt those accusations.

```
What's the WORST thing they might say or accuse me of?
1. ___________________________________________
2. ___________________________________________
3. ___________________________________________
4. ___________________________________________
5. ___________________________________________

The 3 sharpest labels I'll deliver in the opening:
1. "You may feel like _____________________"
2. "It seems like ________________________"
3. "You're going to think ___________________"
```

**It is theater.** Plan pacing. Plan pauses. Practice out loud. Voss's first-day-of-class trick: "I want to tell you in advance — it's going to be horrible." Always gets more volunteers than he needs. Naming the worst-case makes the actual experience feel like relief.

---

### Section IV — Calibrated Questions

**3–5 questions** designed to:
1. Reveal value to both sides.
2. Identify and overcome deal-killers.
3. Influence the people behind the table you can't see.

Categories to include:

**Discovery questions** (motives beneath stated positions):
- What about this is most important to you?
- How does this fit into what you're trying to accomplish?
- What's the biggest challenge you're facing here?

**Behind-the-table questions** (deal-killers you can't see):
- How does this affect the rest of your team?
- How on board are the people not on this call?
- What do your colleagues see as their main challenges in this area?
- Who else needs to weigh in before we land on something?

**Deal-killer questions** (cost of inaction):
- What happens if we don't reach an agreement here?
- What does doing nothing cost you?
- How does making this deal resonate with what your company prides itself on?

```
My 3-5 calibrated questions for this negotiation:
1. ___________________________________________
2. ___________________________________________
3. ___________________________________________
4. ___________________________________________
5. ___________________________________________

Follow-up label for each (ready to fire after their answer):
1. "It seems like ________________________"
2. "It sounds like ________________________"
3. "It looks like ________________________"
```

Pair every question with a follow-up label. Without the label, the answer goes unprocessed; with the label, the counterpart feels heard and you build toward "that's right."

**Remember:** a high % of negotiations hinge on **non-financial** drivers — self-esteem, status, autonomy, perceived loss, internal political risk. Find ways to make your counterpart look good in the face of change.

---

### Section V — Non-Cash Offers

Inventory the non-monetary items the counterpart could give you that would have value. Ask:

> **"What could they give that would almost get us to do it for free?"**

```
Non-cash items I could ask for:
- Introductions to peers / their network
- Reference call rights / case study rights
- Multi-year lock-in
- Faster timeline / shorter ramp
- Branding rights / logo placement
- Co-marketing collaboration
- Mentorship / advisory role
- Beta access / product feedback partnership
- Speaking slot at their event / conference
- Hiring referrals from their network
- Specific milestones or commitments
- Non-disparagement / exclusivity terms
- Title / scope (in salary negotiations)

My top 3 non-cash asks for THIS negotiation:
1. ___________________________________________
2. ___________________________________________
3. ___________________________________________

What I'd give in exchange:
___________________________________________
```

These are essential for the **Ackerman closer** — when you make your final-stage offer, throw in a non-monetary gift to signal you're at your limit. The reciprocity multiplier is real.

## The Ackerman Bargaining Model (when hard haggling is required)

For situations where the counterpart is squeezing you on price:

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Set your **target** price (your goal). |
| 2 | First offer = **65%** of target. |
| 3 | Calculate three increasing offers: **85%, 95%, 100%**. |
| 4 | Use empathy + many ways of saying "no" between offers, to make them counter before you raise. |
| 5 | Final number: a **precise non-round** number ($37,893 not $38,000). |
| 6 | On the final offer, throw in a **non-monetary item** to signal you're at your limit. |

```
My target: $______________

65% (first offer): $______________
85% (second offer): $______________
95% (third offer): $______________
100% with non-cash gift (final): $______________ + ______________

"No" responses I'll use between offers:
- "How am I supposed to do that?"
- "I'm sorry, that just doesn't work for me."
- "Your offer is generous, but I'm afraid I just can't."
```

**When NOT to use Ackerman:** when the relationship matters more than the deal. Hard anchoring damages long-term trust. For renewal pricing with key accounts, soften — anchor close to your target and use Voss's calibrated questions instead.

## The first 60 seconds — opening choreography

The opening of a negotiation conversation, in order:

1. **Constructive "fair":** "I want you to feel like you're being treated fairly throughout this conversation. So please stop me at any point if you feel I'm being unfair, and we'll address it. Sound good?"

2. **Accusation audit (3 labels):** Deliver the 3 labels from Section III. Pause after each. Let them deny.

3. **Summary (Section II):** Deliver the summary from Section II. Wait for "that's right."

4. **First calibrated question (Section IV):** Hand them the floor. Let them define the conversation.

After this, you're in tactical empathy mode — listen, label, mirror, summarize, propose.

## Real-world example — populated template

**Scenario:** Salary negotiation. You've been offered $115K base for a senior IC role. Market data says $130–145K. Your goal: $140K + signing bonus + early review.

### Section I — Goal

- **Want:** $140K base + $15K signing bonus + 6-month performance review (instead of standard 12-month).
- **Why reasonable:** Glassdoor / levels.fyi P75 for this role/location is $138K base. Two competing offers in hand at $135K and $142K.
- **Walk-away:** $128K base + signing bonus, OR $135K base.

### Section II — Summary

> "Where things stand: you've offered me $115K base for the senior IC role. We've talked about the scope being [X, Y, Z]. From our conversations, it sounds like getting the right person in fast matters more than getting them in cheap, and that the role's scope is meaningfully larger than what the original requisition was written for."

(Designed to elicit "that's right.")

### Section III — Labels

1. "You're going to think I'm being greedy."
2. "It might feel like I'm not appreciating the offer."
3. "You may feel like I'm trying to renegotiate before I've even started."

### Section IV — Calibrated Questions

1. "What about my background made you decide to extend an offer at this level?"
2. "How does the band for this role compare to what you'd offer someone with [my specific differentiated skill]?"
3. "How am I supposed to take this offer over the others I have when the gap is meaningful?"
4. "How would you make sure this hire is set up to succeed in the first 6 months?"

(Question 4 is the "recruit a mentor" move — turns the boss into your ally.)

### Section V — Non-Cash Offers

- Faster start date.
- Open to lateral move into [adjacent team] in 12 months if needed.
- Willingness to be a public reference / case study for the hiring process.
- Shorter PTO accrual / waive guarantee bonus floor.

**Final-stage non-cash gift:** "Happy to commit to delivering [specific 90-day outcome] within the first quarter as part of this — gives you a built-in checkpoint."

## After the negotiation — review

```
What did I get? ___________________________
What did I give? __________________________
What did I learn about their priorities? ____________
What did I learn about their constraints? ___________
What would I do differently next time? _____________
What's the next step, and when? ____________________
```

Top negotiators run this review every time. Most run it never. The compounding effect — over 5 years of deals — is the difference between a senior IC making $200K and one making $400K.

## The 10 principles to internalize

1. **Negotiation = communication with results, not a battle.** Goal is discovery.
2. **Slow it down.** Speed kills rapport.
3. **Subsume your ego.** The other side must feel the solution is theirs.
4. **Listening is the cheapest concession.** And the most powerful.
5. **The adversary is the situation, not the person.** Never make an enemy.
6. **No deal is better than a bad deal.** Be willing to walk.
7. **The Golden Rule is wrong.** Treat them how *they* need to be treated, not how you'd want to be treated.
8. **Yes is nothing without How.** Drive past agreement to implementation.
9. **There is always a team on the other side.** Influence the deal-killers behind the table.
10. **Embrace honest, regular conflict.** It's the only path to truth, creativity, and resolution.

---

## One last thing

**Never split the difference.**

Compromise is wearing one black shoe and one brown shoe. Driven by fear, not goals. You can either close the right deal — or no deal. Both are fine. Splitting the difference rarely is.
