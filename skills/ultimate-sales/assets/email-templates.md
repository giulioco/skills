# Email Templates

Verbatim, adaptable email templates drawn from Blount + Voss + Carnegie + Challenger. Use them as scaffolding; rewrite in the user's voice and the buyer's specific context. **Never send a template unedited** — buyers smell template from the subject line.

The four-element backbone applies to every email below:
1. **Hook** — subject + first sentence get them to keep reading.
2. **Relate** — demonstrate you get them and their problem.
3. **Bridge** — connect their problem to how you can help.
4. **Ask** — clear, specific, easy-to-act-on. Offer a day/time.

Subject line rules: 3–6 words, ≤50 characters, statement (not question), about THEM.

## Cold outbound — first contact

### Template 1: The teaching opener (Challenger)

> **Subject: COO — The Toughest Job in the Bank**
>
> Lawrence,
>
> Ernst & Young recently reported that the COO has the toughest role in the C-suite. The COOs I work with tell me that the increasing complexity of the banking environment has made their job harder and more stressful than ever.
>
> My team and I help COOs like you reduce complexity and stress with strategies to optimize growth and profit, mitigate credit risk, allocate resources effectively, and minimize regulatory surprises.
>
> While I don't know if we are a good fit for your bank, why don't we schedule a short call to help me learn more about your unique challenges? From there we can decide if it makes sense to set up a deeper conversation.
>
> Wednesday at 11 AM or 3 PM your time?
>
> [Sender]

Why it works: opens with their pain in their language (Carnegie), bridges to capability (not features), uses "while I don't know if we're a good fit" (humility, low pressure — Voss-flavored), assumptive ask with two specific times.

### Template 2: The reframe opener (Challenger + Carnegie)

> **Subject: 22% of [thing] is outside your visibility**
>
> [Name],
>
> Most ops leaders we work with start by squeezing the planned 40% of their MRO budget — vendor renegotiation, contract terms. What we found across 60+ companies your size: the *unplanned* 60% (emergency purchases, off-contract spend) runs 22% higher than the negotiated portion. For a company at your scale, that's roughly $X million sitting in a gap nobody can see in real time.
>
> A 30-minute spend audit using your last 12 months of AP data would tell you the actual dollar amount in your case. We've done it for [reference customers] in your space.
>
> Worth 30 minutes Tuesday or Thursday next week?
>
> [Sender]

Why it works: reframes the problem (the unplanned 60%, not the negotiated 40%), uses data (rational drowning), offers a specific low-risk Advance (audit, not a sales meeting).

### Template 3: The compressed Voss + Blount opener (when you have one shot)

> **Subject: Is [your team] still on the [legacy approach]?**
>
> [Name],
>
> Quick one. We help [role] at [type of company] solve [specific problem] without [common pain point]. Three of your peers — [Logo 1, Logo 2, Logo 3] — have moved off [legacy approach] in the last 6 months and saw [specific outcome] inside Q1.
>
> Have you given up on solving [the problem] this year? If not, I have 20 min Wednesday at 2 PM or Thursday at 10 AM.
>
> [Sender]

Why it works: short, hook + relate + bridge + ask in 4 sentences. "Have you given up?" is Voss's non-response email pattern, even on first contact. Forces a "no" (which feels safe) or a "yes, we're still working on it."

## Follow-up after a meeting

### Template 4: Post-discovery summary

> **Subject: Recap — [their company] / next steps**
>
> [Name],
>
> Thanks for the time today. Quick recap of what I heard, so we're aligned:
>
> **Where things are now:**
> - [Current state fact 1]
> - [Current state fact 2]
> - [Current state fact 3]
>
> **What's not working:**
> - [Problem 1] — costing you roughly [impact 1]
> - [Problem 2] — costing you roughly [impact 2]
>
> **What success looks like 12 months out:**
> - [Future state outcome 1]
> - [Future state outcome 2]
>
> Did I get that right? If not, would love to know where I'm off.
>
> Next step: I'll send over a draft business case for your review by [day] — feedback welcome before we walk it through your CFO.
>
> [Sender]

Why it works: drives toward "that's right" (Voss). Confirms current state, problems, impact, future state — the Gap Selling discovery loop. Ends with a specific Advance (calendared).

### Template 5: The thank-you that asks for the Advance

> **Subject: Thank you — and one ask**
>
> [Name],
>
> Genuinely appreciate the time today and the transparency on [specific thing they shared]. The framing on [specific thing] was sharper than what I usually hear at this stage.
>
> One ask — to keep momentum, would it help if I scoped a 14-day pilot focused specifically on [specific outcome]? I can have a draft scope to you by Wednesday.
>
> Either way, thanks again.
>
> [Sender]

Why it works: Carnegie sincerity (specific praise), assumptive Advance proposal, low-risk ("would it help if…"), zero pressure tail ("either way, thanks again").

## Re-engagement (the prospect has gone cold)

### Template 6: The Voss non-response email (the magic one-liner)

> **Subject: [their company]**
>
> [Name],
>
> Have you given up on this project?
>
> [Sender]

Why it works:
- "No"-oriented question — safe answer is "No," which forces engagement.
- Implicit threat to walk away triggers loss aversion.
- Forces them to define their position.
- Voss has used this in cultures (Arabic, Chinese) famous for never saying "No."

Variations:
- "Have you given up on solving [problem] this year?"
- "Have you given up on settling this amicably?"
- "Have you given up on the [project name]?"

### Template 7: The accusation audit re-engager

> **Subject: I've probably been a pain**
>
> [Name],
>
> You're going to read this and think "great, another follow-up email." You probably already know what I'm going to ask, and you've been polite about ignoring me — which I respect.
>
> So I'll just say this: if [project / problem] is genuinely off the table, please tell me and I'll stop. If it's not, but the timing's wrong, just tell me when.
>
> Either way I appreciate the honesty.
>
> [Sender]

Why it works: pre-empts every reason they're avoiding you (Voss accusation audit). Once you've named "you've been polite about ignoring me," the buyer's reflex is to respond. Often: "No, no, we haven't ignored you, we've just been bogged down by Q4…" — which is the engagement you wanted.

### Template 8: The break-up email (the one that gets replies)

> **Subject: Last note from me**
>
> [Name],
>
> I've followed up a few times and haven't heard back, which usually means one of three things:
>
> 1. [Problem] isn't a priority anymore.
> 2. You've solved it another way.
> 3. You're swamped and this fell off your plate.
>
> Whichever it is, I want to respect your time. I'll stop the follow-ups. If anything changes — even a year from now — happy to pick up where we left off.
>
> Best,
> [Sender]

Why it works: gives three "no"-oriented options (Voss). Buyers often reply because (a) they don't want to be seen as the person who ghosted, (b) "you're swamped" is the easiest face-saving option, and (c) the implicit walk-away triggers loss aversion. Reply rates on this format are 2–3× a generic "checking in."

**Important:** mean the breakup. If the buyer doesn't reply, actually stop. Sending another email after this one destroys all the credibility the email built.

## Pricing / negotiation emails

### Template 9: Soft no on a discount ask

> **Subject: Re: pricing**
>
> [Name],
>
> Your offer is generous and I appreciate you being direct about your budget. I'm sorry, that just doesn't work for me at the proposed scope.
>
> If we're stuck on price, the most useful thing I can do is help you figure out what's possible. Two paths:
>
> 1. Same scope, current pricing — we proceed with the proposal as-is.
> 2. Phased scope: we ship [Phase 1] in Q1 at $X (which fits your budget), and add [Phase 2] in Q2/Q3 at $Y. Same outcome, just sequenced.
>
> How would you like me to proceed?
>
> [Sender]

Why it works: "Your offer is generous" + "I'm sorry, that just doesn't work for me" is Voss's softest no. Then offers a non-monetary trade (phasing). Closes with a calibrated question that hands them control.

### Template 10: The Voss anchor + Ackerman email

(For more formal price proposals where you need to set the anchor.)

> **Subject: [Their company] — proposed scope and price**
>
> [Name],
>
> Walking through the conversations we've had with you and [other stakeholders], here's what we believe the right scope is to hit [their stated outcome] inside [their stated timeline]:
>
> [Scope bullets — Outcome → Result Driver → Capability]
>
> Best path forward: we'd start at $X (full retail), but given [reason — multi-year, reference, Q4 timing], we can get to $Y all-in. To make that work, we'd need [specific non-monetary trade — e.g., reference call, case study rights, multi-year lock].
>
> What about this is most important to you? Happy to flex on the trade if there's something that lands better on your end.
>
> [Sender]

Why it works: anchors high (Voss anchoring), names a specific lower number, asks for a non-monetary trade (Ackerman closer), ends with Voss calibrated question.

## Internal / champion enablement

### Template 11: Arming the champion for an internal meeting

> **Subject: For your CFO conversation Friday**
>
> [Name],
>
> Pulled together the one-pager for your CFO conversation. Three things I wanted to flag:
>
> 1. **The number that matters most** — based on what you shared, the cost-of-inaction over 12 months is roughly $X. That's the headline.
> 2. **The question your CFO will likely ask** — "What's the implementation risk?" Page 2 has the de-risking plan and the timeline.
> 3. **The one slide to walk in with** — Slide 3. If you only show one slide, show that one.
>
> If you want, I can do a 15-min walkthrough Thursday afternoon so you're fully comfortable with the math before Friday. Up to you.
>
> [Sender]

Why it works: Carnegie principle 7 (let them feel the idea is theirs). You're handing them the work, but framed so they feel ownership. Specific, prescriptive, and ends with low-pressure offer.

### Template 12: The thank-the-team move (Carnegie)

> **Subject: Quick note re: [team member]**
>
> [Name],
>
> Wanted to send this directly to you — not [team member] — because I think it matters.
>
> [Team member] has been the difference between this deal making sense and not. The framing on [specific thing they did] is genuinely the sharpest I've seen at this stage of a deal, and the way they pushed back on [specific moment] saved us from a worse outcome.
>
> Just thought you should know.
>
> [Sender]

Why it works: pure Carnegie principle 2 (sincere appreciation, public/external recognition of a champion to their boss). Champions who get this email move heaven and earth for you in subsequent renewals.

## Writing checklist (run before sending)

For every outbound email you write, check:

1. [ ] Is the subject line **3–6 words**, statement, about **them** not me?
2. [ ] Could I make this **30% shorter**? (Almost always yes.)
3. [ ] Is the **first sentence** about them, not me?
4. [ ] Did I use the word **"because"** to give a reason for the ask?
5. [ ] Is there a **specific Advance** (calendared time, named outcome)?
6. [ ] Did I avoid the words: "circling back," "checking in," "wanted to follow up," "just touching base," "any update," "synergy," "leverage" (as a verb)?
7. [ ] Did I avoid **questions in the subject line**? (Statements outperform.)
8. [ ] Did I avoid **>1 link**? (Including signature.)
9. [ ] Would the **Carnegie filter** approve — is this about *what they want* or *what I want*?
10. [ ] Could a competitor send the **exact same email**? (If yes, it's too generic — sharpen the reframe.)

## Sequence templates

When the user asks "what should my sequence look like?", these are reasonable starting structures. Adapt to your industry and motion.

### Cold outbound, mid-market B2B (10 days, 7 touches)

| Day | Channel | Type |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Email | Teaching opener (Template 1 or 2) |
| 1 | LinkedIn | Connect with personalized note referencing email |
| 3 | Phone | First call attempt; voicemail if missed |
| 3 | Email | Reply to D1 thread, light "saw you might've missed this" + new angle |
| 6 | Phone | Second call attempt; different time of day; voicemail |
| 8 | LinkedIn | Engage on a recent post they made |
| 10 | Email | Voss non-response email or break-up (Template 6 or 8) |

### Re-engagement of a stalled deal (14 days, 4 touches)

| Day | Channel | Type |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Email | Voss non-response (Template 6) |
| 4 | LinkedIn | Engage on something they posted |
| 7 | Phone | Voicemail referencing a specific recent change in their world |
| 14 | Email | Break-up (Template 8) |

If they don't respond to the break-up: stop. Truly. Add to nurture; revisit in 90 days.

## When in doubt

If you're unsure what to send, the answer is almost always **shorter, more specific, more about them.**

The hardest thing about good sales email is that the right answer is rarely a clever trick. It's: did you actually do the work to know this person and their world? If yes, the email writes itself. If no, no template will save you.
