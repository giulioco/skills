# Call Scripts

Verbatim phone scripts, voicemail templates, and objection turnarounds. Adapted from Blount (Fanatical Prospecting) with Voss-flavored options for high-emotion or stalled situations.

**The principle:** scripts are scaffolding, not chains. Memorize them, then let them dissolve into your own voice. Reading a script is dead-on-arrival; *internalizing* a script is how you sound natural under pressure.

---

## The 5-step phone prospecting framework

Every cold call follows this sequence. **No pauses between steps** — pauses surrender control of the call.

1. **Get attention with their name.** "Hi, Julie."
2. **Identify yourself.** "My name is [X] and I'm with [Company]."
3. **State why you're calling.** "The reason I'm calling is to set up an appointment with you."
4. **Bridge — give them a *because*.** Specific reason, in their language.
5. **Ask for what you want, and shut up.** Assumptive, specific.

**Do NOT say:**
- "How are you doing?" → triggers salesperson alert + brush-off.
- "Is this a good time?" → invites a "no."
- "I was wondering if…" → passive, weak.
- "Do you have a few minutes?" → high-rejection ask.

## Cold call openers (verbatim)

### Opener 1 — Information gathering / appointment setting

> "Hi, Ian, this is [Sender] with [Company]. The reason I'm calling is I read in the paper that you're building a restaurant on the 44 bypass and I want to learn more about your process for purchasing kitchen equipment. Can you tell me how you make those decisions and when the selection process will begin?"

### Opener 2 — Inbound lead conversion

> "Hi, Corrina, this is [Sender] from [Company]. The reason I'm calling is you downloaded our white paper on creating more effective landing pages, and I'm interested to learn what triggered your interest. I work with a number of marketing executives who've been struggling to bring in enough quality leads, and I've got a few best practices I'll be happy to share. Can you tell me more about your situation?"

### Opener 3 — Direct appointment ask (when you have a strong specific bridge)

> "Hi, Lawrence, this is [Sender] with [Company]. The reason I'm calling is several COOs in your space have moved off [legacy] in the last six months and saw [outcome] inside Q1. I'd like to grab 20 minutes to learn what your situation looks like and see if we'd be useful. How about Wednesday at 11 or Thursday at 3?"

### Opener 4 — Voss-flavored re-engagement call

> "Hi, [Name], this is [Sender] with [Company]. Look, you might be thinking 'who is this and why are they calling me' — totally fair. We talked back in [month] about [topic], and I haven't heard from you since. The reason for the call: have you given up on solving [problem]?"

(The "have you given up" is the same Voss non-response trigger from email, in spoken form.)

### Opener 5 — Drop-in / in-person opener

> "Hi, my name is [Sender], I'm with [Company]. The reason I'm here is [neighbor company] next door is one of my customers and they said I should stop in and introduce myself to your owner, [Name]."

OR

> "Hi, my name is [Sender], I'm with [Company]. The reason I stopped by is I provide my service to several of the businesses in this industrial park and I wanted to learn more about your company and situation to see whether or not working with you might be a good fit."

## Voicemail framework (5 steps, ≤30 seconds)

Doubles your callback rate vs. unstructured voicemails.

1. **Identify yourself** — name + company.
2. **Say your phone number twice — slowly.** UP FRONT, before anything else. They might delete the rest, but they have your number.
3. **State the reason for the call.**
4. **Give them a reason to call back** — curiosity, value, insight.
5. **Repeat your name and number twice again** at the end.

### Voicemail script (verbatim)

> "Hi, Rick, this is [Sender] from [Company]. My phone number is 555-360-2249, that's 555-360-2249. The reason I'm calling is you downloaded our white paper on cold calling and I want to learn more about your situation and what triggered you to seek out this information. I also have some additional resources on voicemail messages and phone prospecting I thought you might be curious to learn about. Let's get together this week. Give me a call back at 555-360-2249, that's 555-360-2249."

### When NOT to leave a voicemail

Don't waste 20–30 seconds × 100 dials/day on cold low-value prospects. Leave voicemails when:
- It's a conquest account.
- It's an in-window prospect.
- It's a high-value referral.
- You have a specific recent trigger event to reference.

For everyone else, just hang up and try again later.

## RBO turnarounds (Anchor → Disrupt → Ask)

Three-step framework. **Avoid the phrase "I understand"** — it's filler that telegraphs you weren't listening.

### "Look, I'm busy."

> **Anchor:** "Nancy, that's exactly why I called."
> **Disrupt:** "I figured you would be, so I want to find a time that's more convenient for you."
> **Ask:** "How about we get together next Wednesday at 3:00 PM instead?"

### "We're not interested."

> **Anchor:** "You know, that's what a lot of my current clients said the first time I called."
> **Disrupt:** "Most people say they aren't interested before they see how much they could save. I don't know if my service will be a good fit for you, but doesn't it make sense for us to at least get together for a short meeting to find out?"
> **Ask:** "How about Friday at 2:00 PM?"

### "We're really happy with our current provider."

> **Anchor:** "That's fantastic!"
> **Disrupt:** "Anytime you're getting great rates and great service, you should never think about changing. All I want to do is come by and get to know you a little better. Even if it doesn't make sense to do business with me at the moment, I can give you a competitive quote that will help you keep those other guys honest."
> **Ask:** "How about I come by on Tuesday at 11:30 AM?"

### "Just send me some information."

> "Tell me specifically what you're looking for."

(Calls the bluff. Forces engagement. If they can't articulate it, they weren't going to read your info anyway.)

### "I'm not interested."

> "That makes sense. Most people aren't."

(Disrupts their pattern by agreeing. Often they'll continue: "What is it you're calling about, exactly?")

### "We're under contract."

> **Anchor:** "Got it — and that's exactly why I'm calling now and not in 11 months."
> **Disrupt:** "Most of the customers we work with switched at renewal because the conversation we had 8–10 months ahead of time gave them the data they needed to push for a better deal — either with us or with their existing vendor. I just want to be on your radar before that conversation starts."
> **Ask:** "Can we get 20 minutes on the calendar in the next two weeks so I can show you what those customers found?"

### "We don't have budget."

> **Anchor:** "Of course — I hear that from almost everyone we eventually end up working with."
> **Disrupt:** "What we typically find is the budget conversation is downstream of the urgency conversation. If [problem] is a top-3 priority, budget tends to materialize. If it's not, no amount of price flexibility on our side will make it real for you. So instead of talking price, can I show you what [3 reference customers] in your space did once they saw the actual cost-of-inaction number?"
> **Ask:** "20 minutes Thursday or Friday?"

### "Send me a proposal first."

> "Happy to — but proposals built without enough discovery are usually wrong, and wrong proposals waste both our time. The fastest path is a 25-minute call where I learn enough about [their situation] to write you a proposal that's actually useful. Tuesday or Wednesday work?"

### "Call me back in [3 / 6] months."

> **Anchor:** "Got it — happy to."
> **Disrupt:** "Quick question before I disappear: what changes between now and then? Is there a specific event or decision driving the timing? Just want to make sure I show up at the right moment."
> **Ask:** "And — would it be useful to get one piece of value from me in the meantime so the conversation in [N] months is faster?"

### "I'm going to have to think about it."

> "Fair. What specifically would you want to think through? If I can be helpful with any of it now, happy to — and if I can't, I'll get out of your way."

(Surfaces the real concern hiding behind the brush-off.)

## When the horse is dead, dismount

After two RBOs and no progress, move on graciously. Don't fight.

> "Got it. Sounds like the timing isn't right. I won't keep pushing — if anything changes, you have my number. Thanks for being direct."

The card on Blount's desk for 25 years: **NEXT.** Beating a dead horse is self-destructive.

## Discovery call openers (after meeting is booked)

The 5-element opener for a first discovery call:

1. **Reaffirm time and agenda.** "Thanks for the time today. Plan is for us to spend about 25 minutes — first 5 on context, next 15 on your world, last 5 on whether and how we move forward."
2. **State your hypothesis (Challenger Warmer).** "Before we dive in: we work with [N] companies like yours, and the patterns we see at this stage are usually [Challenge 1, Challenge 2, Challenge 3]. Curious if any of those resonate, or if your situation is different."
3. **Hand them the floor.** Buyer talk-time should now go to ~70%.
4. **Listen for problems, impacts, root causes.** Use SPIN sequence — Problem → Implication → Need-payoff. Voss labels when emotion enters.
5. **Close with a specific Advance proposal.** "Based on what you've shared, the most logical next step would be [specific action]. Could we get [calendared time] booked for that?"

### Discovery call closer (driving toward "that's right")

Before proposing the next step, summarize:

> "Let me play back what I'm hearing, and tell me where I'm off:
> - Where you are now: [current state in their words].
> - What's not working: [problem 1, problem 2, problem 3].
> - What that's costing you: [impact, in their numbers].
> - Where you want to be 12 months out: [future state in their words].
> Did I get that right?"

If they say **"that's right"** — proceed to the Advance.
If they say **"yeah but…"** — let them correct you. Don't argue. Update your understanding, summarize again.

## Demo openers and structure

A demo without discovery is a feature dump. Even when scheduled as a "demo," open with a 5-min recap.

> "Before I show anything: based on our last conversation, what I want to demonstrate is specifically how we'd address [Problem 1] and [Problem 2]. Anything changed since we last talked, or any other priority I should weave in?"

(Builds the demo around their explicit needs — Rackham's "Benefits only exist after Explicit Needs.")

Demo rules:
1. **No demo without discovery.** Ever.
2. **No "if" demos** — don't show what your product *could* do; show what it *does* against their actual stated need.
3. **≤6 features.** A great demo shows few things deeply, not many things briefly.
4. **Anchor on their data when possible.** A demo with their logo and their numbers in it is 10× more memorable than a generic walkthrough.

End every demo with:

> "What about that landed for you? And what's missing — what would you still need to see to feel confident this is the right fit?"

(Calibrated questions, Voss. Surfaces concerns before the close.)

## QBR / customer call scripts

### Opening a renewal-risk QBR

> "Thanks for the time today. I want to be direct — I know the last quarter didn't go the way either of us wanted. Before we get into anything else, I want to acknowledge what didn't work and own my part of it. Then I want to listen to what you've experienced, and only then talk about what we do differently going forward."

(Carnegie Principle 3: admit mistakes quickly and emphatically. Voss accusation audit.)

### Opening a healthy renewal QBR

> "Three things I wanted to cover today: where things stand vs. what we set out to do, what's working that we should double down on, and what's evolving in your world that we should adapt to. Sound right, or anything else you want to add to the agenda?"

### When a customer escalates / threatens to churn

> "I hear you. I don't blame you for feeling that way — if I were you I'd be saying the exact same thing. Before I respond, I want to make sure I understand all of it. Walk me through what's happened from your perspective."

(Carnegie: "I don't blame you one iota for feeling as you do." Then *listen* for at least 5 minutes before responding.)

After they've vented:

> "Here's what I'm hearing — tell me where I'm off…"

(Drive toward "that's right.")

Then own:

> "You're right that we [specific mistake]. That's on us. Here's what I commit to changing, and here's what I need from you to make it work."

## Negotiation by phone

### Opening a price negotiation call

> "I want you to feel like you're being treated fairly throughout this conversation. So please stop me at any point if you feel I'm being unfair, and we'll address it. Cool?"

(Voss's constructive use of "fair.")

### When they push hard on price

> "$[Price]?"
> *[silence — count to 4]*

(Mirror. Then shut up. Almost always they'll fill the silence with the real constraint.)

### When they go silent on price

> "How am I supposed to do that?"
> *[silence]*

(Forces empathy. Voss's single most useful phrase.)

### When you need to walk away or threaten to

> "I want to make sure I'm being honest with you. At the price you're asking, this isn't a deal that works for either of us long-term — and I'd rather not start a relationship that's already underwater. What about this is most important to you, and is there a way we get there without the price piece?"

## Internal call scripts (with reps, managers, champions)

### Coaching a rep on a stalled deal

> "Walk me through where this deal is. Specifically: what's the current state, what's the problem, what's the impact, what's the root cause, and what's the next concrete step you've asked for. Don't tell me 'they're thinking about it' — tell me what action they've committed to and when."

(Forces the rep to use the discovery loop language and produces an actual diagnosis.)

### Asking a champion to walk you through internal politics

> "I want to ask a question that might be uncomfortable: who on your side wants this *not* to happen? Who's the most likely person to derail it, and what would they say if they were trying to kill it? I'd rather get ahead of it now than be surprised in week 4."

(Voss behind-the-table calibrated question. Most reps never ask. Champions usually love it because it shows you're serious.)

## Tone and voice

How you say it matters more than what you say. Voss's three voice modes:

- **Late-night FM DJ voice** (slow, low, calm) — for de-escalation, tense moments, asserting control.
- **Positive / playful** (smile audible) — DEFAULT for everything else.
- **Direct / assertive** — almost never. Only when other modes have failed and you must signal hard limits.

**Smile before you dial.** It carries through the phone in your voice. Studies show prospects can hear the smile.

**The phone-tone diagnostic:** if you can hear nervousness in your voice, the prospect can. Stand up. Take a breath. Pretend you're calling a peer, not a target.

## What to do after every call

Plan-Do-Review (Rackham):

1. **Did I achieve my call objective?** (Specific Advance, calendared.)
2. **What would I do differently if I were doing this call again?**
3. **What did I learn that influences future calls on this account?**
4. **What did I learn that I can use elsewhere?**

Top performers do this for *every* meaningful call. Most reps do it never. The compounding effect over 12 months is enormous.

## The one-line summary

**Ask, then shut up. The buyer's silence is the buyer thinking. Don't rescue them from it.**
