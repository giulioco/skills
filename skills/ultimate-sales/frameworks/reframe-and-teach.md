# Reframe and Teach

The Challenger Sale's most important contribution: in B2B, **how you sell drives 53% of customer loyalty** — more than brand, product, and service combined. The single highest-ranked sales-experience attribute is "the rep offered me a unique perspective on the market." Not "the rep was responsive," not "the rep was easy to work with." **The rep taught me something.**

This means your job in the demonstrating-capability stage is not to *show your product*. It's to *teach the buyer something they don't already know about their own business* — and connect that teaching to a capability where you outperform competitors.

This document covers: when to reframe, what makes a reframe great, the 6-step Commercial Teaching choreography, and how to deliver a reframe without violating Carnegie's never-tell-them-they're-wrong rule.

---

## The teaching imperative

Three datapoints from CEB research:

1. **53% of B2B loyalty** = sales experience itself. (Brand+Product+Service combined = 38%.)
2. **Challengers = ~40% of stars overall, ~54% of stars in complex sales.** Relationship Builders = 7% of stars, near 0% in complex sales.
3. **Customer's top-ranked sales attribute:** "rep offers me unique and valuable perspectives on the market."

The implication: you can be the most likable rep in the territory and still lose if you can't teach. Carnegie alone doesn't close complex deals. Reps who *only* build relationships ("how was your weekend?", "let me know how I can help") underperform reps who challenge.

---

## What a reframe is — and isn't

A **reframe** is an unexpected insight about the buyer's business that makes them say:

> "Huh. I never thought about it that way."

A reframe is **not**:

- Restating what they already know in your words. (That's a paraphrase.)
- Asking them what their problem is. (That's discovery.)
- Telling them about your features. (That's a pitch.)
- Validating their existing thesis. (That's flattery.)

**Failure mode:** if the buyer reacts to your "insight" with "Yes, I totally agree, that's exactly what we're working on!" — **you have failed**. You have not reframed; you've only confirmed their current thinking. At best you're "teaching at the margins" — and the buyer has probably already started thinking about solutions (likely from your competitor).

The right reaction: thoughtful pause. "I hadn't thought about it that way." Or "What else don't I know?"

## The four rules of Commercial Teaching

Teaching that doesn't reliably win the deal is *free consulting* — it benefits the buyer but not you. **Commercial Teaching** is teaching that produces a commercial win.

### Rule 1: Lead to your unique strengths

Every insight must connect back to a capability where YOU outperform.

The Deb Oler test (W.W. Grainger):

> "Why should our customers buy from us over anyone else?"

If the answer is generic ("we have great service," "we're customer-focused"), you don't have a teaching pitch — you have a brochure. CEB found that only **14% of B2B companies' claimed unique benefits** are perceived by customers as both unique AND beneficial. "Innovative," "customer-focused," "green," "solutions-oriented" are not among them.

The reframe must walk the buyer down a path where your unique capability is the answer. If a competitor could deliver against your reframe just as well, you're teaching the buyer into someone else's solution.

### Rule 2: Challenge customers' assumptions

The keyword is **reframe**.

Speak directly to the customer's world in a way that makes them say, "Huh, I've never thought about it that way." If you and the customer agree on the framing of the problem before you've taught them anything, you're competing on price.

> "Rapport and reframe are not the same thing. Just because you 'get' the customer's business doesn't mean you automatically get the customer's business." — Dixon & Adamson

### Rule 3: Catalyze action

Changing how customers think isn't enough — get them to act. Build a business case that creates real discomfort about the current state.

The right ROI calculator measures **the cost of inaction on the reframe**, NOT the ROI of buying your product. The buyer must buy the *problem* before they can buy your *solution*.

### Rule 4: Scale across customers

Teach the same insight to many customers (with diagnostic questions to map the right insight to the right buyer). Marketing builds the insights at scale; reps deliver and tailor. The "everyone teaches their own pitch" approach fails — quality is uneven, and reps promise things the company can't deliver.

## The 6-step Commercial Teaching pitch (the choreography)

A world-class teaching conversation is a **story**, not a presentation. It moves through 6 discrete steps with drama and a clear arc — from a dark place (the buyer's problem reframed worse than they thought) to the light at the end of the tunnel (your solution).

### Step 1: The Warmer

**Goal:** demonstrate "I understand your world" without making them educate you.

After formalities, state YOUR assessment of the customer's key challenges before they speak. This is **Hypothesis-Based Selling.**

**Template:**

> "We've worked with [N] companies similar to yours, and we've found that these three challenges come up again and again as the most painful: [Challenge 1], [Challenge 2], [Challenge 3]. Is that what you're seeing too, or would you add something else to the list?"

Why this works: customers are tired of being interrogated. Senior buyers especially want a "get" not a "give" — show them you've done your homework, then ask them to validate or correct.

**Avoid:** generic "tell me about your business" openers. They signal you didn't prepare.

### Step 2: The Reframe

**Goal:** the moment of "Huh, I never thought of it that way."

Connect the challenges from Step 1 to a deeper, less-obvious framing. Examples:

- W.W. Grainger reframe: companies think they spend too much on direct materials → actually, the bigger waste is the **unplanned MRO purchases** scattered across departments and locations. (Setup for Grainger's "Power of Planning the Unplanned.")
- ADP reframe: HR teams think turnover is a recruiting problem → actually, it's a *front-line management* problem (most departures happen because of the boss, not the job). Setup for ADP's manager-coaching tools.
- Office furniture reframe: companies buy furniture by the desk → actually, real estate cost is 5–10× furniture cost, so buying furniture that lets you fit more people per square foot is the lever.

**Test for a real reframe:** does the buyer pause? Or do they say "yeah we know"?

If they say "yeah we know," your reframe is too obvious. Sharper insight needed.

### Step 3: Rational Drowning

**Goal:** quantify the cost of the current state with data so it can't be ignored.

Drop a credible stat or analysis that shows the size of the problem. Numbers, not adjectives. "67% of our customers in your industry have this problem" beats "many companies struggle with this." Specific, sourceable, recent.

This is where most reps go too short. The buyer needs to feel the math.

### Step 4: Emotional Impact

**Goal:** turn the data into a felt experience.

Tell a story — a customer who had this exact problem, what it cost them, what their week looked like, how the CFO reacted, what happened to the team. Pain Chain narrative.

Voss's principle applies: emotion drives decisions. The data + story combo is more persuasive than either alone. Data without story is forgotten; story without data is dismissed as anecdote.

### Step 5: A New Way

**Goal:** show what *different* would look like — but not yet your product.

What would the world look like if the reframed problem were solved? What capabilities would be required? What would the buyer's week look like, what would their CFO say, what would their team feel?

Critically: this is *capability-level*, not product-level. You're describing the *shape* of the right solution. If you've done it well, the buyer is now mentally evaluating their current vendor against this shape — and finding their vendor short.

### Step 6: Your Solution

**Goal:** finally connect to your product, against the spec you just built.

Now and only now. Your solution maps to the new way you laid out — feature-by-feature, capability-by-capability. The buyer's reaction should be "of course, that's exactly what we need" — because you spent steps 1–5 building exactly that spec.

**The arc:** start dark (their world is worse than they thought) → rational + emotional weight → up the slope to the new way → land on your product as the inevitable answer.

## How to deliver a reframe without violating Carnegie

Carnegie says: never criticize, never tell them they're wrong, never argue. Challenger says: challenge assumptions, take control, be comfortable with constructive tension.

**These are not in conflict.** The resolution is *manner.* You challenge the *idea* while honoring the *person.*

**Bad (violates Carnegie):**

> "Most companies make the mistake of thinking [X] is the issue. They're wrong. The real issue is [Y]."

**Good (Challenger + Carnegie):**

> "What we've seen across [N] companies is that [X] is what most teams focus on first — and for good reason; it's the most visible. But the data we've collected suggests the bigger lever is actually [Y]. I'd love to walk you through what we've seen and get your read."

The reframe is identical. The first version makes the buyer feel they've been an idiot for thinking about [X]. The second version positions [X] as a reasonable starting place and elevates them to co-investigator on [Y]. They thank you for the insight.

**The Challenger reframe template:**

> "Most [type of buyer] we work with start by thinking about this as [obvious framing], and that makes sense. But what the data shows — and what surprised us when we dug in — is that the real driver of [outcome] is [reframed framing]. The teams that actually move the needle on this turned out to be the ones who [shift in approach]."

Notice:
- Honors the obvious framing as reasonable (Carnegie).
- Names the surprise (Challenger).
- Uses data, not assertion (Rational Drowning preview).
- Frames the alternative as what works (Catalyze Action preview).
- Doesn't yet name your product.

## When to reframe — and when not to

**Reframe when:**

- The buyer's stated problem isn't the real problem (Keenan's headache/brain tumor — they think they need a charger, but the real problem is a battery-draining case).
- The buyer has been pitched a competitor's framing and you need to break it.
- The buyer is a sophisticated executive who has heard every standard pitch before.
- You're in a complex sale and you need to *create* urgency that doesn't yet exist.

**Don't reframe when:**

- The buyer is already aligned with the right framing and just needs help executing. (You're past teaching; you're now in delivery.)
- You don't have a real insight. Faking it produces "yeah we know" and burns trust.
- The buyer is junior and not the decision-maker. Save the reframe for the meeting with the executive sponsor.
- You don't have data or a story to back it up. Reframes without ammunition land as opinions.

## The reframe construction worksheet

Use this when building a reframe for a specific buyer or segment.

```
1. What is the buyer's CURRENT framing of the problem?
   (What do they think the issue is?)

2. What is the FACTUAL framing — what data shows the real driver?
   (Where does the buyer's framing fall short?)

3. What's the BRIDGE — the move from current framing to factual framing?
   (Often: "X matters, but Y is where the real lever is.")

4. What's the DATA that supports the reframe?
   (Specific, sourceable, recent.)

5. What's the STORY — a customer who lived this?
   (Concrete, named industry, named outcome.)

6. What's the NEW WAY — capability shape, not product features?

7. How does YOUR solution map to that new way?
   (1:1 capability mapping. Not feature dump.)

8. Test: would the buyer say "Huh, I never thought of it that way" — or "yeah we know"?
   (If the latter, sharpen the reframe.)

9. Test: could a competitor deliver against this reframe just as well?
   (If yes, you're teaching them into someone else's solution. Sharpen.)

10. Test: is this Carnegie-compatible — does it honor the buyer's intelligence?
    (If you're making them feel dumb, rephrase.)
```

## Real-world reframes (drawn from the books)

**W.W. Grainger — "The Power of Planning the Unplanned":**
- Customer thinks: MRO spend is too high overall.
- Reframe: 40%+ of MRO spend is *unplanned* — emergency purchases at premium prices, decentralized buyers, no consolidation.
- New way: visibility tools + supplier consolidation that catches unplanned spend before it happens.
- Grainger's solution maps perfectly because they're the only national distributor with the breadth to consolidate.

**ADP — Manager Coaching:**
- Customer thinks: turnover is a recruiting problem (HR's job).
- Reframe: most departures happen in the first 18 months because of the manager, not the job. Front-line managers are undertrained.
- New way: manager-coaching tooling integrated with HRIS.
- Maps to ADP's strength because they own the HRIS data.

**Office furniture — "Square Foot Cost":**
- Customer thinks: furniture should be cheap.
- Reframe: real estate cost is 5–10× furniture cost; furniture that lets you fit 25% more people per floor pays for itself in 6 months.
- New way: density-optimized layouts.
- Maps to a vendor that has space-planning expertise as well as furniture.

**Pharma — "Patient Journey":**
- Customer (hospital) thinks: treatment efficacy is the metric that matters.
- Reframe: ~30% of patients drop out of the prescribed treatment path due to friction (pickup logistics, side-effect education, refill timing). Treatment efficacy is moot if patients don't stay on it.
- New way: end-to-end patient-journey support, not just the drug.
- Maps to pharma cos that have invested in patient-support services.

The pattern: each reframe takes a problem the buyer thinks they understand, exposes a *bigger or different* angle the buyer hadn't seen, and connects to a capability the seller uniquely has.

## The relationship between Reframe, Tailor, Take Control

The three T's of Challenger only work in combination:

- **Teach without Tailor** = irrelevant ("interesting insight, doesn't apply to us").
- **Tailor without Teach** = sounds like every other vendor ("we understand your industry").
- **Take Control without Teach** = annoying (pushy without value).
- **All three together** = the Challenger profile that wins 54% of star deals in complex sales.

Tailoring layers:

1. **Industry** — speak the vertical's language.
2. **Company** — name their specific situation.
3. **Role** — CFO cares about cash flow; CRO cares about pipeline; IT cares about integration.
4. **Individual** — know the human in front of you.

The "Outcome → Result Driver → Capability" chain (CEB):
- Outcome: the business result the company wants.
- Result Driver: the lever that produces that outcome.
- Capability: the vendor capability that operates the lever.

Always show the chain. Never start with capability.

## Take Control — being comfortable with tension

Challengers don't wait to be invited to talk about price, scope, or pushback. They lead.

Three misconceptions about taking control (CEB):

1. *It means being aggressive.* No — assertive ≠ aggressive. The Challenger is firm, not hostile.
2. *It only happens in negotiation.* No — Challengers take control throughout the sale, including in discovery (deciding which questions matter, redirecting unproductive topics) and in pricing (refusing to discount without value tradeoffs).
3. *It works without teaching.* No — taking control without teaching is just being annoying. The Teach earns the right to Take Control.

Three signs you're taking control well:
- You're comfortable bringing up money before the buyer does.
- You'll redirect a buyer who's chasing the wrong squirrel.
- You'll push back on scope creep with a "no" that doesn't kill the deal.

Three signs you're not:
- You let the buyer set every meeting agenda.
- You discount because they asked, without trading for something.
- You feel queasy when the buyer pushes back, and concede prematurely.

## A complete teaching pitch — the W.W. Grainger example

(Compressed from the Challenger Sale chapter.)

**Warmer:**
> "Most ops leaders we work with tell us their MRO budgets are getting squeezed — and that they think the answer is renegotiating with their main suppliers. We've worked with 60+ companies your size and found that's where most teams start. Is that consistent with what you're seeing?"

[Buyer agrees.]

**Reframe:**
> "Here's what surprised us: when we dug into a sample of 200 companies' actual MRO spend, the *negotiated* portion of MRO — the part you and your buyers manage — turned out to average around 40%. The other 60% was *unplanned* purchases. Emergency replacements. Different departments using different vendors. Items bought at full retail because someone needed it now. None of that is in your contracts."

**Rational drowning:**
> "Across that sample, unplanned MRO ran 22% higher than planned — same items, same SKUs, same suppliers, just bought outside the negotiated channel. For a company your size, that's roughly $X million a year sitting in the gap between what you negotiated and what your team actually buys."

**Emotional impact:**
> "One of the customers we worked with — call them Acme Manufacturing — found out at the end of Q3 that they'd blown through their MRO budget by 18%. Their CFO came to the ops director, asked what happened. He didn't have an answer because his team had never been able to see the unplanned purchases until they hit the AP system. He spent two months reconstructing where the money went. It was career-affecting."

**A new way:**
> "What the teams who fixed this did differently was that they stopped trying to negotiate harder on the planned 40%. They got visibility into the unplanned 60%. They consolidated the long tail of suppliers, put the unplanned spend into a single channel, and added a hard guardrail in their procurement system. Suddenly, the unplanned purchases were either consolidated under negotiated rates or flagged before they happened."

**Your solution:**
> "That's exactly what Grainger built around. We're the only national distributor with the breadth to consolidate the long tail of MRO into one supplier, and we have the integration with [systems] to capture the unplanned spend the moment it happens. That's why our customers in this category typically see 12–18% reduction in total MRO within the first two quarters — not from harder negotiation, but from closing the gap on the unplanned 60%."

**The Advance:**
> "The next step that's been most useful for the teams we've helped is a 30-minute spend audit — we'd pull a sample of the last 12 months of your MRO and quantify your planned vs. unplanned mix. We'd come back with the specific dollar amount you're losing on unplanned purchases. From there you can decide if it's worth a deeper conversation. Could we get that scheduled for next Wednesday?"

Notice:
- 6 steps, in order.
- No feature list.
- Ends on a specific, calendared Advance.
- Reframe is real (the unplanned-spend insight is non-obvious).
- Carnegie-compatible (never says the buyer was dumb).
- Voss-compatible (story drives emotion).

## One last thing

The hardest part of being a Challenger is **giving up the urge to be liked**.

Carnegie reps optimize for likability. Buyers like them. Buyers also call them when they're ready to buy — *from someone else.*

Challengers are respected, not always loved. They take stances. They reframe. They challenge. The buyers who buy from them often say things like "they're tough but they made me think differently."

Carnegie's rules still apply — never insult, never criticize, never argue. But Challenger adds: **be willing to be the rep who tells the truth even when it's uncomfortable.** That tension is the cost of being remembered as a teacher, not a vendor.
