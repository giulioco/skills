# Tactical Empathy — The Voss Negotiation Toolkit

This document is the working version of Chris Voss's *Never Split the Difference* applied to sales: pricing pushback, contract terms, stalled deals, hostile buyers, internal escalations, and any moment where emotional content matters more than rational argument.

The premise: **emotions are not obstacles to negotiation. They are the means.** Rational arguments don't move emotional brains. The fastest path through a hard negotiation is through the emotional layer, not around it.

---

## The mindset shift

Most salespeople treat negotiation as a battle of arguments — make your case, defend your case, find a compromise. Voss's research (FBI hostage negotiation; you can't compromise with a kidnapper) found this approach fails reliably under pressure.

Replace it with:

| Old mindset | Voss mindset |
|---|---|
| Negotiation is a battle | Negotiation is communication with results |
| Goal is to win | Goal is discovery |
| The other side is the adversary | The *situation* is the adversary; never make the person an enemy |
| Compromise is mature | Compromise is "wearing one black shoe and one brown shoe" |
| "Yes" is the goal | "That's right" is the goal; "no" starts the negotiation |
| Logic persuades | Emotion persuades; logic justifies |
| Speed wins | Slowing down wins |

The Behavioral Change Stairway Model (BCSM) — the FBI's actual progression:

**Active Listening → Empathy → Rapport → Influence → Behavioral Change.**

You cannot skip steps. Most sales reps try to jump from Active Listening to Influence (or worse, start at Influence). It doesn't work. The middle layers — Empathy and Rapport — are the entire engine.

---

## Tactical empathy: the umbrella concept

**Tactical empathy** = recognizing the other side's perspective AND vocalizing that recognition, in order to increase your influence in everything that follows.

It is **not**:
- Sympathy (agreeing with their values).
- Pity.
- Capitulation ("I see your point, I'll cave").
- Manipulation through fake feeling.

It **is**:
- Listening for the *underlying* emotion (not just the words).
- Naming that emotion out loud, neutrally.
- Letting silence do the work after you name it.

Why it works (neuroscience): when emotions are *named*, brain activity shifts from the amygdala (fear, threat) to areas governing rational thinking. Naming the emotion drains its intensity. Lieberman's fMRI study confirmed this. You can literally see the buyer's stress drop on a brain scan when they hear their feeling labeled.

The cheapest concession in any negotiation is **listening**. It costs nothing and signals more than any concession on price.

## The five core tools (in order of when to use them)

### 1. Mirroring

**The technique:** repeat back the last 1–3 critical words of what the other person just said. As a question (slight upward inflection). Then **shut up for at least 4 seconds.**

**Why it works:**
- Triggers the unconscious imitation instinct ("we're alike, trust me").
- Buys you time without offering content.
- Encourages them to elaborate and reword (they will never repeat exactly the same way).
- Less aggressive than "What do you mean by that?" which incites defensiveness.

**Example:**

> Buyer: "Look, the price is just way too high for what we're trying to do here."
> You: "Way too high?"
> [silence]
> Buyer: "I mean, our budget got cut last quarter and we're being told to do more with less, and even the $40K we'd budgeted for this is going to be a stretch — I'd need a really compelling reason to push for the original $60K."

You just got: the real budget ($40K), the political constraint (recent cut), and the path to escalation (a "really compelling reason"). All from a 2-word mirror and silence.

**The four-step pit-bull defusal sequence** (when someone's escalating):
1. Late-night FM DJ voice (slow, low, calm).
2. Start with "I'm sorry…"
3. Mirror.
4. Silence — at least 4 seconds.
5. Repeat as needed.

**Trap:** after mirroring, **shut up**. Don't follow up with a question. Voss has watched himself blow his own mirrors by not respecting the silence. Let it work.

### 2. Labeling

**The technique:** verbally identify the underlying emotion. Three opening phrases:

- "It seems like…"
- "It sounds like…"
- "It looks like…"

**Never start with "I"** — it puts the other side's guard up and signals you're focused on yourself. The phrases above are neutral and invite responsiveness.

**Form labels for both layers:**
- **Presenting** behavior (what's visible).
- **Underlying** feeling (what's motivating).

Always label the *underlying.* The cranky grandfather isn't cranky — he's lonely. The defensive procurement officer isn't aggressive — she's been burned before.

**Examples:**

| Surface signal | Bad label (presenting) | Good label (underlying) |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer pushes hard on price | "It seems like you want a discount." | "It seems like you've been burned by vendors who hid costs." |
| Champion goes silent | "It seems like you've been busy." | "It seems like there's something internal that's making this harder than expected." |
| Procurement adds clauses | "It seems like you want stronger terms." | "It seems like there's been a vendor incident that made the legal team nervous." |
| CFO cuts the budget | "It seems like budget's tight." | "It seems like you're getting pressure from above to show savings this quarter." |

**Fill-in-the-blank label templates** (build your own bank for your industry):

- It seems like _____ is valuable to you.
- It seems like you don't like _____.
- It seems like you value _____.
- It seems like _____ makes it easier.
- It seems like you're reluctant to _____.
- It seems like _____ is important.
- It seems like you feel like my company is in a unique position to _____.
- It seems like you are worried that _____.

**Tactical labels for specific moments:**

- *Negative labels diffuse*. "It seems like you're frustrated by how long this has taken." Naming a negative emotion drains it.
- *Positive labels reinforce.* Use sparingly to amplify shared ground.
- *Self-deprecating labels disarm.* "Look, I'm an asshole" or "I know I haven't been responsive enough." If you've messed up, label your own failure first — it pre-empts theirs.

If they push back ("That's not what I said!"): "I didn't say that *was* what it was. I just said it *seems* like that." Total recovery.

### 3. The Accusation Audit

**The technique:** before a tense conversation, list every negative thing the other side could possibly say or accuse you of. Then say them yourself, *first*, before they can.

**Why it works:**
- Denying a negative gives it credence ("I don't want to seem pushy, but…" — now they're thinking about whether you're pushy).
- Naming the negative bathes it in sunlight, draining its power.
- Once spoken, accusations sound exaggerated; the other side instinctively says "no, no, we don't think that."
- Brings you to a "safe zone of empathy" — every human wants to feel understood.

**How to run it:**
1. List every accusation, no matter how unfair or ridiculous.
2. Distill to 3–5 sharpest possible labels.
3. Role-play. Plan pacing and pauses. **It's theater.**
4. Open the conversation by stating them yourself.

**Example for a price-increase email:**

> "You're going to read this and probably think 'great, here come the price hikes after we just got embedded.' You're going to wonder if we're going to do this every year. You may even feel like we're holding you over a barrel because switching now would cost you a quarter of disruption."

[Then continue with the actual context.]

The buyer's reaction: "No, no, we don't think you're holding us over a barrel. Tell me what's going on."

You've defused 80% of the negative charge before it ever fired.

**Voss's first-day-of-class trick:** "I want to tell you in advance — it's going to be horrible." Always gets more volunteers than he needs. Naming the worst-case makes the actual experience feel like a relief.

### 4. Calibrated Questions

Open-ended questions starting with **What** or **How** (almost never **Why**). Covered in detail in `question-arsenal.md` §6. The negotiation-specific applications:

- **"How am I supposed to do that?"** — single most useful phrase. Gentle no, forces empathy. Voss bought his $36K Toyota for $30K with this phrase + silence + repeat.
- **"What about this doesn't work for you?"** — better than "Why don't you like it?"
- **"How can I help make this better for us?"** — collaborative framing.
- **"How would you like me to proceed?"** — hands them illusion of control.
- **"What would you be able to offer to make that a good price for me?"** — non-aggressive ask for trade.
- **"How does this look to you?"** — better than "Does this work?"

**The deadly Why exception:** "Why would you ever change from your existing supplier? They're great!" — uses defensive instinct to make the buyer argue *for* change *to* you. Use rarely.

### 5. Getting to "That's Right"

**The technique:** drive the conversation toward a moment where the other side affirms your understanding of their world with "that's right" — not "you're right."

**Why "that's right" >>> "you're right":**

| Phrase | What it really means |
|---|---|
| "You're right" | Fake compliance. They're trying to get rid of you. |
| "That's right" | They've internally embraced your understanding as their own. Behavior change is now possible. |

The path:

**Listening → Paraphrase → Label → Summary → "That's right."**

A *complete* summary of "the world according to your counterpart" leaves no possible response other than "that's right."

**Example (from the Voss book — pharma rep):**

A pharma rep had a doctor who refused to prescribe her drug. She didn't pitch the drug. She summarized his frustration with treatment outcomes for his patients, his belief that the system was failing them, his pride in caring for them more than the average doctor. She summarized completely. He said "that's right." Then she asked for the prescription.

**The signal you've nailed it:** the buyer says "that's right" without prompting, and a moment of relaxation enters the room. They feel *heard.* From that moment, influence is possible.

## The voice modes

How you say things matters more than what you say.

| Voice | When to use | How to deliver | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Late-Night FM DJ** | When you need to make a point or de-escalate | Slow, low, downward inflection. Calm. | Conveys "I have it covered." Authority without triggering defensiveness. |
| **Positive / Playful** | DEFAULT mode for most conversation | Smile while talking (audible on phone). Light. | Counterpart in positive frame thinks faster, problem-solves more. |
| **Direct / Assertive** | Almost never | Forceful. | Triggers pushback or passive-aggression. "Slapping yourself in the face." |

Downward inflection: "I'm in control, this isn't up for discussion."
Upward inflection: invites response, concedes the lead.

## The "No" doctrine

"No" starts the negotiation. "Yes" without "How" is meaningless.

**Why "No" is gold:**
- Triggers feelings of safety, security, control, autonomy. People will fight to the death for the right to say no.
- Lets the other side feel protected so they relax and listen.
- Surfaces real concerns instead of cheap agreement.

**"No" rarely means literal rejection.** It usually means:
- I'm not yet ready to agree.
- You're making me uncomfortable.
- I don't understand.
- I can't afford it.
- I want something else.
- I need more information.
- I want to talk it over with someone else.

**The three "yeses" (must distinguish):**

| Type | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Counterfeit Yes** | They plan to say no but find "yes" the easier exit. Or want to keep the conversation going to extract info. |
| **Confirmation Yes** | Reflexive affirmation to a black/white question. No commitment to action. |
| **Commitment Yes** | True agreement, leads to action. The signature. |

The three sound nearly identical. Most counterparts have become masters at the counterfeit yes because pushy salespeople hammered them into it.

**Inviting "No" — phrasing flips:**

- ❌ "Do you have a few minutes to talk?" → ✅ **"Is now a bad time?"**
- ❌ "Can we agree on this?" → ✅ "Have you given up on this project?"
- ❌ "Is this a priority?" → ✅ "Have you given up on solving [problem]?"

After hearing "no," ask:
- "What about this doesn't work for you?"
- "What would you need to make it work?"
- "It seems like there's something here that bothers you."

**No "no" = no go.** If the counterpart cannot or will not say no, they're indecisive, confused, or hiding an agenda. Walk away.

## The Ackerman Bargaining Model

For hard haggling. Voss uses this; ex-CIA Mike Ackerman developed it; Howard Raiffa endorsed it.

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Set your **target** price (your goal). |
| 2 | First offer = **65%** of target. |
| 3 | Calculate three increasing offers: **85%, 95%, 100%**. |
| 4 | Use empathy + many ways of saying "no" between offers, to make them counter before you raise. |
| 5 | Final number: a **precise non-round** number ($37,893 not $38,000). |
| 6 | On the final offer, throw in a **non-monetary item** to signal you're at your limit. |

**Why it works:**

- 65% sets an extreme anchor → fight-or-flight → bends their reality.
- Decreasing concessions (each is half the previous) → they feel they're squeezing every drop. People feel *better* extracting concessions than receiving a fair offer.
- Reciprocity → your concessions trigger theirs.
- Odd numbers sound thoughtful, immovable.
- Non-monetary gift → reciprocity multiplier; signals 100% is the ceiling.

**Mishary's apartment example:** Landlord asked $2,100. Mishary's target: $1,830.
- Bid 1 (65% of $1,830 ≈ $1,189? No — Voss adapts to context; he used $1,730 as anchor).
- Bid 2: $1,790.
- Bid 3: $1,810.
- Final: $1,829.

Accepted with a non-monetary trade.

**When NOT to use Ackerman:** when the relationship matters more than the deal. Hard anchoring damages long-term trust. For renewal pricing with key accounts, soften.

## Black Swans

Unknown unknowns — pieces of information neither side imagined would matter, but which change everything once revealed. Voss assumes 3–5 Black Swans on the table in every negotiation.

**Three types of leverage:**

| Type | Definition | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Positive** | Your ability to give or withhold something they want | "Whenever they say 'I want…' you have leverage." |
| **Negative** | Your ability to make them suffer (loss aversion). | Sparingly. Threats are nuclear. Prefer to *label* the negative ("It seems you don't care what position you're leaving me in") than threaten. |
| **Normative** | Using their own stated norms/values against their inconsistencies. | "No one wants to look like a hypocrite." Speak their language back to them. |

**"Religion":** the counterpart's worldview — what the market, experts, God, or society have determined to be fair. People defer to authority. Speak the dialect of their religion and you gain influence.

**How to find Black Swans:**

- **Get face time** — first/last few minutes, interruptions, meals before/after the formal meeting are gold. Reporters never turn off their recorders.
- **Listen, listen again, listen more.** Use a backup listener in the room whose only job is to listen between the lines.
- **Similarity Principle** — find shared identity to gain trust.
- **Hopes and dreams** — express passion for what they want; people follow you.
- **"Because" reasons** — even idiotic ones increase compliance (Langer copy-machine study: 60% → 90%).
- **When they seem crazy, they aren't.** Three explanations:
  - **Ill-informed** — give them missing data.
  - **Constrained** — legal/political/promised constraints they can't reveal.
  - **They have other interests** — hidden agendas.
- **Mislabel intentionally** to provoke a corrective revelation: "It seems the seller doubts the market." — "Actually, the seller has tougher properties in Atlanta and needs to pay back mortgages." Black Swan revealed.

## Bend Their Reality

Loss aversion (Kahneman/Tversky): losses loom 2× larger than equivalent gains. Use this.

**Six tactics:**

1. **Anchor their emotions first** with an accusation audit, then pitch. The bad scenario you painted didn't happen, so your offer feels like relief. Voss cut $2,000/day contractors to $500 by anchoring low first.

2. **Let them go first on numbers — most of the time.** You may learn their first offer is better than your hoped close. Exception: if you know the market and they're a rookie, anchor extreme.

3. **Establish a range** ("at top places this role pays $130–170K"). Columbia study: applicants who name a range get higher offers.

4. **Pivot to non-monetary terms.** "Let's set price aside and talk about what would make this work." Non-monetary items often cost the counterpart nothing but are valuable to you.

5. **Use odd, non-round numbers.** Round numbers feel like placeholders. $37,263 feels like the result of analysis.

6. **Surprise with a gift.** After staking an extreme anchor and getting rejected, offer an unrelated, unexpected concession. Triggers reciprocity. (Sadat addressing the Knesset, 1977.)

**On compromise:** never split the difference. Compromise is what we do to feel safe and save face — driven by fear, not goals.

**On deadlines:** almost always self-inflicted, almost always flexible. Engage in the process; don't let the clock force a bad deal. Counter-intuitively, *revealing* your deadline often gets you a better deal — hidden deadlines push you to concede while the other side waits. **No deal is better than a bad deal.**

**On "Fair" (the F-word):**

- **Defensive judo:** "We just want what's fair." Triggers irrational concession from a guilt-stricken counterpart.
- **Manipulative attack:** "We've given you a fair offer." Distracts from substance. Defuse by mirroring: "Fair?" + label: "It seems like you're ready to provide the evidence that supports that."
- **Constructive (Voss recommends):** Open with "I want you to feel like you are being treated fairly at all times. So please stop me at any time if you feel I'm being unfair, and we'll address it."

## Email and text negotiation

**The non-response email (the magic one-sentence email):**

> **Have you given up on this project?**

Why it works:
- "No"-oriented question → safe answer is "No," which forces engagement.
- Implicit threat to walk away triggers loss aversion.
- Forces them to define their position and explain.
- Natural inclination: "No, our priorities haven't changed. We've just been bogged down…"

Voss has used this in cultures (Arabic, Chinese) famous for never saying "no." It's direct but cloaked in safety. Being ignored is rude; this isn't.

**Other email rules:**
- Email gives counterpart too much time to compose. Black Swans rarely emerge by email.
- For tense subjects, push to phone or face time.
- Multi-step "No" via email works because there's no real-time pressure (Joaquin/Bruno €23K example).

## The one-sheet negotiation prep template

Voss's signature pre-game tool. **"You don't rise to the occasion — you fall to your highest level of preparation."**

### Section I: The Goal
- Think through best/worst-case extremes.
- Write down only the **best-case, optimistic-but-reasonable** target. (Not BATNA — that caps your aim.)
- Make it specific. Bring the written goal into the room.

### Section II: Summary
- 1–2 sentences capturing the known facts that led to this negotiation.
- Must be a summary the counterpart would respond to with **"That's right."**
- Why are you here? What do you want? What do they want? Why?

### Section III: Labels / Accusation Audit
- Anticipate everything they could throw at you — fair or not.
- Distill to **3–5 labels** that pre-empt those accusations.
- Role-play. Plan pacing.

### Section IV: Calibrated Questions
- **3–5 questions** to (a) reveal value to both sides, (b) identify and overcome deal-killers, (c) influence behind-the-table players.
- Categories:
  - **Discovery** (motives beneath stated positions).
  - **Behind-the-table** ("How does this affect the rest of your team?").
  - **Deal-killer** ("What happens if you do nothing?").
- Pair each question with a follow-up label ready to fire.

### Section V: Non-Cash Offers
- Inventory non-monetary items the counterpart has that would be valuable to you.
- Ask: **"What could they give that would almost get us to do it for free?"**
- Examples: introductions, exclusivity, references, longer terms, faster timelines, branding, mentorship, milestones.
- Build these into your final-stage Ackerman offer.

## Putting it all together — a complete negotiation play

Scenario: a buyer pushes back on your proposal price 35% below your target.

**Old way (rational argument):**
> Buyer: "$60K is too high. Our budget is $40K. Can you do $40K?"
> Rep: "Well, the $60K reflects our [feature], [feature], and [feature]. We could maybe do $55K."
> Buyer: "$45K."
> Rep: "$50K."
> Buyer: "$45K, last offer."
> Rep: "OK, $48K."

Result: 20% discount, no trades, buyer learns they can grind you next year.

**Voss way:**

> Buyer: "$60K is too high. Our budget is $40K. Can you do $40K?"
> Rep: "$40K?"  *(mirror)*
> [4 seconds of silence]
> Buyer: "Well, our budget got cut last quarter, and even that's a stretch. I'd need a really compelling case to push for more."
> Rep: "It sounds like you've been put in a really tough position by the cut, and you don't want to be the one who broke the budget on this one."  *(label)*
> Buyer: "Yeah, exactly. My CFO is watching."
> Rep: "How am I supposed to take a third off our price and still deliver everything we discussed?"  *(calibrated question)*
> [silence]
> Buyer: "I mean, what could you take out of scope?"
> Rep: "What about this is most important to you?"  *(calibrated question + reframe to value)*
> Buyer: "Honestly, the [primary outcome] is non-negotiable. The [secondary outcome] would be nice but we could phase it."
> Rep: "It seems like the [primary outcome] is the actual buying decision, and everything else is timing."  *(label, summarizing toward "that's right")*
> Buyer: "That's right."  ← **stealth victory**
> Rep: "OK. If we phase the [secondary outcome] into year 2 and lock you in at the same rate, I think I can get to $48K for year 1, $52K for year 2 — which gets you under your budget for this fiscal year and gives me something to take to my finance team. And I'd ask, in exchange, for a multi-year agreement and an introduction to your peer at [other division]. Is that something we could land on?"

Result: 20% discount but with multi-year lock-in and a referral. The buyer feels heard, the rep gets the trade, and the buyer doesn't try to grind harder next year because they know you don't fold without value exchange.

The difference: emotion was *named* and *worked*, not avoided.

## Three principles to internalize

1. **Slow it down.** Speed kills rapport.
2. **Subsume your ego.** The other side must feel the solution is theirs.
3. **The adversary is the situation, not the person.** Never make an enemy.

Voss's most important sentence: **"He who has learned to disagree without being disagreeable has discovered the most valuable secret of negotiation."** (Robert Estabrook, quoted by Voss.)

You're not trying to win. You're trying to make the truth visible to both sides. When the truth is visible, the deal often closes itself.
