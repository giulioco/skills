# Diagnose-First Sales Loop

The single most reliable upgrade you can make to any sales situation: **slow down and diagnose before you reach for a tactic.** Most failed deals trace not to bad scripts but to a stage mismatch — a small-sale move applied to a complex sale, a closing technique deployed before need was developed, a pitch sent to a buyer who has no problem you've articulated.

This document is the deeper version of the loop in `SKILL.md`.

---

## The five-question pre-flight check

Before answering any sales question — yours or someone else's — answer these five.

### 1. Where in the journey is this happening?

Map every situation onto five stages. Each stage has different rewards and different traps.

| Stage | What it is | Rewarded behavior | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Prospecting** | First contact; no relationship; no problem yet articulated | Familiarity-building, curiosity-spike, specific reason to engage | "Let me tell you about my company" feature dump |
| **Discovery** | Mapping current state, problems, impact, root cause | Asking, listening, summarizing back | Pitching solutions before problems are surfaced |
| **Demonstrating capability / teaching** | Showing how you close the gap; reframing if needed | Benefits tied to expressed Explicit Needs; Challenger reframe | Demoing every feature; "show up and throw up" |
| **Negotiation / closing** | Pricing, terms, commitment | Tactical empathy, calibrated questions, proposing concrete Advances | Closing techniques; pressure-asking; splitting the difference |
| **Re-engagement / stalled** | Deal has gone cold or buyer ghosted | "No"-oriented framing (Voss), accusation audit, low-friction next step | Passive-aggressive "just checking in" emails |

Ask the user (or yourself): **"Which stage is this?"** If they say "I want to send a follow-up," they could be in any of these — and the right move is completely different in each.

### 2. How big is this sale?

Rackham's most underrated insight: **small-sale tactics actively damage large sales.** Most sales advice on the internet comes from the small-sale world; most of the user's struggles come from applying it to a big one (or vice versa).

| Dimension | Small sale | Large / complex sale |
|---|---|---|
| Cycle | One call | Months, multiple calls |
| Decision moment | Seller present | Between calls, often without you |
| Buyer memory | Same-day decision | >50% of pitch forgotten in a week |
| Risk to buyer | Low — hidable | High — public, multi-stakeholder, career-visible |
| Pressure | Often works | Backfires (pressure scales negatively with stakes) |
| Closing techniques | Improve close rate | *Reduce* close rate (American Airlines: 0/1/2+ closes → 22%/61%/<20%) |
| Implied Needs | Sufficient buying signal | Not predictive — you need *Explicit* Needs |
| Features/Advantages | Effective | Create objections |
| Time horizon to value | Now | Often months — value perception must survive between calls |

**Rule of thumb:** if the buyer has ≥3 stakeholders, ≥30 days of decision time, or ≥$10K of commitment, you are in big-sale territory.

### 3. Who's the audience?

Not just "the prospect" — specifically:

- **Individual contributor / manager / executive?** Their language differs. Executives don't deal in features; they deal in implications.
- **Mobilizer or Talker?** (Challenger Customer concept.) Mobilizers can drive change inside the org; Talkers love your pitch but can't move it. The biggest mistake reps make is investing in Talkers because they're easy to reach.
- **Single decision-maker, or committee?** If committee, who else is at the table? Voss: "There is always a team on the other side." 5.4 average decision-makers in B2B (Gartner).
- **What's their personal driver?** Career advancement, fear of failure, mandated initiative, internal political win? The deal closes when their personal driver and your value proposition align.

If you can't answer who the audience is, you're not ready to write outbound. Stop and research.

### 4. What does the buyer feel right now?

Sales is emotional even when buyers swear it isn't. Voss-style label of underlying emotion before deciding what to write:

- **Skeptical?** They've been pitched before. Open with an accusation audit ("You're probably thinking 'great, another vendor'…").
- **Indifferent?** No urgency. You don't have a problem; reframe to manufacture one.
- **Frustrated?** Vendor pain. Validate first ("It seems like you've been burned before"), then earn the right to talk about your alternative.
- **Defensive?** They feel attacked or pressured. Carnegie + Voss: agree, ask, listen. Never argue.
- **Eager?** Be careful — eagerness is often "Counterfeit Yes." Probe with calibrated questions before assuming intent.

Most outreach emails get the emotion wrong because they're written from the rep's eagerness, not the buyer's reality.

### 5. What's the next concrete step?

Rackham's distinction is the most useful single tool here:

- **Order** — they buy. Rare in big sales.
- **Advance** — a specific action that moves the sale forward. (Demo on Tuesday. Intro to CFO by Friday. Trial scoped by next call.)
- **Continuation** — sale continues but no specific action agreed. ("Great call! We'll be in touch.") **This is failure dressed in pleasant clothes.**
- **No-sale** — explicit refusal. At least clean.

Bad call objectives produce Continuations no matter how good the rep:
- "Build a relationship" → Continuation
- "Get to know each other" → Continuation
- "Have a great call" → Continuation

Good call objectives produce Advances:
- "Get her to commit to a 30-min demo with the technical buyer next week"
- "Get an intro to the head of finance by EOM"
- "Get verbal agreement to run a 14-day pilot scoped on this call"

**If the user can't tell you the specific Advance they want, fix that first.**

## The diagnostic prompt template (use this when stuck)

When the user comes to you with a vague or stuck sales situation, spin up the diagnostic in your head — or, if it'd actually help, ask the smallest set of questions that gets you unstuck. Default order:

```
1. What's the situation in 1-2 sentences?
2. What's the next step you want them to take?
3. What's the relationship history? (Cold? Met once? Customer?)
4. What problem does your product solve for *this* prospect specifically?
5. What did they last say or do? (Verbatim if possible.)
```

If the user can't answer 4, you're working an upstream problem — they don't have a clear thesis on the prospect's pain. Pivot to building a Problem Identification Chart (PIC) for that prospect (`question-arsenal.md`) before drafting anything.

If the user can't answer 5, they may not have actually engaged the prospect yet — they're pitching to a guess. That's fine, but be honest about it: targeted bridges (educated guesses about a class of prospects) are different from strategic bridges (researched, single-prospect-specific). Both work; pretending one is the other doesn't.

## Common diagnostic mistakes

### "What should I say?" before "What stage am I in?"

The same words land differently at different stages. "Tell me about your business" is mediocre at first contact (Rackham: top reps rarely open the same way twice; senior buyers hate idle Situation Questions). It's catastrophic at late discovery (you should already know this). It's a champion-empowerment move at re-engagement (giving the buyer the floor to vent about internal politics).

### Treating Implied Needs as buying signals in big sales

A buyer saying "yeah, our system's slow" is an Implied Need. In a small sale, that's enough. In a big sale, that buyer just told you a problem they don't actually intend to act on — and a rep who jumps to demo will get told "thanks, we'll think about it." Develop the Implied Need with Implication Questions until it becomes Explicit ("we need a faster system" or "I'm looking for X by Q3"). Only Explicit Needs predict close in big sales.

### Mistaking "you're right" for victory

When a prospect says "you're right" or "great point," your alarm should go off, not relax. Voss: that's the sound of someone trying to end the conversation. The signal you want is "that's right" — they've embraced your understanding of their world as their own. If you're getting "you're right," you haven't summarized deeply enough yet.

### Closing without checking concerns

Rackham: most "objections" surface during the close because the rep tried to close before checking. Surfacing the concern by closing creates antagonism; surfacing it with a check ("Are there any areas you'd like me to tell you more about?") creates alignment. Always check before you propose the Advance.

### Forecasting on Continuations

Sales managers and reps both do this. Great call → strong forecast. But the call that produced "we'll be in touch" with no calendared follow-up didn't move the deal. Forecast on Advances only. Push every "great" call to: "What did they specifically commit to do, and when?"

## The diagnose-first quick rubric

When in doubt, ask yourself in this order:

1. **Have I diagnosed before pitching?** Do I actually know their current state, problem, impact, root cause? Could I fill in their PIC?
2. **Am I matching tactic to stakes?** Is this a small-sale move I'm using on a big sale (or vice versa)?
3. **Am I asking more than I'm telling?** Talk-time check: am I going to be at <50% on the next call?
4. **Am I challenging the *idea* or the *person*?** (Carnegie filter: never criticize the person; Challenger: always challenge the assumption.)
5. **Have I named a specific Advance, on a calendar?**

If all five are yes, you're in good shape. If any are no, fix that one first — that's almost always the real problem.
