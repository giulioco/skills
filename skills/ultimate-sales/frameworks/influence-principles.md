# Influence Principles — Carnegie's Human Layer

Dale Carnegie's *How to Win Friends and Influence People* (1936) is the oldest book in this skill, and arguably the most important — not because it's the most sophisticated, but because every other book is built on the truths it laid down.

The premise: **humans are not creatures of logic. They are creatures of emotion, ego, and the craving to feel important.** Every sale, every negotiation, every relationship runs through that filter first. Modern sales research (Voss, Challenger, SPIN, Gap Selling) refines and extends Carnegie. None of it overturns him.

This document gives you Carnegie compressed for the sales operator — what to do, why it works, and where it needs upgrading by the more modern frameworks.

---

## The four laws of human gravity

Carnegie's book is structured in four parts. They map cleanly to four problems salespeople face every day:

| Carnegie Part | Problem it solves | Modern parallel |
|---|---|---|
| Part 1 — Handle People | "How do I open without getting blocked?" | Voss tactical empathy + accusation audit |
| Part 2 — Make People Like You | "How do I become someone they want to talk to?" | Modern relationship-building, ABS playbooks |
| Part 3 — Win People to Your Way of Thinking | "How do I get them to agree without arguing?" | SPIN questions, Voss calibrated questions, Challenger reframe |
| Part 4 — Be a Leader (change without resentment) | "How do I redirect them when they're going the wrong way?" | Coaching, champion enablement, post-sale management |

## Part 1 — Handling people (the foundation)

### Principle 1: Don't criticize, condemn, or complain

**Why it works:** criticism puts the other side on the defensive and forces them to justify themselves. It wounds pride, hurts the sense of importance, and arouses resentment. Behavior rarely changes. The relationship always suffers.

**The data behind it:** Skinner showed reinforcement learning beats punishment learning. Hans Selye: "As much as we thirst for approval, we dread condemnation."

**Carnegie's example:** Lincoln, after Meade let Lee escape, wrote a furious rebuke and *never sent it.* Bob Hoover, the test pilot whose mechanic filled his plane with jet fuel: "I want you to service my F-51 tomorrow." That mechanic never made another mistake.

**Sales applications:**

- **Never tell a prospect they're wrong about a competitor.** Even if true, you trigger justification mode and lose the deal.
- **Don't bash the incumbent vendor** — the prospect chose them; bashing them = bashing the prospect's judgment.
- **When a deal slips, do not blame the champion.** "I don't see how I could have done any differently" is what they will say to themselves.
- **Don't trash your own ops/eng team to score points** — it tells the prospect you criticize people.
- **Skip the passive-aggressive "haven't heard back" emails.** They generate resentment, not response.

**The edge case:** Challenger Sale says you should challenge buyers' assumptions. Doesn't this conflict with Carnegie? No. You can challenge the *idea* without criticizing the *person.* The sentence "You're wrong about X" violates Carnegie. The sentence "What we've seen across many companies is that the obvious framing actually misses the bigger lever" challenges the same assumption while honoring the buyer.

### Principle 2: Give honest and sincere appreciation

**Why it works:** appreciation feeds the deepest craving. People will work, buy, marry, or follow whoever satisfies the hunger to feel important.

Charles Schwab earned a million-dollar salary not because he knew steel — he had men under him who knew more — but because of his ability to arouse enthusiasm. Schwab: "I am hearty in my approbation and lavish in my praise."

**Flattery vs. appreciation:** "One is sincere and the other insincere. One comes from the heart out; the other from the teeth out." Flattery is "telling the other person precisely what he thinks about himself." Sincere appreciation requires you to think about the other person's actual good points — which is what 95% of people don't do because they're thinking about themselves.

**Sales applications:**

- **Open meetings by recognizing something specific** the buyer's team built or shipped — not the company's marketing, but a thing *they* personally drove. Specificity is the proof of sincerity.
- **When a prospect makes a sharp observation** about their own market, name it: "That's the cleanest framing of that problem I've heard from a CFO this quarter." Only say it if you mean it.
- **For champions:** praise them by name to their boss in writing. Most champions never get this from a vendor and will fight harder for the deal.

**Edge case:** modern outbound at scale has weaponized "sincerity." Every cold email opens with "I loved your post on..." Buyers' BS detectors have evolved. Carnegie's tools work; their *ubiquity* has raised the bar. Specificity, brevity, and a willingness to be less liked are the modern moat.

### Principle 3: Arouse in the other person an eager want

**Why it works:** "There is only one way under high heaven to get anybody to do anything... by making the other person want to do it." Henry Ford: "If there is any one secret of success, it lies in the ability to get the other person's point of view and see things from that person's angle as well as from your own."

**Carnegie's example:** Carnegie's hotel raised his rent 300%. Instead of arguing, he listed the manager's "Advantages" and "Disadvantages" of the increase on a sheet — emptier ballroom, lost cultured-audience advertising, no income at all if Carnegie left. Got the increase reduced to 50%. "Mind you, I got this reduction without saying a word about what I wanted."

**Sales applications:**

- **Never open a cold email with what your company *desires*.** "Mr. Blank" — Carnegie's radio-agency letter — is every modern outbound email that starts with "Our company desires to..." Reframe everything to buyer-side outcomes.
- "We have an API" → "Your ops team stops the 4 PM data-entry shift."
- **For deals stuck in committee:** build the buyer-side advantages/disadvantages list *for them*. They will use your framing internally because it sounds like their own thinking.
- **Salespeople fail because** "they are always thinking only of what they want. They don't realize that neither you nor I want to buy anything... we are eternally interested in solving our problems."

## Part 2 — Making people like you (so they answer your calls)

The six principles, each compressed:

### 1. Become genuinely interested in other people

> "You can make more friends in two months by becoming interested in other people than you can in two years by trying to get other people interested in you."

- Research before every call: not just LinkedIn title, but recent posts, podcast appearances, what their kid's soccer team is up to. Reference specifically.
- The "stamps for the son" move (Walters got the bank report by bringing foreign stamps for the executive's stamp-collecting 12-year-old): send a relevant book, a thoughtful Loom about something *they* posted, a referral that helps *their* career.
- Talk to the gatekeeper, the SDR, the procurement clerk like humans.

### 2. Smile

A smile says "I like you. You make me happy. I am glad to see you."

- Smile before you dial. Voice carries it.
- On video, the camera amplifies flat affect into hostility. Default state = slight smile.
- Warmth on entry sets the price ceiling for the rest of the meeting.

### 3. Remember names

A name is the only word that belongs solely to that human. Using it correctly is "a subtle and very effective compliment." Forgetting or misspelling places you "at a sharp disadvantage."

- Use the prospect's name 2–3 times in the opening minutes — and never again in a way that sounds salesy. ("Sarah, the question I keep coming back to is...")
- **Name the deal after them.** Andrew Carnegie named his Pittsburgh steel mill "Edgar Thomson Steel Works" — Thomson was president of Pennsylvania Railroad. Guess who bought rails? Modern equivalent: name the internal Slack channel, the deal-room, the success plan after them.
- For procurement / legal / IT / SE — the support cast — addressing them by name in a multi-stakeholder call signals you see them as people. They control the deal more than the C-suite admits.

### 4. Be a good listener; encourage others to talk about themselves

> "Few human beings are proof against the implied flattery of rapt attention."

- Discovery call talk-time: 70%+ buyer (Carnegie 1936; Gong 2024 confirms it).
- When a prospect gets emotional about an internal frustration — let them vent. Do not jump to solutioning. The vent is the buy signal. "He raved on and I listened for nearly three hours."
- The Detmer move: when a customer escalates, thank them for telling you, agree it's serious, side with them against your own org if needed. Customers buy *more* from vendors who admit honestly.

### 5. Talk in terms of the other person's interests

> "The royal road to a person's heart is to talk about the things he or she treasures most."

- Pre-call research isn't optional. Pull their LinkedIn posts, podcast appearances, recent quotes, board memberships.
- Open on *their* topic, not yours.
- For champions: their currency is internal political capital. Frame your product as something that elevates *them* in their org, not just solves their problem.

### 6. Make the other person feel important — and do it sincerely

> "Talk to people about themselves and they will listen for hours." — Disraeli

- For senior buyers: ask about origin stories. "How did you end up running this org?" Most have never been asked. They will reveal motivations, fears, and timelines no discovery question would surface.
- "Little phrases such as 'I'm sorry to trouble you,' 'Would you be so kind as to —?' 'Would you mind?' 'Thank you' — little courtesies oil the cogs of the monotonous grind of everyday life." Procurement remembers reps who said "thank you for your time" and meant it.

## Part 3 — Winning people to your way of thinking (the conversion layer)

The 12 principles. Compressed.

### 1. The only way to get the best of an argument is to avoid it

When a prospect bashes your product or praises a competitor, **agree first.** "Sounds like Whose-It would be a fine choice for that use case." Removes the wall, then you can position differently.

In RFP debrief calls when you lost: do not argue the decision. "Makes sense. Thank you for explaining how you weighed it." You will be invited back for the next cycle. Reps who argue are blacklisted.

### 2. Show respect for the other person's opinions; never say "you're wrong"

The magic phrase: **"I may be wrong. I frequently am. Let's examine the facts."** Leaves the buyer's pride intact.

When a buyer makes a factually incorrect claim about your product, *never* "actually..." them. Instead: "That's a really common reading of how it works — let me share what I've seen and you can tell me if it lands."

### 3. If you are wrong, admit it quickly and emphatically

When you missed a deadline, broke a SLA, or sent a wrong proposal: own it before the call starts. "I dropped the ball on the timeline. I should have caught it. Here's what I'm doing to fix it." Most reps over-explain or deflect — this destroys trust faster than the original error.

In QBRs where churn risk is the topic: don't defend. "You're right that the Q3 implementation didn't deliver what we promised. Here's what I think happened, and here's the plan to make Q4 different." Customers buy more from vendors who admit honestly.

### 4. Begin in a friendly way

"A drop of honey catches more flies than a gallon of gall."

Never open a price negotiation by attacking the price. Open with what you appreciate about the relationship. *Then* introduce the constraint.

In escalations, the first 30 seconds set the ceiling for the resolution. Warmth in those seconds is worth thousands of dollars in concessions.

### 5. Get the other person saying "yes, yes" immediately (the Secret of Socrates)

Once a person says "no," their entire neuromuscular system "sets itself on guard." Pride demands consistency. A "yes" puts them in "forward-moving, accepting, open" posture.

**Modern caveat:** Voss says explicitly that the "yes ladder" is a trap when used as a manipulation. The buyer recognizes the pattern and gives you counterfeit yeses to escape. Use the Socratic method *honestly* — get yes to questions where yes is genuinely true, then build to the conclusion that follows from those yeses. Don't manufacture leading yeses.

### 6. Let the other person do a great deal of the talking

> "If you want enemies, excel your friends; but if you want friends, let your friends excel you." — La Rochefoucauld

Modern data: top performers cluster at ~43% talk-time on calls (Gong). The 7th frustration the buyer mentions is the real budget driver. Don't redirect.

### 7. Let the other person feel that the idea is his or hers

> "We have much more faith in ideas that we discover for ourselves than in ideas that are handed to us on a silver platter."

In late-stage deals, hand champions a draft business case with deliberately-incomplete sections. "What numbers would your CFO want here?" They co-author and then defend it internally as theirs.

Pricing: present 2–3 options and let the buyer pick the structure. Their own choice will be defended internally.

### 8. Try honestly to see things from the other person's point of view

Before every cold outreach, write 2 sentences answering: "Why has this person ignored 50 outreach emails this week?" That answer is your opener.

In multi-threaded deals: each stakeholder sees a different problem. The CFO's view is not the CRO's view is not the IT director's view. Map them separately.

### 9. Be sympathetic with the other person's ideas and desires

> "I don't blame you one iota for feeling as you do. If I were you I would undoubtedly feel just as you do."

This is precisely Voss's tactical empathy — labeled 80 years before Voss codified it.

When a buyer says "we don't have budget" — never argue. "Of course. With everything else on your plate this quarter, that makes sense. If I were you I'd say the same thing." Then explore.

**Modern caveat:** sympathy can become enabling. If you sympathize forever with "we have no budget," no deal happens. Use empathy as a *lever* to surface the deeper objection — not to validate the surface one.

### 10. Appeal to nobler motives

J. P. Morgan: "A person usually has two reasons for doing a thing: one that sounds good and a real one."

Closing-line move: "I trust you'll make the right call for your team." Hands the ethical/professional standard to the buyer to live up to.

When negotiating concessions, frame your concession as evidence of trust ("I'm doing this because I believe you'll be a great long-term partner") not as bargaining weakness.

### 11. Dramatize your ideas

> "Merely stating a truth isn't enough. The truth has to be made vivid, interesting, dramatic."

In demos, show, don't tell. The screenshot of *their* data running through your product beats slides about your platform.

In ROI conversations, calculate the cost of doing nothing and write it on a napkin. "$2.4M / quarter sitting on the table."

### 12. Throw down a challenge

Schwab walked into a struggling steel mill, asked the day shift their heat count (six), chalked a "6" on the floor. Night shift saw it, made it "7." Day shift came back with "10." Mill went from worst to best.

For champions: frame the deal as their proving moment internally. "If you ship this by EOY, you'll be the team that drove the biggest infrastructure modernization in your division's history."

For executives sitting on the fence: appeal to their legacy/peer-comparison instinct. Not "you should do this" but "the leaders ahead of the curve on this are X, Y, Z."

## Part 4 — Leading without resentment (the renewal layer)

These nine principles apply when you need to *change* someone — a customer who's misusing the product, a champion who's underperforming, a stakeholder who's misframing the deal. They're the renewal/CSM layer.

### 1. Begin with praise and honest appreciation

When delivering bad news (price increase, service degradation, missed deadline), open with sincere appreciation. Pre-delivery praise must be specific and true.

### 2. Call attention to mistakes indirectly

"But" negates the praise. Replace "but" with "and."

For internal teams underperforming: don't say "you missed X *but* did Y well." Say "great work on Y *and* if we add X next time...". For customers misusing the product: instead of "you're using it wrong," show the cleaner workflow with a praise hook on what they're already doing right.

### 3. Talk about your own mistakes before criticizing the other person

When coaching reps or critiquing a champion's deck: open with "I made this exact mistake when I was running my own deal — let me show you."

When customers escalate, share a comparable failure your own team had and what you learned. Vulnerability earns trust.

### 4. Ask questions instead of giving direct orders

Forecast calls: instead of telling reps what to do, ask "If you were the buyer, what's the next email you'd want to receive?" Champion enablement: "What slide would you want to walk into your CFO meeting with?" The asking *is* the coaching.

### 5. Let the other person save face

When closing a deal where a champion got a feature request rejected: never say "your priority wasn't strategic enough." Frame it as "your timing on this is ahead of the roadmap — when it ships, you'll be the first to validate."

When a customer cancels: thank them for what they tried, never publicly blame them for poor adoption.

### 6. Praise the slightest improvement and praise every improvement

Specific praise > general flattery. "Your slide 4 framing of the cost-of-delay is the best I've seen all year" beats "great deck."

For new reps: praise the first sign of improvement publicly, in a deal review or Slack channel — not just one-on-one. Buyers too: "you're the first prospect this week who pushed back on slide 7 — that's exactly the right question."

### 7. Give the other person a fine reputation to live up to

Tell your champion "you're the most strategic thinker on the buying committee" — they will rise to it.

Tell a stalled procurement officer "I've worked with a lot of buyers, and you're one of the most thorough I've seen on actually reading the contract" — they'll move *faster*, not slower.

### 8. Use encouragement; make the fault seem easy to correct

When a champion says "I can't get my CFO to sign off in 30 days," don't agree it's hard. Say "Three of our last five deals had the exact same constraint — here's the one-pager that got it done."

For new logos worried about implementation: "Your team is more technical than the average customer who got this live in 6 weeks. You'll be ahead of them."

### 9. Make the other person happy about doing the thing you suggest

Frame the ask so they see their own benefit. Match the benefit to *their* want.

**The Ben Franklin "ask a small favor" move:** asking your prospect for advice on something they're an expert on disarms them and makes you a friend. Albert Amsel split open a hostile plumber's account by asking him for advice on a Queens Village location. The plumber lectured, became friends, started buying.

## Where Carnegie misleads (the modern overlay)

Carnegie is necessary but not sufficient for modern complex sales. Pure relationship-building falls short in four predictable ways:

### 1. The Challenger critique

Dixon & Adamson's CEB study found the lowest-performing sales archetype in complex B2B is the "Relationship Builder" — the Carnegie disciple who is liked by everyone, never argues, and never pushes. The highest performer is the Challenger who *teaches the customer something they didn't know*, *tailors* the message, and *takes control* of the conversation — including respectful tension and reframe.

Carnegie says: never tell anyone they are wrong. Challenger says: your job is to tell sophisticated buyers their assumptions are incomplete — that's the value they pay for. **Both are right; the resolution is *manner*.** Challenge the idea while honoring the person.

### 2. The "tell me about yourself" trap

Carnegie elevates listening to the highest discipline. Modern data (Gong) shows top reps still talk 43% of the time — not 30%. Why? Because in complex deals the buyer often does not know what they don't know. Pure listening leaves them stuck in their own frame. Carnegie underweights the salesperson's role as *educator* and *frame-setter*.

### 3. The sympathy loop

"Be sympathetic with the other person's ideas and desires" can become enabling. If a CFO says "we have no budget," sympathizing forever produces no deal. Voss's tactical empathy is specifically designed to *use* empathy as a lever to surface the deeper objection — not to validate the surface one.

### 4. The sincerity loophole

Modern outbound at scale has weaponized "sincerity." Every cold email opens with "I loved your post on..." Buyers' BS detectors have evolved. Specificity, brevity, and a willingness to be less liked are the modern moat.

### 5. Nothing about pipeline math

Carnegie says nothing about prospecting volume, activity discipline, or pipeline coverage. Blount's *Fanatical Prospecting* is the antidote. Carnegie is the *quality* book; you still need a *quantity* book.

## Both/and — the integrated operator

The complete sales operator runs Carnegie at the *human* layer (warmth, listening, importance, sincerity) and Challenger/Voss/SPIN/Gap at the *deal* layer (insight, tension, calibrated questions, problem-centric discovery).

Reps who choose only one fail predictably:

- **Pure-Carnegie reps** are loved and never close.
- **Pure-Challenger reps** win with sophisticated buyers and burn warm relationships.
- **Pure-Voss reps** tactical-empathy themselves into manipulation accusations.
- **Pure-SPIN reps** ask great questions and never deliver insight.

The operator who wins the most: warm-and-respected like Carnegie, sharp-and-teaching like Challenger, calibrated-and-curious like Voss, problem-centric like Keenan, methodical like Rackham, persistent like Blount.

## The 11 retellable Carnegie stories every coach should know

1. **Lincoln's unsent letter to Meade** — write the angry email, save as draft, send 24 hours later, or never.
2. **Bob Hoover's mechanic** — when someone screws up a deal, they already know. Trust them with the next one.
3. **Schwab's chalk "6"** — visible, peer-comparable challenges beat threats.
4. **Edgar Thomson Steel Works** — name the deal-room, channel, success plan after the customer.
5. **Stamps for the executive's son** — the personal angle before the LinkedIn angle.
6. **Westinghouse motors too hot** — Socratic yes-yes turnaround.
7. **G.B.R.'s lost voice** — when you can't talk, the buyer must — and they sell themselves.
8. **Eugene Wesson's unfinished sketches** — give the buyer authorship of the solution.
9. **Detmer Woolen and the customer who came to fire him** — disarm with agreement, recommend competitors.
10. **Patrick O'Haire and the truck competitors** — agree first, position second.
11. **The Hoover-mechanic, the cop with the dog, the editor with Warren** — admit your mistakes faster and harder than expected.

These are the parables. Use them when teaching, deal-reviewing, or talking a struggling rep off the ledge.
