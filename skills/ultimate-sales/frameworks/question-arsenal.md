# Question Arsenal

The unified discovery toolkit, drawn from SPIN (Rackham), Gap Selling (Keenan), and Never Split the Difference (Voss). Most reps have ~5 stock questions and reach for the same ones every call. Top performers have 50+ questions catalogued by purpose, choose deliberately, and **shut up after asking** so the buyer's words land in the silence.

The principle behind everything in this file: **the buyer needs to talk themselves into change. Your job is to ask the question that lets them do it.**

---

## The four levels of questions, in order of leverage

| Level | Type | Purpose | Where it comes from | Average rep | Top rep |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Situation** | Get context / facts | SPIN | Many | Few — homework eliminates most |
| 2 | **Problem** | Surface dissatisfactions / Implied Needs | SPIN + Gap | Some | Many |
| 3 | **Implication** | Make the problem feel painful enough to act | SPIN | Almost none (1 in 20 questions) | 10× more |
| 4 | **Need-payoff / Future state** | Buyer articulates the value of solving | SPIN + Gap | Skipped half the time | 10× more |

Layered on top of all four:
- **Calibrated questions** (Voss) for emotional, negotiation, or stuck moments.
- **Provoking questions** (Keenan) when the buyer is too comfortable in their current state.

---

## 1. Situation Questions (use sparingly)

**What they do:** establish context. "How long have you had your current system?" "What's your annual sales volume?" "How many people on the team?"

**Use them only when:**
- You couldn't have answered through research.
- The data is essential for the next question.
- The buyer is junior enough that warming-up questions are welcome.

**Don't use them when:**
- A 5-minute LinkedIn check would have answered it.
- The buyer is senior. Executives hate Situation Questions — Rackham: "There's a special place in hell reserved for wicked salespeople where they sit for all eternity being forced to answer their own Situation Questions."

**Stat:** Successful calls have *fewer* Situation Questions, not more. Inexperienced reps over-rely on them because they feel safe.

**Better alternative — start with informed assumption** (Hypothesis-Based Selling, Challenger): "Companies your size typically run [X process] like this — does that match yours, or do you do it differently?" Same data acquired, but you're now *teaching* in the question rather than pulling free education.

---

## 2. Problem Questions (the workhorse)

**What they do:** surface dissatisfactions, frustrations, gaps. They turn "things are fine" into Implied Needs.

**Examples:**
- "What's frustrating about [X process] today?"
- "Where does [X] break down most often?"
- "What's the most painful part of how this works now?"
- "How reliable is [X] today?"
- "How happy is your team with [X]?"
- "What are the biggest disadvantages of how you're handling this now?"
- "Where do things slip through the cracks?"
- "What's something about this that keeps coming up in team meetings?"

**Mistake to avoid:** asking about problems your product can't solve. You'll inflate a need you can't meet, and lose credibility when you can't address it.

**Pre-call:** for every prospect, write down at least 3 likely problems your product solves. Then write the Problem Question for each.

**Keenan's framing:** **don't stop at the first problem.** A great discovery surfaces 5+ stacked problems. The first problem is often surface; the third is often the real one.

> "Don't just find problems — hunt them down."

---

## 3. Implication Questions (the highest-leverage move in big sales)

**What they do:** take an acknowledged problem and explore its consequences. They make the problem grow until the buyer feels acting is more urgent than not acting.

**Why they matter:** in big sales, Problem Questions alone don't predict success. The cost side of the value equation (price + risk + change pain) is too high for an Implied Need to tip the scales. Implication Questions are how you build value on the problem side until the equation tips.

**Implication Question Planner (Rackham, verbatim):**

1. Write down a likely problem.
2. List every difficulty or knock-on effect that problem could create.
3. Turn each difficulty into a question.

**Example chain:**

- **Problem:** existing machine is hard to use.
- **Difficulty 1:** shortage of qualified operators → "What's it costing you in overtime when an operator is out?" / "How hard is it to recruit replacements?"
- **Difficulty 2:** training cost → "What does training a new operator cost?"
- **Difficulty 3:** bottlenecks during transitions → "What's the lost-output cost when there's a transition?"
- **Difficulty 4:** quality issues from outsourcing overflow → "How does sending work outside affect delivery and quality?" / "Who absorbs the schedule risk?"

**Generic Implication Questions** (memorize and adapt):

- "How does that affect [downstream metric]?"
- "What does that mean for [other stakeholder/team]?"
- "What's the cost of leaving this as-is for another quarter / year?"
- "If your senior people are spending time on this, what's the opportunity cost?"
- "How does this affect your ability to [strategic priority]?"
- "What happens at peak load / quarter-close / [stress moment]?"
- "Who else is affected when this goes wrong?"
- "How does this show up in your numbers?"
- "What's the impact on morale / retention / customer experience?"
- "What's the worst day this caused last quarter?"

**Quincy's Rule (Rackham):** Implication Questions are SAD. They make the problem feel worse. That's the point. The buyer (not you) does the math on the cost. Self-persuasion is the strongest persuasion.

**Mistake to avoid:** drowning the buyer. After the buyer is clearly worried, *stop*. Switch to Need-payoff Questions before you depress them into despair.

**Statistic:** in average sales calls, only 1 of 20 questions asked is an Implication Question. Top performers ask far more. This is the single biggest skill gap in big-sale selling.

---

## 4. Need-payoff Questions (the closer in big sales)

**What they do:** get the buyer to articulate the value of solving the problem. The *buyer* (not you) describes the benefits.

Why this matters: benefits the buyer says out loud to themselves are 10× more persuasive than benefits you tell them. Plus, in committee sales, you need the buyer to **rehearse internal selling** — they're going to advocate for you to colleagues you'll never meet, and they need to have already articulated the value in their own words.

**Examples:**

- "Why is solving this important to you?"
- "How would it help if we could…?"
- "Would it be useful if [X]?"
- "What would the value be of fixing this?"
- "How would your team benefit from that?"
- "Suppose you didn't have to spend that time. What could you do with the time you saved?"
- "Then anything that reduced [X] wouldn't just help you, it would also help [other stakeholder]?"
- "What does success look like 12 months from now if this works?"
- "How would your week look different if this just… went away?"

**Quincy's Rule (Rackham):** Need-payoff Questions are HAPPY. They're solution-centered. The mood of the call should shift up.

**Mistakes to avoid:**

1. **Asking too early** — before problems are surfaced. "If I could show you something interesting, would you be interested?" puts buyers on the defensive.
2. **Asking about needs you can't meet** — worst possible move. Buyer says "I need X"; you can't do X; "Why is that important?" inflates a need you can't satisfy. Conversely, the *best* time is when you CAN meet the need — but ironically that's when most reps stop asking and start pitching.
3. **Skipping them entirely.** In nearly half of studied calls, sellers used zero Need-payoff Questions.

**Stat:** top performers ask >10× more Need-payoff Questions per call than average reps.

## 5. Provoking Questions (Keenan)

When the buyer is too comfortable with their current state — especially relevant in re-engagement or "we're happy" stalls.

**Examples:**

- "What happens if [X] doesn't change in the next 12 months?"
- "Has there ever been a time when [problem] caused [bigger consequence]?"
- "If you did [X], what do you think would happen?"
- "What would it take for you to consider changing [vendor / approach]?"
- "If you had a magic wand, what's the first thing you'd change about [process]?"
- "If [your boss / the board / the market] looked at this 12 months from now, what would they want to see?"

These are gentle provocations — they nudge the buyer to consider their state from a new angle without challenging directly.

## 6. Calibrated Questions (Voss) — for negotiation, emotion, stuck moments

Open-ended, beginning with **What** or **How**. Almost never **Why** (which feels accusatory). Calibrated questions hand the buyer the illusion of control while you frame the conversation.

**Why they work:**
- Make the buyer use *their* mental energy to solve *your* problem.
- Carry no target for attack (unlike statements).
- Educate the buyer on the problem instead of telling them.
- Implicitly ask for help → triggers reciprocity.

**The go-to set (memorize):**

- **"How am I supposed to do that?"** — single most useful phrase. Gentle no, forces empathy.
- **"What about this is important to you?"** — surfaces values.
- **"How can I help make this better for us?"** — collaborative reframe.
- **"How would you like me to proceed?"** — hands them the illusion of control.
- **"What's the objective?" / "What are we trying to accomplish here?"** — resets when off-track.
- **"What is the biggest challenge you face?"** — opening probe.
- **"How does that affect things?"** — the universal Implication-as-calibrated-question.
- **"What's the core issue here?"**
- **"How will we know we're on track?"**
- **"How will we address things if we find we're off-track?"**
- **"What about this doesn't work for you?"** — better than "Why don't you like it?"
- **"What caused you to do it?"** — better than "Why did you do that?"

**Behind-the-table calibrated questions** (find the deal-killers you can't see):

- "How does this affect the rest of your team?"
- "How on board are the people not on this call?"
- "What do your colleagues see as their main challenges in this area?"
- "Who else needs to weigh in?"

**Deal-killer questions** (cost-of-inaction probes):

- "What are we up against here?"
- "What happens if you do nothing?"
- "What does doing nothing cost you?"
- "How does making this deal resonate with what your company prides itself on?"

**Avoid:**

- "Why" anything (except in the rare case of "Why would you ever change from your current vendor?" — which uses the defensive instinct to make the buyer argue for change *to* you).
- Yes/no verbs ("Can you…", "Is this…", "Do you…").
- "Who/When/Where" — they elicit one-word factual answers.

## 7. The Gap Selling discovery sequence (one complete play)

Keenan's master discovery flow, mapped to the question types above:

| Step | Goal | Question types | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Establish context | Situation (minimal) | "Walk me through how [the relevant process] works today." |
| 2 | Surface problems | Problem | "What's frustrating about that?" / "Where does it break down?" |
| 3 | Get to root cause | Probing + "Why?" laddered | "And why does that happen?" → repeat until they can't answer |
| 4 | Quantify impact | Implication | "What does that cost you each month?" / "How does it affect [X stakeholder]?" |
| 5 | Surface emotional impact | Implication + Voss labels | "What's the worst day this caused?" / "It seems like this has been weighing on you." |
| 6 | Buyer articulates the gap | Need-payoff | "What would it look like 12 months from now if [X] just worked?" |
| 7 | Buyer articulates the cost of inaction | Provoking + deal-killer | "What happens if nothing changes?" |
| 8 | Validate | "What I hear you saying is… Did I get that right?" | Drives "that's right" |
| 9 | Propose Advance | Specific next-step proposal | "Then the most logical next step would be…" |

Keenan's framing: **don't stop at problem; go all the way to root cause and impact**. Most reps stop at step 2 ("ah, you have a problem we solve!") and pitch. They lose to the rep who got to step 5 because the buyer felt their pain.

## 8. The Problem Identification Chart (PIC) — pre-call planning tool

Before you ever talk to a prospect, fill this in. If you can't, you're not ready to call.

| Problem | Impact (physical / technical / business / emotional) | Root cause |
|---|---|---|
| | | |
| | | |
| | | |

**How to fill it in:**

1. **Problems column** — list every real problem your product or service solves. Don't list features. List problems.
2. **Impact column** — for each problem, list downstream consequences across all four dimensions:
    - Physical: literal, observable, countable consequences (hours wasted, records mis-keyed)
    - Technical: process, system, capability consequences (data integrity, broken workflow)
    - Business: revenue, cost, growth, market consequences (lost deals, margin compression, churn)
    - Emotional: how the people involved feel (frustration, fear of being fired, embarrassment)
3. **Root cause column** — for each problem, list *why* it exists. There are usually only a finite number of root causes for any given problem. If you don't know, that's the discovery agenda.

**The PIC is your map for discovery.** Without it, you ask vague questions and chase impacts you can't help with. With it, you arrive *prepared* to teach the buyer about their own problems.

## 9. Active listening tactics (Voss)

Asking great questions doesn't matter if you don't listen. The 6 active listening behaviors that drive toward "that's right":

1. **Effective Pauses** — silence drains emotion from the dialogue. After your question or label, count to four before saying anything else.
2. **Minimal Encouragers** — "yes," "okay," "uh-huh," "I see." Keep them talking.
3. **Mirroring** — repeat the last 1–3 critical words as a question. Then *be silent for 4 seconds.* They will elaborate.
4. **Labeling** — "It seems like…", "It sounds like…", "It looks like…". Validates by acknowledging.
5. **Paraphrase** — repeat what they said in *your own* words.
6. **Summarize** — paraphrase + label. Capture "the world according to your counterpart" so completely that the only natural response is "that's right."

The path to "that's right":
**Listen → Paraphrase → Label → Summarize → "that's right."**

## 10. The talk-time check

After every call, ask: what was my talk-time ratio?

| Talk-time | Diagnosis |
|---|---|
| <30% rep / >70% buyer | Excellent discovery (Carnegie territory) |
| 30–50% rep | Top-rep zone (Gong data clusters here for high performers) |
| 50–70% rep | You're pitching, not discovering |
| >70% rep | You lost. Even if they said "great call." |

Modern Gong/Chorus data confirms what Carnegie wrote in 1936: top performers talk less. The exception is *teaching* moments (Challenger reframe) — those are concentrated bursts where you talk a lot, but they're a small % of total call time.

## 11. Question taxonomy quick reference

When you need a question and don't know which type, use this diagnostic:

- Need to **establish a fact**? → Situation (sparingly).
- Need to **surface a problem**? → Problem.
- Need to **make a problem feel bigger**? → Implication.
- Need the **buyer to articulate value**? → Need-payoff.
- Need the **buyer to consider change** when they're comfortable? → Provoking.
- Need to **defuse an emotion** or **handle a stuck moment**? → Calibrated (How / What).
- Need to **uncover hidden stakeholders**? → Behind-the-table calibrated.
- Need to **quantify cost of inaction**? → Deal-killer calibrated.
- Need to **validate** what you've heard? → "What I'm hearing is X. Did I get that right?"

---

## One last principle

The best question is the one you don't ask out loud — because the buyer is already telling you the answer if you'd shut up and listen.

Voss again: "Listening is the cheapest concession a negotiator can make. And the most powerful." Most "I don't have a question to ask" moments are actually "I haven't paused long enough to hear the next one."

Practice: after asking your next question, count to 5 in your head before saying anything. The data point you'll get in those 5 seconds is usually the one that closes the deal.
