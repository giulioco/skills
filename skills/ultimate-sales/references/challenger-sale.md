# The Challenger Sale — Extracted Frameworks

*Matthew Dixon & Brent Adamson, CEB (Corporate Executive Board), 2011*

---

## 1. Core Thesis

CEB studied 6,000+ B2B sales reps across 90+ companies, controlled for tenure, geography, and account size. Every rep falls into one of **five behavioral profiles** (derived via factor analysis on 44 attributes). When you compare profile to actual performance against goal:

- **Challengers dominate.** ~40% of all star performers are Challengers.
- **Relationship Builders fail.** Only 7% of stars are Relationship Builders.
- **In complex/solution sales, Challengers are 54%+ of stars** — Relationship Builders nearly disappear.
- **In transactional sales, the gap narrows** — Hard Workers do nearly as well as Challengers.

> "While there may be five ways to be average, there's clearly a dominant way to be a star. And that, by far, is the Challenger profile, comprising nearly 40 percent of all high performers in our study."

The deeper insight: this isn't a "down economy" finding. Challengers win because they've mastered the **complex sale**, not a complex economy.

> "How you sell has become more important than what you sell."

> "An effective sales force is a more sustainable competitive advantage than a great product stream."

### Why Solution Selling Broke Old Methods

Solution selling places huge burdens on customers (time, timing, consensus). Customers responded with four behaviors that broke traditional selling:

1. **Rise of consensus-based buying** — senior decision makers won't bet alone on big purchases.
2. **Increased risk aversion** — customers want suppliers to share risk and tie compensation to customer business outcomes.
3. **Greater demand for customization** — "if you're solving my problem, customize for free."
4. **Rise of third-party consultants** — extracting "value" by squeezing suppliers on price.

Result: **In solution-selling environments, the gap between core and star reps is ~200% (vs. 59% in transactional).** Stars are carrying entire companies.

> "Solutions fatigue" — customers are sick of long discovery, ROI ping-pong, and acts of faith.

## 2. The Five Sales Rep Profiles

Built from factor analysis of 44 attributes. Five behavioral clusters emerged statistically — not invented categories. Reps "major" in one profile but have traces of all five.

### The Hard Worker
- Self-motivated, doesn't give up easily
- Makes more calls, more visits than anyone
- Seeks feedback, follows the sales process
- Believes effort + process = results
- **Wins in transactional sales** (call volume matters more than call quality)

### The Relationship Builder
- Builds personal/professional relationships and advocates inside customer org
- Generous with time, accessibility-driven, service-oriented
- "Whatever you need, I'm here to make that happen"
- **Strives to RESOLVE tension** — to be accepted into customer's comfort zone
- Focused on customer convenience, not customer value
- **Worst-performing profile in complex sales** — only 7% of stars

### The Lone Wolf
- Deeply self-confident, follows own instincts not rules
- "Prima donnas" — no process compliance, no CRM entries, no trip reports
- Drive sales leaders crazy but hit numbers
- "I'd fire them if I could, but I can't, because they're crushing their numbers."
- Scarce and unmanageable; second-best in complex sales but not scalable
- A team of Lone Wolves is "a team that doesn't sell anything"

### The Reactive Problem Solver
- Highly reliable, detail-oriented
- Focused on post-sales follow-up and ensuring promises are kept
- "A customer service rep in sales rep clothing"
- Solves existing customer problems instead of generating new business

### The Challenger
- Deep understanding of customer's business
- Pushes customer thinking — teaches them something new about how to compete
- Not afraid to share contrarian views
- Assertive — presses customers on thinking and pricing
- Pushes own managers as well
- Maintains **constructive tension** in every sale

The defining traits of a Challenger (statistically significant):
1. Offers the customer unique perspectives
2. Has strong two-way communication skills
3. Knows the individual customer's value drivers
4. Can identify economic drivers of the customer's business
5. Is comfortable discussing money
6. Can pressure the customer

### Performance Distribution (Star Performers)

| Profile | % of Stars (Overall) | % of Stars (Complex Sales) |
|---|---|---|
| Challenger | ~40% | ~54% |
| Lone Wolf | ~25% | ~25% |
| Hard Worker | ~17% | low |
| Reactive Problem Solver | ~12% | low |
| Relationship Builder | **7%** | **~0%** |

Distribution among **core (average) performers** is roughly even across all five profiles. There are five ways to be average, but only one dominant way to be a star.

### Why Relationship Builders Fail in Complex Sales

The job in complex sales is **disrupting** the customer — getting them to change behavior. Relationship Builders try to defuse tension and accommodate; they cannot drive disruptive change. Familiarity alone doesn't win business when customers are reluctant to buy.

> "While the Challenger is focused on pushing the customer out of their comfort zone, the Relationship Builder is focused on being accepted into it... The Challenger rep wins by maintaining a certain amount of constructive tension across the sale. The Relationship Builder, on the other hand, strives to resolve or defuse tension, not create it."

> "A service-oriented quarterly check-in call with your customer can be a great way to find business, but it's not a very good way to make business."

## 3. The Three T's of Challenger: Teach, Tailor, Take Control

The Challenger Selling Model rests on three pillars, executed simultaneously through constructive tension.

### Teach (for differentiation)
- Offer unique perspectives on the customer's business
- Two-way dialogue
- Insight that helps them save money or make money in ways they didn't know
- Connect every insight back to a capability where you outperform competitors
- "Don't lead with, lead to"

### Tailor (for resonance)
- Know individual stakeholder's value drivers and economic drivers
- Adapt the message to industry, company, role, and individual
- Build consensus across the buying committee
- "Address each customer stakeholder as if they actually was the customer"

### Take Control (of the sale)
- Comfort with money/pricing conversations
- Ability to pressure the customer when needed
- Push back on discount asks; push for value, not price
- Move customers out of their comfort zone with control, diplomacy, empathy
- Take control THROUGHOUT the sale, not just at the negotiating table

### The Combination Matters

> "If you teach without tailoring, you come off as irrelevant. If you tailor but don't teach, you risk sounding like every other supplier. If you take control but offer no value, you risk being simply annoying."

All three must operate together. Cherry-picking elements partially helps but never delivers the full performance gain.

### Skill vs. Organization

| Capability | Individual rep skill | Organizational capability |
|---|---|---|
| Teach | low | high (marketing must build pitches) |
| Tailor | medium | medium (research, segmentation, intel) |
| Take Control | high | low (training, frameworks) |

Teaching content cannot be left to individual reps — they will promise things you can't deliver. Marketing must build commercial teaching pitches at scale.

## 4. Customer Loyalty Drivers (the 53% Insight)

CEB surveyed 5,000+ B2B customers across product, brand, service, price-to-value, and sales experience attributes, against three loyalty questions:
- Willingness to keep buying
- Willingness to buy more going forward
- Willingness to advocate for the supplier

### Result

| Loyalty Driver | % of B2B Customer Loyalty |
|---|---|
| Brand, Product, Service combined | 38% |
| Price-to-value ratio | 9% |
| **Sales experience itself** | **53%** |

> "Loyalty isn't won in product development centers, in advertisements, or on toll-free help lines: Loyalty is won out in the field, in the trenches, during the sales call."

> "Over half of customer loyalty is a result not of what you sell, but how you sell."

Customers see far less differentiation between suppliers' brands/products than suppliers think they do. Brand/product/service are the **price of entry**, not the basis for loyalty.

### Top Sales-Experience Drivers of Loyalty (in rank order)

1. Rep offers unique and valuable perspectives on the market
2. Rep helps me navigate alternatives
3. Rep provides ongoing advice or consultation
4. Rep helps me avoid potential land mines
5. Rep educates me on new issues and outcomes
6. Supplier is easy to buy from
7. Supplier has widespread support across my organization

> "Stop wasting my time. Challenge me. Teach me something new."

The customer's #1 ask of a sales rep is not to be diagnosed — it's to be **taught**.

> "What I really need is a rep who knows my needs better than I do — one who can challenge me to think differently about my business altogether."

## 5. Commercial Teaching: The Four Rules

Teaching alone isn't enough — you can teach customers something great and lose the deal to a competitor (free consulting). **Commercial Teaching** is teaching that reliably produces a commercial win.

### Rule 1: Lead to Your Unique Strengths

> "The sweet spot of customer loyalty is outperforming your competitors on those things you've taught your customers are important."

Every insight must connect back to a capability where YOU outperform. Otherwise you're providing free consulting.

The "Deb Oler question" (from W.W. Grainger):
> "Why should our customers buy from us over anyone else?"

CEB found:
- Only 35% of B2B companies can claim true preference over competition
- Only ~half of "unique benefits" are perceived as relevant by customers
- Only **14%** of companies' claimed unique benefits are perceived by customers as both unique AND beneficial
- "Innovative," "customer-focused," "green," "solutions-oriented" are NOT among them

Don't "teach customers into the desert" — make sure you have a real solution to the problem you've reframed.

### Rule 2: Challenge Customers' Assumptions

The keyword: **reframe**.

The teaching must speak directly to the customer's world in a way that makes them say, **"Huh, I've never thought about it that way."**

> "Rapport and reframe are not the same thing. Just because you 'get' the customer's business doesn't mean you automatically get the customer's business."

If the customer says "Yes, I totally agree, that's exactly what's keeping me up at night!" — **you have failed**. You haven't reframed; you've only confirmed what they already knew. At best you're "teaching at the margins" and they've probably already started thinking of solutions (likely your competitor's).

The right reaction: thoughtful reflection, "What else don't I know?"

### Rule 3: Catalyze Action

It's not enough to change how customers think — get them to act. Build a business case that makes them feel real discomfort about the problem.

The right ROI calculator measures **the cost of inaction on the reframe**, NOT the ROI of buying your products.

> "Before they buy your solution, the customer has to buy the solution."

### Rule 4: Scale Across Customers

Build a small set of well-scripted insights with diagnostic questions to map the right insight to the right customer. Segment by need or behavior, not just industry/geography. Marketing serves as the "insight generation machine."

## 6. The 6-Step Commercial Teaching Pitch (The Choreography)

A world-class teaching conversation moves through **six discrete steps**, each building to the next. It's a story, not a presentation — with drama, suspense, and a roller-coaster from a dark place to the light at the end of the tunnel (your solution).

### Step 1: The Warmer

After formalities, lay out **your assessment** of the customer's key challenges before they speak.

> "We've worked with a number of companies similar to yours, and we've found that these three challenges come up again and again as by far the most troubling. Is that what you're seeing too, or would you add something else to the list?"

This is **Hypothesis-Based Selling** — lead with hypotheses of customer needs, not open questions. Customers love it because it's a "get" not a "give." You demonstrate "I understand your world" without making them educate you.

### Step 2: The Reframe

The pivot point of the entire conversation. Connect the challenges they just acknowledged to a **bigger problem or opportunity than they ever realized**. Just the headline — surprise them, make them curious.

The litmus test: **"Huh, I never thought of it that way before."**

If they say "Yes, totally!" — you've failed. If they're enthusiastically agreeing, you're not teaching, you're confirming.

### Step 3: Rational Drowning

Lay out the data, charts, and analysis that quantify the cost or opportunity. Make them squirm. **FUD factor** (fear, uncertainty, doubt) — make them feel they're drowning in unseen costs/missed opportunity.

Right reaction: "Wow, I had no idea we were wasting that kind of money!"

ROI calculator goes here — but it calculates the ROI of solving the reframed problem, NOT of buying your product.

### Step 4: Emotional Impact

Customer's natural defense: "I see what you're saying, but we're different."

You can't beat "we're different" with more data. You beat it with a story. Tell a vivid narrative of how other companies — just like them — went down a similar painful path, with details so specific they'll see themselves in it.

> "Whatever you say next has to feel immediately familiar."

Right reaction: a rueful shake of the head, "Wow, it's like you work here. We do that all the time."

### Step 5: A New Way

Convince them of the solution — but not YOUR solution yet. Show the **specific capabilities they would need** to solve this problem. It's about behaving differently, not buying differently.

> "Before they buy your solution, the customer has to buy the solution."

Right reaction: "You're right, that's what we need to do" or "That's the kind of company I want us to be."

### Step 6: Your Solution

NOW you show how YOU specifically can deliver the new way better than anyone else. This is where your unique capabilities pay off.

If your competition is still in play here, you've failed — either you have no truly unique capabilities, or you didn't lead to them well.

> "Don't lead with, lead to."

### Where the Supplier First Appears

The supplier doesn't enter the conversation until step 6. Steps 1–5 are entirely about the customer.

### How to Build a Teaching Pitch (Build Order)

1. Start at **Step 6** — what unique benefits do you have? Especially ones customers currently underappreciate.
2. Then **Step 2** — what reframe will make them value those benefits?
3. Ask: **"What's currently costing our customers more money than they realize, that only we can help them fix?"** Answer = heart of your pitch.
4. Fill in steps 3, 4, 5 to bridge from reframe to solution.

### The SAFE-BOLD Framework (Neil Rackham / KPMG)

A great teaching pitch must be:
- **Big** — more expansive than an ordinary idea
- **Outperforming** — risky enough that customer must take a real bet
- **Leading-edge** — new and unique
- **Difficult** — hard to do (otherwise why hire you?)

Watch out for "Relationship Builders with belt sanders" who soften the edges of pitches to make them safe and forgettable.

## 7. Tailoring for Resonance

Decision makers think of themselves as buying from organizations, not individuals — sales experience matters ~2x more than rep attributes for them. **Their #1 loyalty driver: "widespread support for the supplier across my organization."**

End users and influencers care primarily about the rep's professionalism, then the rep's ability to offer unique perspectives.

### The New Physics of Sales

Old model: rep extracts info from stakeholders, pitches decision maker directly.

New model: rep teaches insight to stakeholders, who then evangelize and build consensus internally. The link rep→decision maker is weak; the link stakeholder→decision maker is strong.

> "While you shouldn't stop calling on decision makers, those efforts do not negate the huge impact key stakeholders can have on driving more business over time."

Two-thirds of suppliers use stakeholder interactions to **extract** insight rather than **provide** it. That's backwards.

### Layers of Tailoring (Outside In)

1. **Industry** — trends, regulatory, M&A activity
2. **Company** — recent press, earnings, strategic priorities
3. **Role** — what specifically does this function care about
4. **Individual** — personal goals and motivations

### Outcome → Result Driver → Capability Chain

For each stakeholder, equip reps with a **functional bias card**:
- **Outcomes**: high-level decision criteria they're trying to achieve
- **Focus**: what they monitor day-to-day
- **Concerns**: questions they ask, what worries them
- **Value areas**: levers they can pull to improve performance

Then build a **Message-to-Role Map** showing:
- Which stakeholders matter for each solution
- Their key outcomes
- The result drivers (mechanisms) they'd use
- Scripting tying your capability to their outcomes

Outcomes are predictable, stable across people, finite per role, and scalable.

### Solae Case Study (Tailoring)

A soy ingredient maker found their reps used identical technical pitches with CMOs and procurement officers — meaningless to non-technical buyers. Solution: functional bias cards + scripting for each role + a **Value Planning Tool** that documents per-stakeholder objectives and gets each one to sign off in advance. Result: when the rep meets the decision maker, consensus is already documented on a single page.

## 8. Taking Control of the Sale

### Three Misconceptions

**Misconception 1**: Taking control = negotiating.
Wrong. Taking control happens throughout the sale. The first opportunity is at the very beginning.

Up to 20% of "opportunities" are veiled verification efforts where the customer has already chosen another vendor. Challengers sniff out these foils and **press for expanded access in exchange for continued dialogue** — if denied, they walk.

> "It's going to cost us $200K to put our best thinking into your problem. We're willing to do it, but we need some assurance that if we invest in you, you'll invest in us." — Neil Rackham anecdote

Challengers also "lead and simplify" — they teach the customer how to buy when the customer doesn't know how to buy a complex solution. Average reps "learn and react" — they let confused customers lead.

**Misconception 2**: Taking control is only about money.
Wrong. Bigger application: taking control of **ideas**. When the customer pushes back on the reframe ("we're different"), Challengers don't fold — they push back: "Your company surely is different, but so are these others we work with — let's explore this in more depth."

**Misconception 3**: Reps will become aggressive if you tell them to take control.
Wrong. The fear is misplaced — reps almost always default to passive, not aggressive.

### The Spectrum

Passive ←——— Assertive ———→ Aggressive

- **Passive** (Relationship Builders): gives in, accommodates, proactively offers discounts
- **Assertive** (Challengers): pursues goals constructively, uses strong language without being off-putting, shows respect and sensitivity
- **Aggressive**: attacks, antagonizes, pursues own agenda blindly

### Why Reps Default to Passive

1. **Perceived power imbalance**: 75% of reps think procurement has more power; 75% of procurement think reps have more power. Both are wrong about themselves.
2. **Recession psychology**: in tough times, reps cave to close any deal.
3. **Bad management messaging**: "be customer-centric" gets interpreted as "give the customer whatever they want."

### Comfort Discussing Money

Challengers are comfortable with money because they're confident in the value they provide. Their confidence comes from having taught the customer about a problem they didn't know they had — they created the burning platform, and they own the solution.

### DuPont Case Study: 4-Step Negotiation Framework

Based on BayGroup's Situational Sales Negotiation methodology:

1. **Acknowledge and Defer** — Get explicit permission to defer the price discussion. Verbatim script:
   > "I understand that price is something we need to address, but before we do, I'd like to take a moment to make sure I completely understand your needs — so we can make sure we're doing everything we can to make this deal as valuable as possible for you. Is that all right?"

2. **Deepen and Broaden** — Uncover all the customer's underlying needs beyond price (warranty, service, shipping, installation, business outcomes). Get them to restate what they like about your offering. When the customer asks for 20% off, ask "What are you looking to achieve with a 20% price reduction?"

3. **Explore and Compare** — Compare the value of various trade-offs. Now you're negotiating across the whole value picture, not just price.

4. **Concede According to Plan** — Don't start small and grow. Don't do "take it or leave it." Start with a meaningful concession and offer smaller and smaller ones — leaves customer feeling they won.

### Pre-Call Negotiation Planning Template

Before any tough call:
- Map relative power positions on every dimension (product, brand, service, relationship, pricing)
- List info you need from the customer + the questions you'll ask
- List info the customer will want + how you'll handle it
- Anticipated tough questions and your prepared responses
- Items you can concede + items you'll request, each scored 1–5 for cost-to-you and value-to-customer

### Powerful Requests

Throughout the sale, make requests that move things forward:
> "From our discussion, we've agreed that the implementation of a rack-based server solution would save you $5 million a year. For you to reap these savings in the current fiscal year, we really need to install the new hardware soon. So to get started, I will need a signed contract from Dave by next week..."

### Master Skill: Comfort with Ambiguity

> "Challengers thrive in ambiguity... They display a remarkable level of comfort with silence during the customer conversation, as well as with keeping negotiation points and customer objections open and on the table longer than one normally would."

The biggest obstacle to taking control is the **desire for closure**. Average reps want to resolve tension; Challengers can sit in it.

## 9. Mobilizers vs. Talkers (Consensus & Hidden Criteria)

Consensus is now the customer's #1 loyalty signal for decision makers. They won't bet alone on a big purchase.

Senior decision makers and procurement officers value the same things:
- Widespread support inside the org (top)
- Ease of doing business
- Rep doesn't waste their time

**Customization, willingness to negotiate price** — surprisingly low on the list.

The implication: **stop trying to bypass stakeholders to get to the C-suite directly.** The decision maker actually wants their team behind the deal. Build consensus by **teaching stakeholders something they value**, equipping them to evangelize internally.

Two-thirds of reps use stakeholder meetings to mine information. The Challenger flips this — they teach the stakeholder, who then sells internally.

## 10. Why Solution Selling Is Broken

Traditional solution selling assumes customers know their needs and will reveal them via skillful questioning. **Wrong.** Customers' single biggest need is figuring out what they need.

> "Rather than asking customers what they need, the better sales technique might in fact be to tell customers what they need."

> "What if customers truly don't know what they need? What if customers' single greatest need — ironically — is to figure out exactly what they need?"

Diagnosis-based selling falls down because:
- Asking customers what they need yields only what they already know
- Customer needs don't differentiate from competitors who are also "discovering" needs
- Solutions fatigue — protracted ping-pong is exhausting
- Customers don't want to teach you their business; they want you to teach them theirs

## 11. Implementing Challenger: Lessons for Sales Leaders

### Not Every High Performer Is a Challenger
Don't assume your top reps are Challengers — only ~40% are. Lone Wolves can also be high performers but their behavior isn't replicable. Use the diagnostic, not your manager's gut.

### Beware the Lone Wolf Trap
A 25% chance of a Lone Wolf being high-performing tempts leaders to want more of them. Don't. Lone Wolves don't follow patterns, can't be modeled, can't sell as a team. **"A team of Lone Wolves is a cancer."**

### Hire for Challengers
Use a Challenger Hiring Guide based on the six attributes. Sample questions:
- "How do you usually open a sales conversation with a customer?"
- "Can you describe a time when you got a customer to think of their problem differently?"
Red flags: feature/benefit-focused pitches, customer-centric platitudes.

### Develop Rep Skill and Org Capability in Parallel
Either alone fails. Reps without good content reject the model; content without trained reps gathers dust.

### Don't Just Change the Training — Change Before and After
87% of training content is forgotten within 30 days (Rackham). Three improvements:
1. Build social demand BEFORE training
2. Create "safe practice" experiential learning
3. Sustained behavioral certification (managers coach to it ongoing)

### Tolerate (Limited) Rejection
Shoot for 80% adoption. Let the final 20% come along — but only as long as they hit goal. The minute they slip, they must adopt or leave.

### Expect Casualties
20–30% of reps won't make the transition. Move them to customer service, marketing, product specialist roles where they thrive.

### Pilot First
W.W. Grainger pilots tools to learn:
1. How big is the early adopter group?
2. Who are early adopters and how do they differ from non-adopters?
3. What metrics predict tool impact?
4. What lessons can drive broader adoption?

Critical insight on rollout: people don't adopt because high performers succeed — they adopt because **people just like them** succeed. Document average performers who became Challengers, not just stars.

### Terminology Matters
Don't water down "Challenger." If reps can't see a clear A→B move (vs. v1→v2), they won't bother changing. The best gauge of message power is how many people disagree and want to debate.

### Globally
The Challenger model works in non-Western markets too. In Asia-Pacific, simply adding "respectfully" before "teach," "challenge," "take control" makes it land. Tailor execution; don't water down principle.

## 12. Lessons for Marketing Leaders

### Stop Telling the World How "Customer-Centric" You Are
Customer-centric language is the price of entry, not differentiation. True customer-centricity = understanding what your customer struggles with for ten hours a day.

### Answer the Deb Oler Question
"Why should your customers buy from you instead of your competitors?" If you can't answer, you don't have a Challenger model.

### Never Put These Ten Words in Your Pitch Deck
Per Adam Sherk's analysis of press releases:
1. Leader (161,000 mentions)
2. Leading
3. Best
4. Top
5. Unique
6. Great
7. Solution
8. Largest
9. Innovative
10. Innovator

> "By definition, there can be only one leader in any industry — and 161,000 companies each think they're it."

> "Every time I hear the word 'value,' my defenses go up, because that's when I know they're trying to sell me something." — procurement executive

Two rules:
1. **Be memorable, not agreeable.** Frame around an edgy insight or be forgotten.
2. **Lead TO your solution, not WITH it.** Teach about a problem they didn't know they had before you describe what you do.

## 13. The Manager's Role in the Challenger Selling Model

Sales Leadership Diagnostic on 2,500+ frontline managers found 5 factors. Recalibrated to the sales side (~75% of total):

| Factor | % of Sales-Side Manager Effectiveness |
|---|---|
| Sales Innovation | 29% |
| Coaching | 28% |
| Selling | 25% |
| Resource Allocation | 16% |

### Hire for Management Fundamentals (the other 25%)
Integrity, reliability, listening — these are binary. Either you have them or you don't. Don't try to develop them. Use experiential, "live fire" simulations to assess candidates before hiring.

> "Great reps don't necessarily make great managers."

### Coaching: Effect on Performance Curve
Improved coaching quality has minimal effect on stars (already great) and minimal effect on poor performers (can't coach away a bad fit). It produces **6–19% performance lift on the core (middle 60%)**.

> "When you improve coaching quality, the performance curve doesn't shift, it tips. The middle moves, but the feet don't."

Don't coach democratically — focus on the core, not the bottom or top.

Bad coaching also drives turnover and saps discretionary effort across the entire force.

### Coaching ≠ Training ≠ Managing
- Training = sharing knowledge in a one-size-fits-all format
- Coaching = ongoing, dynamic, individualized job-embedded behavioral diagnosis and reinforcement
- Managing = telling/doing
- Coaching = asking/guiding

### Give Managers Something to Coach To
A sales-process-aligned coaching guide: each stage has explicit objectives + sample questions. Avoid "spreadsheet coaching" that focuses on outcomes ("your conversion is down") — coach to behaviors.

### PAUSE Framework (Hypothesis-Based Coaching)

- **P**reparation for the conversation
- **A**ffirm the relationship (separate coaching from performance management)
- **U**nderstand expected (observed) behavior
- **S**pecify behavior change
- **E**mbed new behaviors

### Sales Innovation: The Missing Manager Skill

Innovation isn't about new products — it's about **creatively unsticking stalled deals** at the customer level. Three components:
1. **Investigate** — map the customer's decision process and obstacles
2. **Create** — reposition capabilities, shift risk, restructure the deal
3. **Share** — codify and spread innovations across the team

70–80% of manager time with reps is "deal inspection" ("Did you call them? Did you send the proposal?"). That's not innovation — it's checking up. Real innovation = collaborative co-creation.

> "If we had religiously followed our sales process last year, our three biggest deals would have never gotten done."

Star managers are often called "sales ninjas" — they find a way in when no one else can.

### Efficiency vs. Effectiveness
Resource allocation = efficiency. Sales innovation = effectiveness. **Effectiveness focus has nearly 2x the impact of efficiency focus on manager performance** — yet most companies are designed for efficiency.

### Six Cognitive Biases That Block Innovation

1. **Practicality bias** — discard "unrealistic" ideas
2. **Confirmation bias** — ignore unexplainable customer behaviors
3. **Exportability bias** — "if it didn't work here, it won't work anywhere"
4. **Legacy bias** — "the way we've always done it"
5. **First conclusion bias** — first explanation is usually best
6. **Personal bias** — "if I wouldn't buy it, the customer won't either"

### Narrowing vs. Opening Thinking

Narrowing thinking: weigh existing options, produce a single solution. Good for fast resource decisions, bad for innovation.
Opening thinking: generate and vet many alternatives. Required for sales innovation.

### SCAMMPERR Framework (Brainstorming)

A forcing function for opening thinking:
- **S**ubstitute
- **C**ombine
- **A**dapt
- **M**odify
- **M**agnify
- **P**ut to other use
- **E**liminate
- **R**earrange
- **R**eorder/reverse

Apply when a deal is stuck. "What might we substitute? Combine? Adapt from elsewhere? Modify? Eliminate (e.g., custom packaging)?"

## 15. Reframe Examples From the Book

### Reframe Example 1: W.W. Grainger — "The Power of Planning the Unplanned"

**Customer assumption**: MRO purchases (hammers, bulbs, gloves, pumps) are an unimportant operational expense, manageable by transactional buying.

**Reframe**: Most companies break MRO spend by product category (tools, safety, lighting). They should break it by **how they buy** — planned vs. unplanned. **40% of typical MRO spend is unplanned**, and unplanned purchases often cost 5–10x the item price in hidden process costs (people on hold, paperwork, last-minute pickups, "Parts Orphanage" of overbuy inventory, hundreds of last-minute suppliers with zero leverage).

**Capability lead-to**: Only Grainger can supply such a wide product range across so many categories at scale, allowing customers to consolidate their unplanned purchases with one supplier.

### Reframe Example 2: ADP Dealer Services — "Total Dealer Spend"

**Customer assumption**: To save money in a declining auto market, buy point solutions from low-cost vendors for each part of the business (sales office, service center, parts).

**Reframe**: Dealers work with an average of **12 vendors**, creating up to **40% redundant costs** in overlapping IT, training, integration, support. The point solutions you bought to save money are actually costing more in hidden inefficiency.

**Capability lead-to**: ADP is the only single-supplier integrated solution for dealerships, eliminating the duplicative cost.

### Reframe Example 3: Office Furniture Manufacturer

**Customer assumption**: Big collaborative spaces foster collaboration. CEO ordered large conference rooms in the new HQ; competitor selected to furnish.

**Reframe**: "Collaboration doesn't happen in groups of eight. It happens in twos and threes. When you get to seven it stops being productive. You may be building the wrong size conference rooms."

**Capability lead-to**: Movable walls + collaboration-furniture products. Won foothold in account already lost.

### Reframe Example 4: Pharmaceutical Patient Journeys

**Customer assumption**: Family physician treats patient based on visit-by-visit symptoms.

**Reframe**: Patients with this illness average 2.5 exacerbations per year — most go to ER, never reported back to family physician. So the GP is treating a less severe condition than the patient actually has. Showing the "patient journey" lets the GP change treatment to prevent ER visits.

**Capability lead-to**: Deep disease-management knowledge that competitor reps don't have. Got physician access in a saturated, undifferentiated industry.

### Reframe Example 5: Employee Benefits Supplier (RFP Reshape)

**Customer assumption**: We'll put benefits administration out to competitive bid to save money.

**Reframe**: Supplier declined the bid. Then offered free consulting on writing the RFP — telling the customer which 4 supplier claims are wrong, which 3 to insist on, which 2 are unnecessary, all explained by why. Once the customer's RFP was rebuilt to those specs, supplier said: "If that's the bid, we'd like to participate — it describes the partnership we want."

**Capability lead-to**: The RFP the customer ended up issuing was the supplier's unique capability set described as customer requirements. Won the deal.

## 16. Complete Commercial Teaching Pitch Example (W.W. Grainger Walkthrough)

**Setup / Agenda**: Positioned as a "get" for the customer — "We've learned some interesting things about how companies could save a lot of money by thinking differently about their MRO spend."

**Step 1 — Warmer**:
"You face a host of challenges every day — production line issues, workers' comp costs, maintenance and safety issues. Especially the ones that keep your business running every day. Which of these are most pressing for you right now?"
[Customer engages and selects 1–2 challenges. Conversation feels like commiseration between colleagues.]

**Step 2 — Reframe**:
"Most companies think about MRO spend by product category — tools, safety, lighting, janitorial. But there's a different way to look at it: how you buy, not what you buy. Specifically — planned vs. unplanned purchases.

Planned = products bought regularly, on cycle, budgeted in advance.

Unplanned = products bought at the last minute in response to an unforeseen need.

Our research shows 40% of a typical company's MRO spend is unplanned. That makes unplanned bigger than any single product category — millions of dollars in last-minute, one-off spend you've never tracked."

[Reaction sought: "Huh, I'd never thought about it that way."]

**Step 3 — Rational Drowning**:
"It's worse than you think. Most unplanned purchases are also infrequent — bought once, never again. Yet each one requires extra time, effort, people, money. And here's the kicker:

- Companies use a small number of suppliers for planned purchases. For unplanned, they use **hundreds** — wherever they can find the part fastest. No leverage. Every item bought at full retail.
- Real cost is not the item — it's the process: finding the part, generating the invoice, calling, ordering, inventorying, paying. Any single unplanned purchase touches 5–10 people. The buying process is often more expensive than the part itself.
- A $17 hammer actually costs $117."

[Customer feels sick. Doing the math on 40% of total spend.]

**Step 4 — Emotional Impact (The "Pain Chain")**:
"Here's how this plays out at companies just like yours. Your CEO's air conditioner breaks midsummer. You call your usual supplier — sold out, 2 weeks. Call another — doesn't carry it. Call a third — supposedly has 2 in stock, can't find them. Two hours on hold listening to elevator music, and you have nothing.

Finally a fourth supplier across town has the part. You pull two guys off the production line, send them across town in rush hour. They get there and ask 'Boss, they have three of these. Want me to grab the others, just in case?' You say YES because you never want this hell again. You use one to fix the AC. The other two go to the 'Parts Orphanage' shelf in the warehouse. A year later they're obsolete. You'll never use them.

Cash you tied up in inventory — that you could have spent on something you actually need."

[Reaction: "Wow, you know us too well. We do that every single day."]

**Step 5 — A New Way**:
"That's just one purchase in one category. Multiply it across every MRO category, every month. No company is structured to manage this spend across every category — but imagine if you could. Unplanned purchases are a massive amount of unnecessary spend tied up in unnecessary inventory. The opportunity is huge — for any company that can crack it.

A solution would have to: consolidate hundreds of unplanned suppliers into one. Make any product available anywhere, anytime, fast — so you don't have to buy 'just in case.' Provide visibility into what you're spending on unplanned. Eliminate the process tax."

**Step 6 — Your Solution**:
"That's exactly what Grainger does. We carry hundreds of thousands of products. Branches everywhere. E-commerce. Catalog. Whatever you need, wherever, whenever. We replace the parts orphanage with on-demand delivery, the hundred-supplier sprawl with a single agreement, and we give you visibility into the unplanned spend you've never tracked.

[If existing customer: 'Let's look at your purchase history right now.' If new: 'Let's run a diagnostic of your unplanned spend over the last 12 months.']"

**Result**: Customer no longer sees Grainger as the place to buy $17 hammers. They see Grainger as the partner who helps them stop buying $117 hammers. Conversation pivots from price to partnership.

## Appendix: Challenger Hiring Interview Questions

For each Challenger competency, probe with questions like:

**Unique perspective**: "How do you usually open a sales conversation?" "Describe a time you got a customer to think differently about a problem."
- Look for: customer-benefit framing before supplier strengths, unique insights
- Red flag: feature/benefit pitches

**Two-way communication**: "How do you handle a customer who disagrees with your premise?"
- Look for: comfort with pushback, ability to maintain stance with respect

**Knows value drivers**: "How do you prepare for a meeting with a CFO vs. a head of operations?"
- Look for: role-specific tailoring instinct

**Comfortable with money**: "Tell me about a time you held the line on price."
- Look for: confidence, value justification
- Red flag: stories about getting deals approved for discounts

**Pressures customer**: "Tell me about a deal you walked away from."
- Look for: knowing when to disqualify, willingness to push for next steps
