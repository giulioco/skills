# Gap Selling — Keenan
## Extract for sales coaching skill

---

## 1. Core Thesis

Sales = closing the gap between the buyer's **Current State** and **Future State**.

- People don't buy products. They buy a better version of themselves or their business.
- The gap *is* the value of the sale. No gap = no sale.
- Bigger gap = bigger sale. Smaller gap = smaller (or no) sale.
- The salesperson's job is not to pitch — it is to **diagnose** the buyer's problem, the impact of that problem, and the root cause, then build the bridge.

> "You aren't losing sales because you can't sell. You're losing them because you don't understand how to diagnose your customer problem(s) and how the problem(s) drive the sale. Your product doesn't drive the sale. The problem does."

> "It's not about pitching products and services; it's about solving problems and making people's lives easier."

> "Selling is a giving profession."

---

## 2. The Nine Truth Bombs of Selling

| # | Truth | What it means in practice |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **No problem, no sale** | Every sale starts with a problem. If the buyer has no problem, there is nothing to sell. |
| 2 | **In every sale there's a gap** | Distance between current state and future state. Gap size = sale size. |
| 3 | **All sales are about change** | You're not selling a product. You're selling change. |
| 4 | **Customers don't like change** (even when they say they do) | Longevity bias is real. Familiarity wins by default. |
| 5 | **Sales are emotional** | Change is emotional, therefore every sale is emotional. |
| 6 | **Customers DO like change when they feel it's worth the cost** | Pain of staying must exceed pain of moving. |
| 7 | **Asking "Why" gets customers to "Yes"** | "Why" digs to intrinsic motivation. Without it, no urgency. |
| 8 | **Sales happen when the future state is a better state** | Anchor the buyer in the better future. |
| 9 | **No one gives a shit about you** | The show-me economy. Your awards, history, INC 5000 ranking — irrelevant. |

## 3. Current State / Future State / The Gap

### Current State (CS) — where the buyer is *now*
The world your buyer lives in today — their perceived reality. Composed of **5 critical elements**:

1. **The Facts (Physical)** — Literal, physical realities. Org structure, processes, tools, headcount, volumes, current methodology. *Anything quantifiable about how they operate today.*
2. **The Problems** — What's not working in the current state. *People don't buy products — they buy solutions to their problems.*
3. **The Impact** — The downstream consequences of the problems. *This is where urgency, value, and need live.*
4. **The Root Cause** — Why the problem exists. *You can't solve a problem if you don't know why it's happening.*
5. **The Emotional State** — How the buyer feels about the problem. Frustrated? Angry? Afraid? Exhausted?

### Future State (FS) — where the buyer wants to be
The vision of the buyer's world with their problems solved. Includes:

- The **literal outcomes** (faster delivery, more leads, X% revenue growth, etc.)
- The **impacts of those outcomes** (promotion, less stress, hitting board targets, beating the competition)
- The **emotional state** in the future (relief, pride, confidence)

### The Gap = FS − CS
- The gap is where the **value of the sale lives**.
- Buyers rarely see the gap clearly at first. The salesperson's job is to **expose and shape** the gap.
- Sometimes you'll discover the gap is small — you've just qualified out. That's a win, not a loss.
- The bigger the gap (and the more vividly the buyer sees it), the more urgent and obvious the buy decision becomes.

> "When it comes to change, the current state represents the pain, and the future state represents the pleasure."

> "Establishing your customers' current state (pain) while anchoring them in the future state (pleasure) primes them to be open to, and even enthusiastic about, allowing you to help them plan for the journey that will take them across the chasm."

## 4. Problem-Centric Selling: "No Problem, No Sale"

Problems — not solutions, not products, not features — are the **unit of sales**.

- Every sale starts with a buyer who is unhappy, frustrated, angry, sad, or irritated about something.
- That negative emotion is the **catalyst for change**.
- If you cannot articulate a clear problem your buyer has, you do not have a deal.
- A "need" is not a problem. Don't sell to needs — sell to problems.

> "Every sale is initiated with someone being unhappy, frustrated, angry, sad, irritated, or some other emotion about a problem that is negatively affecting them. This frustration with the problem acts as the catalyst for change."

> "If they can't recognize at least one clearly defined, measurable problem, your buyer will not buy."

### Headache / Brain Tumor Analogy
Keenan's signature illustration:
- Headache + cheap OTC drug → tiny gap → no urgency, won't pay much.
- Headache + critical $500K proposal due in 12 hours → moderate gap.
- Headache caused by a brain tumor → massive gap → buyer will pay a million for the pill.

**Same surface problem ("I have a headache"), three radically different impacts, three radically different sales.** A salesperson who doesn't dig past the surface problem treats them all the same and loses two of three.

> "Problems get you to the impact and the impact is where urgency, value, and need live."

### Palm Pilot Story (anti-pattern → pattern)
Keenan walks into a Franklin Covey store wanting a charger because his Palm died. The clerk asks if he wants a case. Keenan, annoyed, says no. Right before ringing him up, the clerk asks: *"Is the reason your Palm is out of battery because the case presses on the buttons in your pocket?"* Yes. The clerk shows him a redesigned case with cutouts so the buttons can't be depressed. Keenan buys both.

**The lesson:**
- Buyer thought he needed a charger.
- Real problem was a battery-draining case.
- A salesperson who sells to the stated need treats the symptom. A gap seller diagnoses and treats the disease.

> "If you only solve the problem your buyer thinks they have instead of the one they really have, you haven't helped them at all. You're like a doctor treating the symptoms instead of the disease."

> "Never sell to need."

## 5. The Four Elements of Every Problem (Impact)

Every problem has impact across four dimensions. Discover all four or you've under-quantified the gap.

| Dimension | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical** | Literal, observable, countable consequences | Manual data entry of 25,000 records/year, 4 hours/day spent on reports |
| **Technical** | Process / system / capability consequences | Data integrity issues, broken workflows, can't scale, integration gaps |
| **Business** | Revenue, cost, growth, market consequences | Lost deals, slower time-to-market, margin compression, churn, missed quota |
| **Emotional** | How the people involved feel | Frustration, fear of being fired, embarrassment, exhaustion, blame, anxiety |

The emotional dimension is the most underused. It's also where deals actually get unlocked, because **change is emotional**.

## 6. Discovery is 80% of the Sale

> "The sale is won or lost during the discovery."

> "The close happens at the beginning, not the end."

### Gong data Keenan cites
- On **won** discovery calls: salespeople spent ~50% talking, ~50% listening (buyer talks > 50%).
- On **lost** discovery calls: salesperson talked ~80%, buyer talked ~28%.
- Number of questions asked early in the cycle is **linearly correlated** with closing the deal.

### Implications
- Discovery is not a stage — it is **the** stage.
- BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timing) is bunk. Discovery itself qualifies the deal.
- ABC ("Always Be Closing") is dead. There is no closing technique that compensates for bad discovery.
- Pitching during discovery is fatal. Stay problem-centric, not product-centric.

### Why BANT Fails (Keenan's breakdown)
- **Budget** — Buyers find the budget when the gap is big enough. ($5 for a headache pill becomes $1M for a brain tumor pill.)
- **Authority** — There are 5.4+ decision makers in B2B sales (Gartner). A non-authority can be a champion / mobilizer.
- **Need** — Sell to *problems*, not *needs*. Many buyers don't know they have a problem yet.
- **Timing** — Timing follows urgency, which follows impact. Surface enough impact and timing solves itself.

## 7. The Problem Identification Chart (PIC)

The pre-call planning tool. Built **before** you ever contact a prospect.

### Structure (3 columns)

| Problem | Impact | Root Cause |
|---|---|---|
| The thing that's broken in the buyer's world | What that problem costs them (physical, technical, business, emotional) | Why the problem exists in the first place |

### How to fill it in
1. **Problems column.** List every problem your product or service can solve. Real problems your buyers actually have. (Low close rates, missed deadlines, communication gaps, can't move inventory, can't generate leads, etc.)
2. **Impact column.** For each problem, list the downstream consequences. (Low close rates → high cost of sale → slow revenue → fewer customers → vulnerable to competition.)
3. **Root Cause column.** For each problem, list the *why*. There are usually only a finite number of root causes for any given problem. (Lack of leads → no marketing, wrong target market, bad messaging, poor prospecting skills, ineffective tooling, etc.)

### Why it matters
- It's your **map** for discovery — without it, you ask vague questions and chase impacts you can't help with.
- The root-cause column is where you build credibility (most reps can identify the problem; few can name the cause).
- It forces you to articulate: *"The reason my product/service exists is to ___________."* If you can't fill the blank, stop selling and figure that out first.

> "You wouldn't set out on a journey into the unknown without a map. This chart will serve as your map as you travel through your customers' world."

## 8. Root Cause vs. Symptom

- The **problem** is the symptom: car won't start, sales are down, leads are leaking.
- The **root cause** is *why*: dead battery, wrong ICP, broken handoff between sales and marketing.
- Treat the symptom and the problem comes back. Treat the cause and you earn loyalty.

> "The doctor who finally gives you an angiogram, confirms that your heartburn is a symptom of arterial blockage, and performs the angioplasty that permanently relieves your discomfort — the one who takes the time to figure out the root cause of the problem and eliminates it — is the one who will earn your loyalty and trust forever."

Heuristic: ask "why" until the buyer can't answer. The point you stop is usually the root cause — or the next person you need to talk to.

## 9. Truth & Lie: What Buyers Want vs. Say They Want

| What buyers SAY they want | What they ACTUALLY want |
|---|---|
| "I need a charger." | A device that doesn't keep dying. |
| "I need a faster system." | To stop being blamed for missed deadlines. |
| "I need cheaper printing." | To stop firefighting jammed machines and grainy outputs. |
| "I need more leads." | To hit quota and not get fired. |
| "I need a relationship-builder rep." | An expert who tells them the truth. |

Customers buy **outcomes and emotional states**, not features. Their stated need is almost always a proxy for a deeper intrinsic motivation. Asking "why" repeatedly surfaces the truth.

> "They may be very up front about the problem they want to solve, but it may not occur to them to tell you the real reason they want to solve it. They may not even know it themselves."

### Two customers, same stated problem, different motivations (Keenan's example)
Two managers want a productivity system to free up time at home. One wants to avoid a road-rage commute that's spiking his blood pressure. The other is adopting twins with special needs. The same surface "solution" delights one and outrages the other — because their **intrinsic motivations** differ. Without asking "why", you only help one of them and lose the other.

## 10. Relationships Don't Matter (Kinda) — Be Credible, Not Liked

- "People buy from people they like" is a myth. People buy from people who provide **value**.
- Liked + no value = no sale. Disliked + huge value = sale (we shop Amazon over the cute local bookstore daily).
- Challenger Sale data: Challengers (40% of top performers) outperform Relationship Builders (7% of top performers).
- Kurlan / Objective Mgmt Group: 89% of top 5% sellers do NOT need to be liked. 82% of bottom-quarter sellers DO need approval.

> "Be an expert, not a friend."

> "People want to go to hockey games with their friends; they want to buy from people who know their shit."

> "The best salespeople could drop out of sales at a moment's notice and start a consulting company that caters to the field in which they sell."

## 11. The Discovery Process — Step by Step

> "Your number one job when selling is to get the customer, buyer, or prospect to let you help them."

### Question Taxonomy (the 4 types)

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Probing** | Open-ended, presses for specific *facts/details* | "Walk me through how you market to first-time buyers." |
| **Process** | Open-ended, asks *how* something is done — at the tactical step level | "Tell me about your ticket-selling process step by step." |
| **Provoking** | Open-ended, gently pushes the buyer to consider their state from a new angle | "What happens when…?", "Has there ever been a time when…?", "If you did X, what do you think would happen?" |
| **Validating** | NOT open-ended. Confirms understanding before moving on. | "What I hear you saying is… Did I get that right?" |

### Command Statement Starter Words (Deb Calvert)
Use these instead of stacking direct interrogative questions — they invite a deeper conversation rather than feeling like an interrogation.

- "Tell me a little bit about…"
- "Help me understand…"
- "Please describe…"
- "Walk me through…"
- "Share with me…"

### The Four Discovery Steps

#### Step 1 — Discover the Facts (Current State, physical layer)
Probing + process questions. Capture every literal, quantifiable detail about the buyer's world that your product could touch — volumes, headcount, processes, current tools, growth rates, customer demographics, throughput. The goal is to know more than they expect you to know.

#### Step 2 — Discover the Problems
As facts emerge, problems surface naturally. Keep digging. One discovery often surfaces 5+ stacked problems. **Don't stop at the first.**

> "Don't just find problems — hunt them down."

Two flavors of problem to keep separate:
- **Technical problem** — broken system, process, or data flow. *Conversation starter.*
- **Business problem** — the impact on revenue, growth, market, customers, people. *What drives the sale.*

> "The technical problem isn't going to drive the sale; it's the conversation starter. The business problem is what is going to drive the sale."

#### Step 3 — Discover the Impact
Force the buyer to articulate the consequences. Quantify everything.

**Banish open-ended answers.** "We're not growing fast enough" is unusable.
- Buyer: "We're not growing fast enough."
- You: "Define that for me. What does 'fast enough' mean to you?"
- Buyer: "We're at 22%. We need to be at 25%."
- You now have a quantified gap.

> "Open-ended answers are unquantified answers."

You're hunting the **OMG moment** — the realization that drives urgency. Don't reveal it; let the buyer arrive at it through provoking questions.

#### Step 4 — Discover the Root Cause
Use provoking questions:
- "Why do you think this problem is happening?"
- "How do you think your current processes are affecting this?"
- "How has the implementation of this product affected your business?"

Problem ≠ root cause. Treating the symptom loses you loyalty; treating the cause earns it forever.

### Sprinkle Validating Questions Throughout
Always validate before changing topics:
- "Am I understanding you correctly?"
- "What I hear you saying is…"
- "Did I get this right?"

### When the Discovery Has Qualified the Deal
You can answer YES to all four:

1. Does the prospect have a problem you can fix?
2. Does the prospect agree they have a problem?
3. Does the prospect want to fix the problem?
4. Will the prospect go on a journey with you to fix the problem?

### Tone & Timing
- Tone is fundamental — same words in different tones mean different things. Match your tone to the buyer's emotional state. When asking sensitive questions (e.g. "your system is broken"), use humility and deference.
- Timing: just because you *can* ask a question doesn't mean you should. The point is to get information, not to ask questions. A question asked too early gets a shallow answer and you've burned it.

### The CRM Challenge
Have a manager or peer pull a random opportunity from your pipeline and read the discovery notes aloud — without naming the account. You should be able to identify which opportunity it is from the notes alone. If the notes could describe any of your deals, your discovery was too shallow. **Every opportunity should be uniquely identifiable from its notes.**

> "If you fail the CRM Challenge, it's a tell-tale sign you're selling your product, not selling to the desired outcome."

### Discovery is a Conversation, Not a Checklist
> "You sure as hell don't want to sound like you're going through a checklist as you speak to your customer. This is a conversation. Ask, then listen, then ask, listen, ask, listen."

## 12. Quantifying the Gap & Cost of Inaction

Once you have CS and FS, do the math:

```
Future State − Current State = The Gap
```

Examples:
- 20% growth target − 10% current = 10pt gap
- $110M revenue − $100M revenue = $10M gap
- 35% close rate − 30% close rate = 5pt gap
- $65K avg deal − $50K avg deal = $15K gap

> "You're not selling a sales package consulting service; you're selling $10M in additional revenue."

### Is the Gap Worth It?
Ask: *Is this gap big enough to justify what change will cost the buyer?*

- $100K to gain $10M? Easy yes.
- $100K to gain $1M? Maybe — depends on switching costs, comfort with status quo, politics.

If the gap looks too small, **try to widen it** with more discovery. If you can't, qualify out — recommend someone else and move on.

### Cost of Inaction (Price-Objection Frame)
Don't price your product. **Price the inaction.**

> "If you don't move forward till next year's budget, you'll lose $50K a month for the next six months. That's $300K. Are you prepared to lose that kind of money?"

The price you sell against is *the price the buyer is paying right now to stay in the current state* — and that price keeps compounding every day.

## 13. The Intersect — Where Solution Meets Gap

Once you know:
1. Current state (facts, problems, impact, root cause, emotional state)
2. Future state (outcomes, impact of outcomes, emotional state)
3. Intrinsic motivation ("Tell me a bit about what's driving this change")

…then and only then do you start pitching.

> "Now you have permission to start talking about your product or service! That's right, you finally get to pitch."

> "Crazy, isn't it, how much later in the process you start to actually sell when you're selling to the gap. People following the traditional selling playbook usually start here. And it's why they fail."

### The Three-Layer Future State (the intersect framework)
Once you understand intrinsic motivation, model the future state in three layers:

| Layer | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Technical Future State** | What the product/system will do | New software increases sales, simplifies buying, enables reports |
| **Business Future State** | The business outcomes that follow | Customer satisfaction up, more orders, word of mouth surges |
| **Core Future State** | The intrinsic motivation it serves | Beat the competition, stock price up, board is happy |

Pitch into all three layers. The core layer is where the real sale happens.

## 14. Demoing & Pitching — Demo Against the Gap

### The Four Rules of a Kickass Demo

#### Rule 1 — No discovery, no demo
Never combine discovery and demo into one meeting. Two separate meetings, ideally 3–4 days apart, so you can process the discovery and customize the demo.

> "If a customer refuses a discovery, inform them that the demo is actually a two-step process, and that without a discovery, it's impossible to conduct a proper, customized demo."

#### Rule 2 — No "ifs"
Demo Challenge: conduct a 45–60 minute demo without ever saying the word "if". If you find yourself saying "if you have this problem…", you didn't do enough discovery. You should *know* what's troubling the buyer.

#### Rule 3 — Stick to six features (max)
A demo should target only the buyer's top business problems, ranked by impact. More than six features = no time to land any of them.

> "It is always better to spend a lot of time highlighting one feature's business value than it is to spend a little time introducing a whole bunch of features that may or may not be relevant."

#### Rule 4 — Anchor the buyer in your solution
After every feature demo, ask an affirming question (the rare time yes/no answers are good):
- "Can you see how this feature will improve your churn rate?"
- "Do you see how this will shorten data input?"
- "Can you see how this will increase your conversions?"

Anchoring makes your solution the **first reference point** against which everything else gets compared.

### Start with the Demo Map (use your PIC)
Take the problems list, sort by impact (high → low), demo only those.

> "If an issue or problem doesn't appear on the list, don't mention it."

> "Your demo should show customers exactly how your product is going to look on them."

## 15. Moving Deals Through the Pipeline

### Three things to nail (in this order)

1. **Decision Criteria** — "How are you going to decide what's the best solution for you? What are the most important factors?"
   - Hold their criteria up against their stated future state. **Do they align?**
   - If they don't, challenge the misalignment ("I'm confused. You said X was your priority, but criterion Y won't get you there. Help me understand.")
   - Your job is to make sure they're using criteria that will *actually deliver* the outcome they say they want.

2. **Buying Process** — "Can you tell me a little about your company's buying process?"
   - Map every step: RFP, internal committee, legal, procurement, CEO sign-off, etc.
   - 5.4 buyers per B2B deal on average (Gartner). You need to know all of them.

3. **The Next Yes** — sales cycles are not linear; they're a *staircase* of small yeses.
   - A "yes" = a renewed agreement to keep working with you.
   - The buyer's effort line should rise with yours. If you're climbing alone, you're not selling — you're chasing.
   - Don't fixate on the final close; focus on the next small yes (the meeting, the test drive, the spec doc, the intro to the CFO).

### "I'm confused. You said…"
Keenan's four favorite words. Use them whenever you spot a gap between the buyer's stated future state and what they're actually doing.

> "Those four words, in that order, are four of my favorites… By prefacing your challenge with 'I'm confused, you said…' you give buyers a way to save face."

It works because:
- It implies the mistake might be yours, not theirs.
- It invites discussion instead of debate.
- It surfaces inconsistencies without attacking.

### You're Not the Customer's B*tch
A great salesperson is a consultant, partner, and asset — not a servant. Signs you've become subservient:
- They say jump, you ask how high.
- They ask for a quote and you send it without asking why.
- They want a free trial but won't share results.
- They make demands and you reciprocate without asking for anything back.

In a true partnership, both sides give. Ask for things in return. Stand behind your price. Refuse to send pricing before discovery.

> "If you find yourself afraid of losing the deal, it's probably a sign you've lost control… If you're afraid, it means you don't understand where the customer is."

## 16. Objections — Most Come from Poor Discovery

> "Never defend your product or service. Use gap selling and what you learn in the process to make the buyer defend their objection."

### The general objection-handling pattern
1. Hear the objection.
2. Hold it up against the buyer's stated future state.
3. Reply with "I'm confused. You said…"
4. Recite their stated outcome / impact / motivation.
5. Ask why this objection matters in the pursuit of that outcome.
6. **Shut up. Listen.**

### When a prospect goes dark
Don't beg with "just checking in". Be direct, reference their stated current/future state, and hold them accountable to their own words:

> "I'm confused. You said you were tired of getting beat out for bigger, more complex projects, and you agreed that my product would improve your value proposition tenfold and get you to your financial goals. So I'm surprised I haven't heard from you. Has something changed?"

### When a deal stalls
Same playbook. Remind them of the cost of inaction:

> "Tom, I'm more than happy to push that meeting off another week, but I'm a little confused. You said that every month that goes by without a new system in place you're losing money, and yet you were hoping to increase your profits next year by 4%. It's going to get awfully hard for you to reach that goal if we keep going at this rate, isn't it? Is that OK?"

### Price objections — the three flavors

| Objection | What's really going on | Counter |
|---|---|---|
| "It's too expensive" | They haven't internalized the value of the gap | "I'm confused. This will deliver $50M by 2020. Why is $80K too much for that outcome?" |
| "We don't have the budget" | The money exists but isn't allocated | Reframe: cost of waiting > cost of reallocating |
| "We can't afford it" | They literally don't have the money | Walk away. Nothing to do here. |

> "Don't let anyone determine the price of what you're selling based on the product, service, or widget. They're buying the outcome of your product, service, or widget."

After delivering the reframe — **shut up and listen**. Make them defend their position.

### Defending against decision criteria mismatches
If a buyer says "we want a local cleaning company", and earlier said "the most important thing is environmentally friendly products" — point out the inconsistency. Make them reconcile their stated criteria against their stated future state.

## 17. Buyer Types & Emotional State

### Kanter's 10 Threats that trigger resistance to change
(Watch for any of these — each can cause a buyer to pause and reconsider mid-deal.)

1. **Loss of control** — "I'll have less ownership of the outcome."
2. **Excess uncertainty** — "Too new, no data, too risky."
3. **Surprises** — Unexpected discounts, deadlines, last-minute pressure.
4. **Too much change at once** — "I have 2,000 other fires to fight."
5. **Loss of face** — "Buying this admits my last decision was wrong."
6. **Insecurity** — "I'm toast if this doesn't work."
7. **Extra work** — "I'll have to learn a new way to do everything."
8. **Ripple effect** — "What about the people downstream of me?"
9. **Past resentments** — "The last vendor screwed us, so I'm suspicious."
10. **Real danger** — "This will cost colleagues their jobs."

### Buyer Awareness Levels (implicit)
- **Unaware** — Doesn't know they have a problem. Demand creation territory.
- **Problem-aware** — Knows something's wrong but doesn't know the cause or solution.
- **Solution-aware** — Knows what kind of solution exists, comparing options.
- **Vendor-aware** — Down to a shortlist; comparing you against named competitors.

The best gap sellers fish in the **demand creation** waters — buyers who don't yet know they have a problem. (Ticketing-company example: buyer says they don't need consolidation; gap seller exposes 25,000/year manual entries and creates demand.)

### Anchoring & Longevity Bias
- Buyers ascribe more positive feelings to things that have been around longer (Univ. of Arkansas chocolate study — same chocolate rated higher when called "73 years old" than "3 years old").
- Use this when relevant, but never as your pitch — *show* longevity in your content, don't *tell* in the meeting.

## 18. Gap Prospecting — The First Yes

> "Prospecting is the first yes."

### The Email/Call Math
```
Offer − Ask = Value
```

If the value is negative or near zero, the prospect's brain will trash you. Write every outreach so the value is unambiguously positive *for them*.

### Why Most Prospecting Fails
The brain runs information triage. Most cold outreach matches a familiar pattern (self-promotional, product-pushy, "let me tell you about us") and gets pre-conscious-rejected. To break through, you need to trigger the **"oh-shit" circuit** — the anterior cingulate cortex's surprise response.

### Three Ways to Create Intrigue (trigger the "oh-shit" circuit)

| Method | What it is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Surprise** | Something unexpected | A box of fresh croissants delivered with the first email |
| **Mystery** | Anticipation that only engagement can soothe | "Did you know that your competitor shortened their manufacturing cycle by three days using our product?" |
| **Knowledge gap** | Show you know something they should know but don't | "Were you aware that only 54% of salespeople make quota because of poor selling environments, not poor sales training?" |

The knowledge gap is Keenan's favorite — it positions you as the expert *and* creates curiosity pain that only engaging can relieve.

### Anatomy of a Compelling Outreach
Every prospecting touch must contain:
1. **Intrigue** (subject line / opening triggers the oh-shit circuit)
2. **A clear, reasonable ask** (proportional to the offer — 15 minutes is "expensive")
3. **An offer of value** (not "let me introduce my product" — instead "let me show you whether you're losing $X")
4. **Net positive for the buyer** (Offer − Ask > 0)

### Examples of bad vs. good asks
| Bad ask | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| "15 min to introduce our expense report tool" | No problem stated, no impact, all about you |
| "30 min to discuss your business and see where I can help" | They have to do all the work, no value offered |
| "45 min to show how our product reduces churn 35%" | Product-first; assumes they have the problem |

| Good ask | Why it works |
|---|---|
| "15 min to determine if your expense process is costing you $10K+/year" | Buyer-centric, quantified, intrigue, ask < offer |

### Cadence
8–12 touches over weeks. Mix channels (email, phone, LinkedIn, video, even mail). Each touch:
- Different message
- Different impact emphasized
- New piece of research / insight
- Always problem-centric, never product-centric

> "I never, ever, ever say no for a buyer. I make the buyer say no."

If silence persists past touch 9 or 10, send a direct accountability email putting the burden back on them.

### Building the ICP from the PIC
1. From the PIC, ask: *what kind of company would have these problems?* (industry, size, departments, roles)
2. Within those companies, *who would suffer most?* That's your ideal buyer.
3. Rank prospects:
   - **A list**: Many problems × high impact → top priority
   - **B list**: Few problems × high impact → priority
   - **C list**: Many problems × low impact → opportunistic
   - **D list**: Few problems × low impact → don't bother
4. **Pain matters more than problem count.** A buyer with one painful problem beats one with five tolerable ones.

## 20. The Healthy Pipeline / Commit Culture (for managers)

### Healthy pipeline checklist
- Accurate close dates (within 30 days)
- Accurate quarterly commits within ±15%
- Clear next yeses
- Clear deal strategies
- Adequate coverage (pipeline / close rate ≥ quota)
- **Evidence** for every claim

### The 7-question pipeline review
1. Do my reps understand the customer's current state?
2. Do they understand the future state?
3. Can they identify the gap and explain how they calculated it?
4. Can they name the buyer's intrinsic motivation?
5. Can they list the decision criteria?
6. Can they provide evidence for all of the above?
7. Do they know what happens next and when?

> "Be skeptical of the talker… people who don't know what they're talking about are the ones who talk the most."

### Commit Culture
- Reps commit to a number; managers cannot override it.
- Misses must stay within ±15%. Under-committing is just as wrong as over-committing.
- Knowing on day 1 that the team will miss by $200K is a *gift* — you have 90 days to fix it.
- > "Commit is an accuracy game, not a goal game."

## 22. Sales Dialogues from the Book

### Dialogue 1 — Demand Creation: Ticketing Company
The classic "I don't have a problem" → "holy crap I have a problem" sequence.

- **Seller:** "Have you ever considered consolidating your ticketing and CRM systems?"
- **Buyer:** "Nope. We don't need to."
- **Seller:** "Why not?"
- **Buyer:** "We send out emails to our customers, our online ticketing works great."
- **Seller:** "When someone buys a ticket, how do you get their name into the CRM?"
- **Buyer:** "We type it in."
- **Seller:** "How many new people come to shows every year?"
- **Buyer:** "About 25% of our customers are first-time buyers."
- **Seller:** "How many tickets do you sell per year?"
- **Buyer:** "About 100,000."
- **Seller:** "So you're manually entering 25,000 people into your CRM every year? And paying someone to do it?"
- **Buyer:** "Yeah."
- **Seller:** "How does that data entry clerk know it's a first-time buyer?"
- **Buyer:** "She cross-references with the database. If they're not in it, she checks a 'first-time buyer' box."
- **Seller:** "Has she ever forgotten to check that box?"
- **Buyer:** "Yes, all the time."
- **Seller:** "What if I told you I could create a system that automatically registered customers, automatically flagged first-time buyers, and automatically sent them a thank-you with a discount? Would that change your business?"
- **Buyer:** "Holy crap. You can do that?"

**Lesson:** The buyer started with "no problem". Five process questions later, the gap was undeniable.

### Dialogue 2 — Manny and the Tow Truck (the price-vs-outcome story)
- **Manny:** "I'm supposed to have dinner with the President. In 40 minutes. Can you take me?"
- **Driver:** "Yeah, I can. It'll cost a thousand dollars."
- **Manny:** "Are you kidding me? That's outrageous!"
- **Driver:** "I'm on my way to another call. Factor in lost income, time, the repair — that's what it costs."
- **Manny:** [pauses, looks at the tow truck] "How fast can you go in that thing?"

**Lesson:** Same product (a tow). Different gap. The future state defined the value, not the cost of the service.

### Dialogue 3 — Stacking Problems Out of a CS Discovery
- **Seller:** "How do you gather the data from each region into one place?"
- **Buyer:** "We can't. At least not consistently." *(Problem #1)*
- **Seller:** "How do you create consistency within the sales team? Get a bird's-eye view?"
- **Buyer:** "We can't do that, either." *(Problem #2)*
- **Seller:** "Are you concerned about hitting your $110M goal without a system that ensures all teams execute at the same level?"
- **Buyer:** "Yep." *(Problem #3)*
- **Seller:** "You said you have varying experience across the sales team. How does that affect quota attainment?"
- **Buyer:** "Sometimes we don't make it." *(Problem #4)*
- **Seller:** "How does it affect your ability to be predictable?"
- **Buyer:** "We're terribly unpredictable." *(Problem #5)*
- **Seller:** "If you're unpredictable, how do you plan?"
- **Buyer:** "It's really hard." *(Problem #6)*

### Dialogue 4 — Banishing Open-Ended Answers
- **Buyer:** "We're not growing fast enough."
- **Seller:** "Define that for me. What does 'fast enough' mean to you?"
- **Buyer:** "We're at 22%. We need to be hitting 25%."

### Dialogue 5 — Quantifying Impact
- **Seller:** "Share with me what's been the impact of not being able to regularly communicate with patrons."
- **Buyer:** "Sure. Our revenue is down by a lot."
- **Seller:** "What is 'a lot' to you?"
- **Buyer:** "Two years ago we were at $4M. This year we're barely going to clear $3.5M."

**Lesson:** The problem was lack of patron communication. The impact: $500K in lost revenue. Now there's a deal.

### Dialogue 6 — When a Prospect Goes Dark
> "I'm confused. You said you were tired of getting beat out for bigger, more complex projects, and you agreed that my product would improve your value proposition tenfold and get you to your financial goals. So I'm surprised I haven't heard from you. Has something changed? Were you able to solve your problem? Have you decided not to pursue bigger projects?"

### Dialogue 7 — Reframing a "Local Vendor" Objection
- **Buyer:** "I'd love to work with you, but I really wanted to support a local business."
- **Seller:** "Wait, I'm confused. I understand your interest in supporting local businesses, but you told me that the most important thing to you when hiring a cleaning company was that it was wholly committed to using environmentally friendly products. Our green cleaning products aren't add-ons; our whole business is built around them. Is there a local company that can make the same promise? Help me understand."

### Dialogue 8 — Theater "Member Seats" Objection
- **Buyer:** "Your system locks subscribers into the same seats. We need them to choose for every show. We're not sure we can move forward."
- **Seller:** "I'm confused. You told me that you're trying to increase new ticket sales by 50,000 per year by focusing on first-time ticket buyers, and that you need this software to launch a major capital campaign. You currently have 1,500 subscribers — that's only 5% of your total ticket sales. Why would you sacrifice everything you're trying to achieve for the sake of 1,500 people who are still going to get to pick their own seats, just not repeatedly?"

### Dialogue 9 — Price Objection: "Too Expensive"
- **Buyer:** "$80K is too expensive."
- **Seller:** "I'm confused. This product will increase lead conversion by 30%, reduce lead generation costs by 50%, and shorten the average sale by 30 days, all of which would lead your company to reach its $50M goal by 2020. Why do you find this to be too expensive?"
- **[Then shut up and listen.]**

### Dialogue 10 — Budget Objection (Cost of Inaction)
- **Buyer:** "It's not in our budget. We'll have to wait until next fiscal year."
- **Seller:** "I understand, but I'm confused: If you don't move forward till next year's budget, you'll lose $50K a month for the next six months. That's $300K. Are you prepared to lose that kind of money, plus forgo competing for high-end projects for six more months? How will that financial loss affect your ability to make your stated goals?"

### Dialogue 11 — Stalled Deal
- **Seller:** "Tom, I'm more than happy to push that meeting off another week, but I'm a little confused. You said that every month that goes by without a new system in place you're losing money, and yet you were hoping to increase your profits next year by 4%. It's going to get awfully hard for you to reach that goal if we keep going at this rate, isn't it? Is that OK?"

## 23. The Complete Gap-Selling Discovery Sequence

A reusable scaffold from intro to articulating the gap. Don't read these as scripts — adapt the substance.

### Stage 0 — Pre-call prep
- Build/refresh the **Problem Identification Chart** for this buyer's industry/segment.
- Identify the 3 most likely problems for this prospect.
- Draft 2 intrigue hooks (knowledge gaps you can drop in the first 5 minutes).

### Stage 1 — Open with intrigue, not introduction
Skip the "let me tell you about our company" preamble. Lead with a problem-centric opener:

> "Before we dive in, I want to share something I've been seeing across [industry]. [Specific knowledge gap or surprising data point.] I'd love to learn whether that's something you're experiencing — and if so, what it's costing you. Mind if I ask a few questions?"

### Stage 2 — Discover the Facts (probing + process questions)
Use command-statement starters: "Walk me through…", "Help me understand…", "Tell me about…"

Sample sequence (adapt to context):
1. "Walk me through how [the relevant function] is structured today."
2. "Tell me about the volumes — how many [things] do you handle in a year?"
3. "Help me understand the process. Step by step, how does [X] happen?"
4. "Who's involved? What roles touch this?"
5. "What systems and tools do you use today?"

### Stage 3 — Discover the Problems
As facts emerge, surface the cracks. Ask one at a time, stack them:
1. "How well is that working?"
2. "Where does it break?"
3. "What happens when [edge case]?"
4. "Has there ever been a time when [bad scenario]?"
5. "What do you wish were different about it?"

Don't stop at problem #1. Keep asking until they've named at least 3.

### Stage 4 — Discover the Impact (banish open-ended answers)
For each problem, force quantification across business + emotional dimensions:
1. "Share with me — what's been the impact of [problem]?"
2. *(Buyer gives vague answer.)* "Define that for me. What does [vague term] mean specifically?"
3. "How is that affecting [revenue / cost / time / customers / your team]?"
4. "What does that cost you per [day/month/quarter]?"
5. "How are people on your team feeling about it?"
6. "If this continues for another 6 months, what happens?"

The goal is the **OMG moment** — the buyer says it out loud themselves.

### Stage 5 — Discover the Root Cause
1. "Why do you think this problem is happening?"
2. "How do you think your current processes are contributing?"
3. "What have you tried? Why didn't it work?"
4. "If you had to bet on the single biggest reason this exists, what would you say?"

### Stage 6 — Validate
Before moving on, mirror back:
> "What I hear you saying is [problem A] is costing you [impact A], because of [root cause A]. Did I get that right?"

### Stage 7 — Discover the Future State
Quantify the destination as rigorously as you quantified the current state:
1. "How would you like this to work?"
2. "How much [revenue / time / leads / efficiency] would that represent?"
3. "By when do you want to be there?"
4. "What would solving this mean for your team / your role / the business?"
5. "What would it feel like to have this fixed?"

### Stage 8 — Find the Intrinsic Motivation
> "Tell me a bit about what's driving this change."

Don't accept surface answers. Dig:
- Why this number? Why this date? Why now and not last year?
- What happens to you / the team / the company if this doesn't get solved?
- Who else is watching this?

### Stage 9 — Articulate the Gap
Do the math out loud, with their numbers:

> "Let me make sure I have this right. You're at [CS metric]. You want to be at [FS metric]. That's a gap of [delta]. Every month you stay where you are, it's costing you [impact]. And the reason you want to close that gap by [date] is because [intrinsic motivation]. Did I get that right?"

### Stage 10 — Confirm the Four Qualifying Questions
Internally check (or ask aloud):
1. Is there a problem you can fix? ✅
2. Does the buyer agree there's a problem? ✅
3. Does the buyer want to fix it? ✅
4. Will the buyer go on the journey with you? ✅

If yes to all four → qualified opportunity. Schedule the demo (separately!) and move to decision criteria + buying process.

If no to any → either widen the gap with more discovery, or qualify out gracefully.

### Stage 11 — Hand off to the next yes
End every conversation with an explicit next yes — a small commitment from the buyer that moves the deal forward (intro to a stakeholder, demo on a specific date, share a doc, get specs).

> "Based on what we've talked about, the next thing I'd recommend is [X]. Does that work?"

## Appendix — One-page Cheat Sheet

- **Sale = Future State − Current State.** No gap, no sale.
- **Discovery is the close.** Talk-to-listen ratio < 50%.
- **5 elements of CS:** Facts, Problems, Impact, Root Cause, Emotional State.
- **4 problem types:** Physical, Technical, Business, Emotional impact.
- **PIC columns:** Problem | Impact | Root Cause. Build before any outreach.
- **Question types:** Probing, Process, Provoking, Validating.
- **Banish open-ended answers.** Every metric must be quantified.
- **No discovery, no demo.** No "if" in a demo. ≤6 features.
- **The four magic words:** "I'm confused. You said…"
- **You're not the customer's b*tch.** Standing strong > capitulating.
- **Price the inaction**, not the product.
- **Make the buyer say no.** Never say it for them.
- **The sale is always in the information.**
