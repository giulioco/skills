# Fanatical Prospecting — Jeb Blount (Extract)

## Core Thesis

Blount's central claim: The number one reason for failure in sales is an empty pipeline, and the root cause of an empty pipeline is the failure to prospect. Top producers ("Superstars") are not separated by talent, skill, or training — they are separated by being **fanatical prospectors** who take personal responsibility for creating their own opportunities.

> "The brutal fact is the number one reason for failure in sales is an empty pipe, and the root cause of an empty pipeline is the failure to prospect."

> "In sales you are owed nothing!"

> "Easy is the mother of mediocrity."

The three things you control in sales:
1. Your Actions
2. Your Reactions
3. Your Mindset

The Paradox of Basics: a truth so blatantly obvious it has become impossibly invisible. Prospecting is brutally simple — but simple is not easy.

The "easy button" doesn't exist. There is no silver bullet, no secret sauce, no one tool that fills the pipe. Anyone selling you "never cold call again" is selling a fantasy.

The mantra of the fanatical prospector: **"One more call."**

## The 30-Day Rule

> "The prospecting you do in this 30-day period will pay off for the next 90 days."

Implications:
- Miss a **day** → it bites you sometime in the next 90 days.
- Miss a **week** → you feel it in your commission check.
- Miss a **month** → tank the pipeline, fall into a slump, wake up 90 days later desperate, feeling like a loser, with no idea how you got there.

In short-cycle transactional sales, this becomes the "One-Week Rule" — same concept.

The 30-Day Rule is the reason slumps in March trace to missed prospecting in December. Salespeople don't connect cause to effect because the gap is 90 days back.

## The Universal Law of Need

> "The more you need something, the less likely it is that you will get it."

This law governs desperation. When the pipeline is empty and survival rests on one or two accounts, failure becomes nearly guaranteed. Why:

1. **Law of Attraction (downside)**: You stop focusing on what's required for success and start focusing on what happens if you fail. You attract failure.
2. **Desperation is sensed**: Tone of voice, words, body language broadcast neediness. Prospects repel weakness and gravitate toward confidence.
3. **Desperation makes you illogical**: Emotional decisions worsen the situation, deepen the hole.

> "When you reek of the foul stench of desperation, people don't want to do business with you."

Antidote: a full pipeline. Sandra (full pipe) gets gravy deals because she doesn't need them. Jerry (empty pipe) loses the few he has because he's desperate.

## The Law of Replacement

Most salespeople ride a roller coaster because of this law.

**The math**: If your closing rate is 10%, then closing one deal effectively removes **10 prospects** from your pipeline (the one you closed plus the nine that statistically would have been long shots in that batch). To stay flat, replace at a rate matching your closing ratio. To grow, exceed it.

> "You must constantly be pushing new opportunities into your pipeline so that you're replacing the opportunities that will naturally fall out."

## Anatomy of a Sales Slump

The death spiral:

1. You stop prospecting (30-Day Rule violated).
2. Pipeline stalls (Law of Replacement violated).
3. Dead pipe → no closes.
4. Confidence erodes.
5. Negative self-talk degrades enthusiasm. You feel like a loser.
6. Loser feeling saps energy → less prospecting.
7. You call dead prospects over and over, fooling yourself it's "prospecting."
8. Pipe gets worse.
9. You hope for silver bullets.
10. **Bam — Universal Law of Need slaps you.**
11. Sales days become "depressing black holes of misery."

**First Rule of Holes**: When you're in one, stop digging.
**First Rule of Sales Slumps**: When you're in one, **start prospecting.**

Recovery rule: ~30 days of dedicated daily activity gets you back on track.

> "The more you prospect, the luckier you get."

## The Numbers Game (Activity Math)

Sales is mathematical: what (quality) × how much (quantity) → output.

Track manually. Top performers track:
- Calls
- Contacts (decision-maker conversations)
- Emails sent / responded
- Appointments set
- Sales closed
- Social touches (LinkedIn etc.)
- Texts sent
- New records / data points added to database

**Efficiency** = activity volume per time block.
**Effectiveness** = ratio of activity to outcome.
**Performance = Efficiency + Effectiveness (E + E = P)**

Examples of imbalance:
- 100 dials/hour with no qualified appointments = efficient but not effective.
- 10 calls in an hour, one qualified appointment = effective but inefficient.

Goal: optimize the balance to maximize outcome.

> "You cannot be delusional and successful at the same time."

The "12 calls thought it was 50" trap: without manual tracking, two harsh rejections distort perception of activity. Manual tracking keeps you honest.

## The Three Ps Holding You Back

### Procrastination
You can't do a month of prospecting in one day. Procrastination is "the grave in which opportunity is buried." Every major failure is the cumulative impact of small disciplines skipped.

### Perfectionism
Perfectionism is correlated with fear of failure and is "the arch enemy of fanatical prospecting." It generates both procrastination and rejection avoidance disguised as preparation.

> "Anything worth doing is worth doing poorly." — Zig Ziglar (quoted)

> "Messy success is far better than perfect mediocrity."

Research is fine — but do it **before and after** Golden Hours, never during.

### Paralysis from Analysis
The "what if" binge: "What if they say no? What if they say this? What if…"

Different from genuine call reluctance (which means you're in the wrong job — quit). Analysis paralysis is fixable: just dial the next number.

### Disrupting the 3Ps
- One call, then the next, then the next.
- Sit next to them and dial too — show you don't get blown out of the water.
- "Just do it." A push breaks the cycle. The Coach Poss method: get hurled off the diving board, swim, discover you can.

## Time: The Great Equalizer

Every salesperson has 24 hours. Only a handful are available for selling — the **Golden Hours**.

### CEO Mindset
You are CEO of You, Inc. Buck stops with you. Resources scarce; time is the scarcest. CEOs find creative solutions to inevitable roadblocks; they don't blame, don't excuse.

### Protect the Golden Hours
Golden Hours = the prime selling hours when prospects are reachable. During these hours you should be doing only revenue-generating activities.

The non-prospecting trap:
- Demos, proposals, follow-ups, CRM entry, approvals all *feel* important.
- Busy work becomes the perfect excuse to avoid prospecting.
- Salespeople get paid to sell, period. If you're not interacting with qualified prospects during the Golden Hours, you're not doing your job.

### Choices for handling non-sales work
1. **Delude yourself** — keep treating busy work as sales work. Doesn't end well.
2. **Just say no** — set boundaries; people learn.
3. **Prioritize** — nobody hitting their number gets fired for missing admin. Plenty get fired for missing the number.
4. **Do non-sales work in Platinum Hours** (before/after the Golden Hours).
5. **Delegate** — leverage support staff, virtual assistants, hire help.

### Platinum Hours
The early-morning and late-afternoon windows for non-revenue work:
- Building prospecting lists, research, precall planning
- Proposals, presentations, contracts, approvals
- Social selling activities, email prospecting
- Email response, calendar/CRM management, reports

### Horstman's Corollary
Parkinson's Law: work expands to fill the time allotted.
**Horstman's Corollary** (the converse): work *contracts* to fit the time allowed. Give yourself 30 minutes for prospecting and you'll do more than in eight unfocused hours.

### Time Blocking (Power Hours)
- Block prospecting into 1–2-hour chunks (or three "Power Hours" spread across the day).
- Treat the block like a customer appointment — sacred. Hang a "Do Not Disturb" sign.
- Stay off email, social, mobile, CRM during the block.
- Take notes during; log them after.
- Anybody can stay focused for an hour.

> "If you invest just an hour a day to make 25 to 50 teleprospecting calls and another hour for e-mail and social prospecting, I can absolutely and unequivocally guarantee that in less than 60 days, your pipeline will be packed."

### Concentrate Your Power
You don't multitask — you suck at it. Brain cycles between tasks; performance degrades.
- One task at a time inside the block.
- Phone in a drawer. Email closed. Inbound disabled.
- Research and CRM logging happen in dedicated separate blocks.

### Beware of the Ding
The two biggest derailers are email and mobile devices. Average person checks their phone every seven minutes. One ding → 7+ minutes lost plus the cost of getting back to where you were.

### Email First Thing = Death
> "Once you open yourself to the demands of the outside world, it is very difficult to bring your full attention and focus to the most important tasks." — Anthony Iannarino

Rule one: don't check email first thing. There's almost never good news in there at 9 AM. Block the first 1–2 hours of the day for telephone prospecting.

### Know Your Worth
Formula:

```
Annual Income Goal ÷ (Working Weeks × Golden Hours per Week) = Hourly Worth
```

Use it as a gauge for whether an activity is worth your time. Doing $10/hour data entry during $50/hour Golden Hours costs you real money. The **Law of Triviality** — wasting time on unimportant tasks while mission-critical work is ignored — is the trap.

## The Four Objectives of Prospecting

Every prospecting touch must have one of four primary objectives:

| Objective | When It's Primary |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Set an appointment** | Complex, high-risk, high-cost B2B sales. Long cycles. |
| **Gather information / qualify** | Database has gaps; product has a defined buying window (contract/budget); new territory. |
| **Close the sale** | Transactional, low-risk, low-cost product. Especially in inside sales or in-person. |
| **Build familiarity** | Cold conquest accounts; long-cycle SPCs; high-value prospects not yet in window. |

> "Don't swing at nothin' ugly." — Coach Sandro (qualifying maxim)

An appointment isn't an appointment unless it's on **both** calendars at a specific time/date. "Just stop by" and "Call me anytime" are not appointments — they are brush-offs.

### Touches needed to engage (rough averages)
| Prospect type | Touches |
|---|---|
| Inactive customer | 1–3 |
| In-window prospect, familiar with you | 1–5 |
| High-familiarity, not in window | 3–10 |
| Warm inbound lead | 5–12 |
| Some familiarity, window-dependent | 5–20 |
| **Cold prospect, no familiarity** | **20–50** |

## The Prospecting Pyramid

Most salespeople treat their database as a square — every prospect equal. Top performers see it as a pyramid and work top-down each morning.

```
        Hot/in-window/contract-expiring (TOP — call first)
       Hot inbound leads & referrals
      Conquest accounts (10/25/50/100)
     Identified DMs + nurture campaigns
    Partial info — identify buying window
   Bottom — name only, unverified data
```

Daily rule: start prospecting blocks at the top of the pyramid (highest probability) while you're fresh and motivated. Move down to qualifying / database-cleanup activities later in the block.

### List Building Filters
Combine these to construct prospecting lists:
- Objective (appointment / info / close / familiarity)
- Channel (phone / email / social / text / in-person / networking)
- Qualification level (highest at top)
- Potential (largest opportunity at top)
- Probability of yes (highest at top)
- Territory plan, postal code, city, day-of-week
- Inbound leads, conquest list, decision-maker role
- Industry vertical, seasonal customers, inactive customers, recent trade-show leads

> "When you build powerful lists, you get powerful results."

## Own Your Database (CRM)

> "The most expensive thing you can do in sales is spend your time with the wrong prospect."

Your CRM is the single most important tool in your sales arsenal because it:
- Remembers things for you and reminds you when they matter
- Lets you sort, segment, filter into targeted lists
- Systematically moves prospects up the pyramid

**Trash can vs. gold mine**: most reps treat their database like a trash can — no notes, no logged calls, stale records. Fanatical prospectors put every detail of every interaction in the CRM. No procrastination, no shortcuts.

## The Law of Familiarity

> "The more familiar a prospect is with you, your brand, and/or your company, the more likely they will be willing to accept and return your calls, reply to your e-mails, accept a social media connection request, respond to a text, and engage when you are prospecting in person."

The **Familiarity Threshold** (Sean Burke): once crossed, you can text, social-DM, and freely contact a prospect without being seen as intrusive.

### The Five Levers of Familiarity
1. **Persistent and consistent prospecting** — every voicemail, email, drop-in, social touch builds it.
2. **Referrals and introductions** — three types:
   - **Customer referrals** — from happy clients. The two-step secret: (a) deliver a legendary experience, (b) **ask**. Sample ask: "Patricia, thank you again for your business. I'm working hard to add more customers like you. Would you be able to introduce me to other people in your network who might want to use our product?"
   - **Personal referrals** — from friends, family, acquaintances. Educate them on your ICP, then keep reminding them.
   - **Professional referrals** — from non-competing salespeople and adjacent professionals.
3. **Networking** — chambers, civic groups, trade shows. Don't sell — connect. Send handwritten thank-you notes; follow up with a text and LinkedIn invite within 24 hours.
4. **Company / brand familiarity** — leverage if you have it; if you're at a startup, contribute to brand-building (blogs, social, trade shows).
5. **Personal branding** — secret weapon: **speak in public regularly.** Chamber of commerce, Rotary, trade groups always need speakers. Volunteer.

## Social Selling

Social selling is **not** selling. Pitching on LinkedIn / Twitter / Facebook gets you blocked. The social channel is for **building familiarity, lead nurturing, research, nuanced inbound, and trigger-event awareness**.

### Five Objectives of Social Prospecting (Outcomes)
1. Personal branding & familiarity
2. Inbound prospecting via education and insights
3. Trigger-event and buying-cycle awareness
4. Research and information gathering
5. Outbound prospecting via direct engagement

### The Five Cs of Social Prospecting (Process)
| C | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Connecting** | Send a connection request to every new prospect, customer, networking contact. Direct, reciprocal, or passive. |
| **Content Creation** | Original articles, video, podcasts, infographics, white papers — positions you as expert. |
| **Content Curation** | Aggregate and share others' relevant content. Three pillars: awareness, intent, tools. |
| **Conversion** | Plan with intent — generate inbound leads and sales conversations. |
| **Consistency** | 30–60 min/day, every day, in Platinum Hours. Compound effect over time. |

### Profile Hygiene Checklist
- Professional headshot (same on every network); genuine smile; neutral background; no cats/beer/sunglasses.
- Cover image (HubSpot: "having a social profile without a cover photo is like a brick-and-mortar without a sign").
- Compelling first-person summary/bio answering: who you are, values, what you do best, why customers trust you.
- **Contact info exposed** (phone, email, website). Privacy is the enemy of sales.
- Cross-link all profiles. Custom URLs.
- Complete history — no gaps.
- Review and update quarterly.

### Tool Categories
1. **Curation** (Feedly, Pocket, Sprout)
2. **Creation** (LinkedIn Pulse, SlideShare, Canva, YouTube)
3. **Distribution** (HootSuite, Buffer, HubSpot)
4. **Engagement** (Bit.ly, TweetDeck, native analytics)
5. **Intelligence** (Google Alerts, native search)

### The Trap
Social selling is **not a panacea**. Phone and email contact rates dwarf social conversion. Social amplifies outbound; it doesn't replace it.

> "Social selling will not solve your pipeline woes."

## Message Matters

> "Prospects meet with you for their reasons, not yours."

You have ~10 seconds to get attention. Messages must be:
- **Quick** — get to the point
- **Direct** — transparent about what you want
- **Relevant** — bridges to *their* problem in *their* language

### What to avoid
- Feature dumps and product brochures
- "Number one this / biggest of that"
- "I'd love to have a few minutes of your time to tell you about my company"
- Long marketing-written scripts
- Ums, uhs, and asking "Is this a good time?" / "How are you doing?"

### The Power of Because (Ellen Langer's Copy Machine Study)
- Asking to cut without reason: 60% yes
- "...because I'm in a hurry": 94% yes
- "...because I need to make copies" (nonsense): 93% yes

**The word "because" is more important than the reason itself.** Just give a reason — any reason — when you ask.

### Bridging — Targeted vs. Strategic
- **Targeted bridges** — common to a class of prospects. Use when you're calling a high volume of similar accounts. Educated guess about their pain.
- **Strategic bridges** — unique to one high-value prospect. Research-driven. Use for C-level, conquest, enterprise.

### The Real Secret
**People decide on emotion first and justify with logic.** Lead with emotional language: stress, anxiety, frustration, fear, peace of mind, options, security.

Three kinds of value to deliver:
1. **Emotional value** — relate to their feelings
2. **Insight value** — give info that creates curiosity / competitive edge
3. **Tangible value** — data, case studies, ROI numbers

The single question to answer before calling: *"What would cause this prospect to say 'so what' to my message?"*

### Assumptive vs. Passive Asks

| Weak / Nonassumptive | Assumptive / Confident |
|---|---|
| "Is this a good time?" | "The reason I'm calling is…" |
| "I was wondering if…" | "Tell me who—how—when—where—what…" |
| "I have the whole day open." | "I'm super busy bringing on new clients, but I do have a slot at 11:00 AM." |
| "What do you think?" | "Why don't we go ahead and get that set up?" |
| "What's the best time for you?" | "I'll be near your office Monday. I'll pick you up for lunch." |
| "Would this be a good time for you?" | "How about we meet at 2:00 PM?" |

Tracked data: **assertive asks → ~70% yes. Nonassertive asks → ~30%.** Assumptive ask + because = even higher.

### The Three Steps to Asking
1. Ask with confidence and assume you'll get it.
2. **Shut up.**
3. Be ready for RBOs.

### The Rule of Thirds (after asking)
- **1/3 yes** — ask, get yes, move on.
- **1/3 no** — quick rejection, move on.
- **1/3 maybe** — RBOs surface here. This is where prospecting is won or lost.

## Telephone Prospecting Excellence

> "The phone is your most powerful sales tool. Period, end of story."

The phone myth ("nobody answers anymore") is wrong. Real-world contact rates: 15–80% depending on industry; 25–40% in B2B services. Trends are *up* ~5 points over the past decade because (1) phones are mobile-anchored, (2) competitors switched to email, (3) prospects are sick of automated email crap.

### The Five-Step Simple Telephone Prospecting Framework

1. **Get their attention by using their name.** "Hi, Julie."
2. **Identify yourself.** "My name is Jeb Blount and I'm with Sales Gravy."
3. **Tell them why you're calling.** "The reason I'm calling is to set up an appointment with you."
4. **Bridge — give them a because.** "I just read an article that said your company is going to add 200 new sales positions over the next year. Several companies in your industry are using Sales Gravy exclusively for sourcing sales candidates and they're very happy with the results."
5. **Ask for what you want, and shut up.** "I thought the best place to start is to schedule a short meeting to learn about your sales recruiting challenges and goals. How about Wednesday afternoon around 3:00 PM?"

**No pauses between steps.** Pauses surrender control of the call.

**Do not say "How are you doing?"** — it triggers the prospect's "salesperson alert" and an immediate brush-off.

### Sample Phone Openers (Verbatim)
- Information gathering:
  > "Hi, Ian, this is Jeb Blount with Acme Restaurant Supply. The reason I'm calling is I read in the paper that you are building a restaurant over on the 44 bypass and I want to learn more about your process for purchasing kitchen equipment. … Can you tell me how you make those decisions and when the selection process will begin?"

- Move into a sales conversation:
  > "Hi, Corrina, this is Jeb Blount from AcmeSoft. The reason I'm calling is you downloaded our white paper on creating more effective landing pages, and I'm interested to learn what triggered your interest. I work with a number of marketing executives who've been struggling to bring in enough quality leads, and I've got a few best practices I'll be happy to share with you. Can you tell me more about your situation?"

### Five-Step Voicemail Framework (Doubles Callback Rate)

1. **Identify yourself** (name + company).
2. **Say your phone number twice — slowly.** Up front, before anything else. They might delete the rest, but they have your number.
3. **Tell them the reason for your call.** "The reason for my call is…" / "The purpose of my call is…"
4. **Give them a reason to call you back** (curiosity, value, insight, special pricing).
5. **Repeat your name and say the number twice again** at the end.

Cap voicemails at **30 seconds.**

Sample voicemail (verbatim):
> "Hi, Rick, this is Jeb Blount from Sales Gravy. My phone number is 1-888-360-2249, that's 1-888-360-2249. The reason I'm calling is you downloaded our white paper on cold calling and I want to learn more about your situation and what triggered you to seek out this information. I also have some additional resources on voice mail messages and phone prospecting I thought you might be curious to learn about. Let's get together this week. Give me a call back at 1-888-360-2249, that's 1-888-360-2249."

When to leave a voicemail: only when it matters (conquest, in-window, high-value). Don't waste 20–30 sec/dial × 100 dials/day on cold low-value prospects.

### Timing Calls = Cop-Out
Salespeople use timing as an excuse to avoid calling. Studies show best vs. worst day differences are tiny (~1.3%). The investor analogy: dollar-cost-averaging beats market-timing every time.

> "Eat the frog." Block your first two hours every day for telephone activity. The longer you stare at the frog, the worse it gets.

## Turning Around RBOs (Reflex Responses, Brush-offs, Objections)

> "Everybody has a plan until they get punched in the face." — Mike Tyson

### Three Categories
| Type | Example | What it really means |
|---|---|---|
| **Reflex Response** | "We're not interested." "We're happy." "I'm busy." | Automatic buyer script. Not a conscious objection. |
| **Brush-off** | "Just send me some information." "Call me later." "Get back to me in a month." | Polite avoidance of conflict. They want you to go away. |
| **Objection** | "We just signed a new contract with your competitor." "Budget is locked." | Logical, often truthful pushback. Has a real because. |

### Disrupt vs. Defeat
Don't try to "overcome objections" — you can't argue someone into agreement. Instead, **disrupt their expectations.** They expect a fight; give them an unexpected response that pulls them toward you.

### The Three-Step Turnaround Framework

**Anchor → Disrupt → Ask**

1. **Anchor** — a "ledge" statement. Buys you a millisecond for your logical brain to override your amygdala. Often agrees with the prospect.
2. **Disrupt** — a statement or question that breaks their expected pattern.
3. **Ask** — assumptive, specific request. Don't pause.

**Avoid the phrase "I understand."** Salespeople use it as filler; it telegraphs that you weren't listening.

### Verbatim Turnaround Scripts

**Prospect: "Look, Jeb, I'm busy."**
> Anchor: "Nancy, that's exactly why I called."
> Disrupt: "I figured you would be, so I want to find a time that's more convenient for you."
> Ask: "How about we get together next Wednesday at 3:00 PM instead?"

**Prospect: "We're not interested."**
> Anchor: "You know, that's what a lot of my current clients said the first time I called."
> Disrupt: "Most people say they aren't interested before they see how much I can save them. I don't know if my service will be a good fit for you, but doesn't it make sense for us to at least get together for a short meeting to find out?"
> Ask: "How about Friday at 2:00 PM?"

**Prospect: "We're really happy with our current provider."**
> Anchor: "That's fantastic!"
> Disrupt: "Anytime you're getting great rates and great service, you should never think about changing. All I want to do is come by and get to know you a little better. Even if it doesn't make sense to do business with me at the moment, I can give you a competitive quote that will help you keep those other guys honest."
> Ask: "How about I come by on Tuesday at 11:30 AM?"

**Prospect: "Just send me some information."**
> "Tell me specifically what you're looking for." (Calls the bluff and forces engagement.)

**Prospect: "I'm not interested."**
> "That makes sense. Most people aren't." (Disrupts pattern by agreeing.)

### When the Horse Is Dead, Dismount
After two RBOs and no progress, move on graciously. Don't fight. Come back another day. The card on Blount's desk for 25 years just says: **NEXT.**

> "Beating a dead horse is self-destructive. Dead horses don't trot, they rot."

## The Secret Lives of Gatekeepers

Gatekeepers are people doing their job — protecting their boss's time. There are no secret tricks; there are strategies.

### Seven Keys
1. **Be likable** — polite, cheerful, respectful.
2. **Use "please" twice.** ("Would you please connect me to Mike Brooks, please?")
3. **Be transparent** — full name, full company, real reason.
4. **Connect** — ask about them. Build relationships with the gatekeepers you'll see again.
5. **Hold the cheese** — no tricks. Tricks land you on the never-list.
6. **Ask for help** — humor + humility opens doors.
7. **Change the game** — call early/late (boss is in before gatekeeper), use LinkedIn InMail, go in person, send handwritten notes.

### The Three Hacks
- **Calling-Other-Extensions Hack** — dial random extensions; friendly people give you hints; loop back to reception with the partial info.
- **Salespeople-Help-Salespeople Hack** — press 1 for sales, ask the rep who answers. Salespeople have empathy and know the org chart.
- **Go-Around-Back Hack** — for in-person, find loading docks, smoke breaks, parking lots. Don't try this where security is real.

> "In sales, persistence always wins. Always."

## In-Person Prospecting (IPP)

The least efficient channel — but indispensable for outside reps in local territories with transactional or semi-complex products.

### Hub-and-Spoke System (5 steps)
1. Plan IPPs around preset appointments (set on the phone).
2. Use CRM to find prospects within a tight zip code radius.
3. Plot 3–5 prospects around each appointment on a map.
4. Build the most efficient driving route.
5. Hit the IPPs before/after each preset appointment until you reach your daily goal.

**T-Calls**: at every preset appointment, look right, left, behind. Walk into those doors too.

### IPP Objectives
- Qualifying (people share more in person)
- Setting future appointments
- Live sales conversations
- Building familiarity (face + name)
- Maximizing the sales day
- Learning the territory

### Five Steps to Plan an IPP
1. **Research** — DMs, history, website, recent press, CRM notes.
2. **Personalize** — relevant questions, recent compliments, leverage neighbor relationships ("We've been doing business with Billy next door for five years and he loves our service.")
3. **Develop an objective** for each call.
4. **Be prepared to close** — bring sales materials, contracts, order forms. You may only get one shot.
5. **Log everything in the CRM** the same day.

### The Five-Step IPP Call Process
1. **Approach with confidence.** Walk in like you own the place. Plan questions in advance.
2. **Identify yourself and say why you're there.** Verbatim opener templates:
   - "Hi, my name is Jeb Blount, I'm with XYZ Company. The reason I'm here is ABC Company next door is one of my customers and they said I should stop in and introduce myself to your owner, Mary."
   - "Hi, my name is Jeb Blount, I'm with XYZ Company. The reason I stopped by is I provide my service to several of the businesses in this industrial park and I wanted to learn more about your company and situation to see whether or not working with you might be a good fit."
3. **Gather information** — open-ended questions, listen, don't pitch.
4. **Ask for what you want.**
5. **Turn around objections.**

### Put Your Sales Goggles On
Always-on mode. Notice trucks with logos. Stop and chat with delivery drivers — they know decision makers. Talk to people in uniformed shirts. Talk to whoever is next to you in line, on planes, in waiting rooms. Photograph signs of new businesses. Hand out cards constantly.

> "Awareness without action is useless."

## Email Prospecting

Email gets 10–20× the engagement of social. The Three Cardinal Rules:

### Rule 1: It Must Get Delivered
- **Don't send bulk email** from your personal account. One-to-one, period.
- **Avoid attached images** (malware association → spam folder).
- **Avoid hyperlinks**; if you must include one — use the full transparent URL, no shorteners, max one link total (counting your signature).
- **Avoid attachments.**
- **Skip spammy words**: ALL CAPS subject lines, exclamation points, "free," "guaranteed," "$," "cash," "buy now," "100% guaranteed," "amazing," "don't delete."
- **Don't send to many contacts at the same company at once** — drip them.
- **Don't over-send** to the same person; getting marked spam can blacklist you across an enterprise.
- **Scrub bounces.** Update the CRM; find the new email.
- **Be careful with sensitive industries** (finance, defense, healthcare) — use plain text only.

### Rule 2: It Must Get Opened
**Familiarity drives opens.** Layer channels: voicemail → LinkedIn engagement → email triples your open rate.

**Subject Line Rules:**
- **3–6 words; 40–50 characters max.** Mobile gets 50%+ of opens — long subject lines die there.
- **No questions.** Statement and list-based subject lines outperform.
- **Make it about them, not you.**

**Examples that work:**
- `3 Reasons Why ABC Chose Us`
- `Jeb Blount Said We Should Talk`
- `Biggest Fail in Industrial Pumps`
- `Loved Your Book!`
- `Keep Those Kegs Rolling`
- `COO — The Toughest Job in the Bank`

**Examples that fail:**
- `Cloud Based Software` — about you
- Anything starting with `Hi, I was browsing LinkedIn…`

### Rule 3: It Must Convert
Conversion = the prospect takes the action you want (appointment, info, intro, download, sales conversation).

### AMMO Planning Framework
- **A**udience — who, role, style, familiarity
- **M**ethod — single email vs. SPC, short vs. detailed, hard vs. soft
- **M**essage — what gets their attention; speak their language
- **O**utcome — define the action you want before writing

### The Four Elements of an Effective Email
1. **Hook** — subject + first sentence get them to keep reading. (Kendra Lee's "glimpse factor.")
2. **Relate** — demonstrate you get them and their problem.
3. **Bridge** — connect their problem to how you can help. Answer WIIFM.
4. **Ask** — clear, specific, easy-to-act-on request. Offer a day/time.

### Sample Email (verbatim, COO of a bank)

> **Subject: COO — The Toughest Job in the Bank**
>
> Lawrence,
>
> Ernst & Young recently reported that the COO has the toughest role in the C-suite. The COOs I work with tell me that the increasing complexity of the banking environment has made their job harder and more stressful than ever.
>
> My team and I help COOs like you reduce complexity and stress with strategies to optimize growth and profit, mitigate credit risk, allocate resources effectively, and minimize regulatory surprises.
>
> While I don't know if we are a good fit for your bank, why don't we schedule a short call to help me learn more about your unique challenges? From there we can decide if it makes sense to set up a deeper conversation.
>
> How about next Thursday at 3:00 PM?
>
> Dave Adair
> Senior Account Executive
> JunoSystems

Why it works:
- Subject: about him (COO + bank), not about the seller.
- Hook: credible source (E&Y) + emotional ("toughest job").
- Relate: doesn't claim to be a COO, but uses "the COOs I work with…"
- Bridge: speaks COO language (growth, profit, credit risk, resources, regulatory).
- Ask: disrupts ("I don't know if we're a good fit"), pulls them in ("learn"), implies short, offers a specific time.

**Don't say "Hi" / "Hello" / "Dear"** before the prospect's name. Only salespeople do that.

### Best Time to Send
For B2B: morning to mid-morning when prospects are processing email. Test with Yesware / Tellwise / Tout / Signals.

### Always Pause Before "Send"
Read it once. Read it twice. Walk away 10 minutes. Read again. Print critical emails. Typos kill credibility.

## Text Message Prospecting

Text is the **third rail** of prospecting — personal, intrusive, but extremely powerful when familiarity is established.

### Familiarity Is Everything
- A standalone text → ~4.8% conversion.
- Same text after a phone contact → **+112.6%** (Lead360 study).
- Even higher when text follows email + social + in-person interactions.

### Four Use Cases
1. **Anchor conversations after networking events.**
   - During the chat: "Sounds good. I'll text you and we can get together."
   - After: send LinkedIn invite same day.
   - Within 24 hours: text thanking them + ask for the meeting.
   - No reply: try one more time the next day.
   - Still nothing: shift to phone/email. Don't keep texting.
   - Bonus: handwritten note within a week.
2. **Following trigger events.** Send a relevant article or resource. Soft approach. Leverages the urgency texts inherently carry.
3. **Nurturing prospects.** Quarterly calls + monthly emails + occasional texts with relevant articles, white papers, congratulations on promotions.
4. **Engagement opportunities.** Short texts to congratulate on a promotion, news mention, award, article. Make them feel important. No solicitation.

### Seven Rules for Text Prospecting Messages
1. **Identify yourself** (name + company up top — they don't have you saved).
2. **Message matters** — full sentences; no risk of misreading tone.
3. **Be direct, brief** — 1–4 sentences, under 250 characters.
4. **Avoid abbreviations** — no LOL/OMG/WTF; no slang; no emoticons.
5. **Use transparent links** — never shortened URLs.
6. **Pause and read it again before send.**
7. **Know your numbers** — track sends, response rate, conversion.

**DO NOT TEXT WHILE DRIVING.**

## Mental Toughness

> "When the going gets tough, the mentally tough keep going."

Mental toughness (a.k.a. **grit**) matters more than talent, experience, education, or technique.

### Loehr's 7 Dimensions of Mental Toughness
1. Self-confidence
2. Attention control
3. Minimizing negative energy
4. Increasing positive energy
5. Maintaining motivation levels
6. Attitude control
7. Visual and imagery control

### Croner & Abraham's 3 Sales Dimensions
- **Optimism** — mother of perseverance.
- **Competitiveness** — mother of persistence.
- **Need for achievement** — mother of self-motivation. ("Intense, prolonged and repeated efforts to accomplish something difficult." — Henry Murray)

### The Four Pillars of Mental Toughness in Sales

#### 1. Desire
Three questions:
1. What do you want?
2. How do you plan to get it?
3. How bad do you want it?

Write goals down — ink on paper. "If you don't have a plan, you will become a part of someone else's plan."

#### 2. Mental Resilience (Sharpen the Saw)
Sometimes you need to slow down to speed up. Invest in yourself. Outlearn = Outearn.

**The 15-Minutes-a-Day Reading Math:**
- 250 working days × 15 min/day = 62.5 hours/year of reading
- Average business book = ~3 hours
- = **~21 books per year**

Use drive time for audiobooks/podcasts ("Automobile University" — Zig Ziglar).

#### 3. Physical Resilience
- **Exercise** — 30 min/day, every day. Stand while making prospecting calls. 10-min chunks count.
- **Sleep** — 7–9 hours. Deprivation kills cognitive ability and discipline.
- **Eat healthy** — fuel matters. Always eat breakfast.

#### 4. Feed Your Attitude
Two beliefs of positive people:
1. **Expect to win.**
2. **Believe everything happens for a reason.**

Five attitude correctives:
- **Change the company you keep.**
- **Change your self-talk.** ("You cannot afford the luxury of a negative thought.")
- **Change your input** — what you read/watch/listen to.
- **Change your focus** — embrace the gift of failure; redirect that energy.
- **Be grateful.**

### When You're On Top, Attack Yourself
> "When you're in second place, attack the leader. When you're in first place, attack yourself."

There is no time for complacency. Real winners pick apart their own brilliant performances and find the small adjustments. The 30-Day Rule will catch up with anyone who rests.

## Prospecting Channel Cheat Sheet

| Channel | Strength | Weakness | Primary Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Phone** | Highest contact rate (15–80%); fastest path to yes/no/maybe | Most rejection; most uncomfortable | Setting appointments, qualifying, closing transactional |
| **Email** | Scalable; extends prospecting window; trackable | Easily ignored; spam filters; subject-line dependent | Bridge between channels; nurturing; SPCs |

| **Text** | High response when familiar; urgency built-in | Personal/intrusive if cold | Post-networking, post-trigger, nurturing existing relationships |
| **In-person** | Highest emotional bandwidth; uses all five senses | Slowest; expensive (windshield time) | T-calls around set appointments; territory learning |

| **Referrals** | Highest conversion of any channel | Requires asking — and most reps don't | Always-on after every customer interaction |

## The Combo Prospecting Stack (Multi-Channel Sequencing)

Layering channels amplifies familiarity exponentially. Example sequence for a high-value prospect:

1. **LinkedIn**: connect; like / comment on a recent post.
2. **Phone**: call; if no answer, leave a 30-second 5-step voicemail.
3. **Email**: send a 4-element email referencing the voicemail.
4. **Wait 3–5 days.**
5. **Phone**: call again; new bridge.
6. **Social**: share or comment on something they posted.
7. **Email**: short, different angle, different subject line.
8. **Text** (only if familiarity is now established): send a relevant article.
9. **In-person / handwritten note** for top conquest accounts.

The compounding effect: each touch raises the next channel's response rate. Cold prospects need 20–50 touches to engage; combo sequencing concentrates those touches into weeks instead of months.
