# Never Split the Difference — Chris Voss

Tactical extract for a general-purpose sales coaching skill. Source: Chris Voss with Tahl Raz, *Never Split the Difference: Negotiating As If Your Life Depended On It* (HarperCollins, 2016). Voss is the FBI's former lead international kidnapping negotiator, founder of The Black Swan Group.

---

## 1. Core Thesis

**Tactical empathy and emotional intelligence beat rational bargaining.** Humans are not rational actors. We are "irrational, emotional beasts" (Kahneman). Negotiation is not a battle of arguments — it is a process of discovery, conducted via active listening on the System 1 (emotional) layer, not the System 2 (rational) layer.

Key implications:
- Emotions aren't obstacles to negotiation — they are the means.
- Negotiation is "communication with results."
- Two functions of negotiation: (1) information gathering, (2) behavior influencing.
- "Getting to Yes"-style win-win problem solving fails when the other side is emotional, irrational, or operates win-lose. Real life is closer to the FBI hostage room than the Harvard classroom.
- The goal is not to be right, but to have the right mindset.
- Maxim: **"He who has learned to disagree without being disagreeable has discovered the most valuable secret of negotiation."** (Robert Estabrook, quoted by Voss.)
- **No deal is better than a bad deal.** Compromise — splitting the difference — is wearing one black shoe and one brown shoe.

## 2. Tactical Empathy

**Definition:** Understanding the feelings and mindset of another in the moment AND hearing what is behind those feelings, so you increase your influence in all the moments that follow.

Distinct from sympathy (agreeing with values) and pity. Empathy = recognizing the other's perspective and vocalizing that recognition. You don't have to agree to acknowledge.

**Why it works:**
- People want to be understood and accepted. Listening is the cheapest, most effective concession a negotiator can make.
- When people feel listened to, they listen to themselves more carefully, become less defensive, more open to other points of view.
- Neural resonance: when you closely observe face/gesture/voice, your brain aligns with theirs. Good listeners can predict what speakers will say next.
- It's listening as a martial art — "listener's judo." Active, not passive.

**How to do it:**
- Make the other person and what they have to say your sole focus.
- Quiet the voice in your own head.
- Imagine yourself in their position; visualize their context with detail.
- Identify their underlying emotions, not just stated positions.
- Talk less. Listen more. Mouth shut, eyes/ears open.
- Recognize "words, music, and dance" — the verbal, paraverbal, and nonverbal signals together.

The FBI's Behavioral Change Stairway Model (BCSM) sequence: **Active listening → Empathy → Rapport → Influence → Behavioral change.** You cannot skip steps.

## 3. Mirroring (Isopraxism)

**Definition:** Repeat back the last 1–3 critical words your counterpart just said. Sometimes called the "magic three."

It triggers the unconscious imitation instinct — "We're alike. Trust me." Birds of a feather. We fear what's different and are drawn to what's similar.

**The four-step pit-bull-defusal sequence:**
1. Use the late-night FM DJ voice.
2. Start with "I'm sorry…"
3. Mirror (last 1–3 words).
4. Silence — at least four seconds for the mirror to do its work.
5. Repeat.

**When and why it works:**
- Buys time without offering content.
- Encourages your counterpart to elaborate and reword (they will never repeat exactly the same way).
- Less aggressive than "What do you mean by that?" which incites defensiveness.
- Especially useful when tone/body language don't match the words.
- Particularly effective on **Assertive** counterparts.

**Example:** Boss: "Let's make two copies of all the paperwork." You: "I'm sorry, two copies?"

**Examples from the book:**
- Hostage Chris Watts: "You guys chased my driver away…" Voss: "We chased your driver away?" → vomiting of information about an accomplice they didn't know existed.
- Wendy the airline gate agent: "There've been a lot of irate consumers." Ryan: "Booked solid?" → upgrade.
- Restaurant tipping study (Wiseman): waiters who mirrored orders got 70% larger tips than those using positive reinforcement.

**Trap:** After you mirror, **shut up**. Voss blew his own mirror by following up with a question. Let silence do its work.

## 4. Labeling

**Definition:** Verbally identifying your counterpart's emotion. Validates by acknowledging.

Why it works: when emotions are named, brain activity moves from amygdala (fear) to areas that govern rational thinking. Labeling drains raw intensity from negative emotions and reinforces positive ones (Lieberman fMRI study).

**The three opening phrases:**
- "It seems like…"
- "It sounds like…"
- "It looks like…"

**Why never "I":** "I" puts the other side's guard up. It signals your interest in yourself, and makes you take responsibility for the words. Neutral statements of understanding invite responsiveness.

**Forming labels:**
1. Detect the emotion. Watch for changes in words/tone/body language ("words, music, and dance") in response to your statements.
2. Label it aloud as a neutral statement (downward inflection) or question (upward inflection).
3. **Be silent.** Let it sit. Do not expand.

**Two layers of emotion to address:**
- **Presenting** behavior (visible).
- **Underlying** feeling (motivating).
Label the underlying. Cranky grandfather is not cranky — he's lonely.

**Tactical labels:**
- Negative labels diffuse the emotion (especially fear).
- Positive labels reinforce it.
- "Look, I'm an asshole" disarms when you've messed up.
- "Bless me, Father, for I have sinned" — Voss's line to a furious senior FBI official whose protocol he'd violated.
- Mislabeling on purpose to provoke a corrective "No": "It seems like you really want to leave your job" (when they don't).

**Fill-in-the-blank labels for prep:**
- It seems like _____ is valuable to you.
- It seems like you don't like _____.
- It seems like you value _____.
- It seems like _____ makes it easier.
- It seems like you're reluctant to _____.
- It seems like _____ is important.
- It seems you feel like my company is in a unique position to _____.
- It seems like you are worried that _____.

If they push back: "I didn't say that *was* what it was. I just said it seems like that."

## 5. The Accusation Audit

**Definition:** List every negative thing your counterpart could possibly say or accuse you of, then say them yourself first, before they can. Lawyers call it "taking the sting out."

**Why it disarms:**
- Denying the negative gives it credence ("I don't want to seem harsh, but…").
- Naming the negative bathes it in sunlight, draining its power.
- Once spoken, accusations sound exaggerated and the other side instinctively says, "No, no, we don't think that."
- Brings you to a "safe zone of empathy" — every human wants to be understood.

**How to run one:**
1. List every accusation, no matter how unfair or ridiculous.
2. Distill to 3–5 sharpest possible labels.
3. Role-play. Plan pacing and pauses. **It is theater.**
4. Open the conversation by stating them yourself.

**Example phrases:**
- "You're going to think I can't budget or plan…"
- "You're going to think I'm a lousy businessman."
- "You may feel like we have treated you unfairly."
- "It sounds like you think we are the big, bad prime contractor trying to push out the small business."

**Anna's $1M contract example:** Before re-negotiating with subcontractor ABC, Anna and Mark listed accusations ("You're the typical prime contractor trying to force out the small guy"; "You promised us this work and reneged"). Opening the meeting with these caused ABC to deny them ("No, no, we don't think that"), defusing months of resentment.

**Voss's first-day-of-class trick:** "I want to tell you in advance — it's going to be horrible." Always gets more volunteers than he needs.

## 6. The Late-Night FM DJ Voice (Three Voice Modes)

How you say it matters more than what you say. There are **three voice tones**:

| Voice | When to use | How to deliver | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Late-Night FM DJ** | Selectively, when you need to make a point or assert calmly | Slow, low, downward inflection. Calm and reasonable. | Conveys "I have it covered." Creates aura of authority + trustworthiness without triggering defensiveness. |
| **Positive / Playful** | DEFAULT mode | Smile while talking (audible on phone too). Light, easygoing | Counterpart in positive frame thinks faster, problem-solves, collaborates. Mental agility increases. |
| **Direct / Assertive** | Almost never | Forceful | Signals dominance. Counterpart pushes back aggressively or passive-aggressively. "Slapping yourself in the face." |

A downward inflection says, "I'm in control, this isn't up for discussion." Upward inflection invites response and concedes the lead.

Voss bought his Toyota 4Runner at $30,000 (sticker $36,000) using late-night DJ + "How am I supposed to do that?" + "I'm sorry, I just can't" + silence.

## 7. Getting to "That's Right"

**Why "That's right" >>> "You're right":**

"You're right" is what you say to make a person stop bothering you. It signals fake compliance, leads to no behavior change. ("You're right, Dad" → goes back to playing the same way next series.)

"That's right" means the counterpart has internally affirmed your understanding of their world. It is a **stealth victory** — they embrace what you said as their own. Behavior change becomes possible.

**The path:**
**Listening → Paraphrase → Label → Summary → "That's right."**

Definitions:
- **Paraphrase:** Repeat what they said in *your own words.*
- **Summary:** Paraphrasing + labeling. Rearticulate meaning + acknowledge emotion behind it. "The world according to ___."

A *complete* summary of "the world according to your counterpart" leaves no possible response except "that's right."

The pharma rep won the doctor's account by summarizing his passion for treating patients (not pitching her drug) until he said "that's right." Then he asked for her product.

**Active listening tactics that drive toward "that's right":**
1. **Effective Pauses** — silence drains emotion from the dialogue.
2. **Minimal Encouragers** — "yes," "okay," "uh-huh," "I see."
3. **Mirroring**.
4. **Labeling**.
5. **Paraphrase**.
6. **Summarize** (paraphrase + label).

When you're delivering an idea you want them to embrace as theirs, design your summary to elicit "that's right."

## 8. "No" Is Better Than "Yes"

"No" starts the negotiation. "Yes" without "How" is meaningless.

**Why "No" is gold:**
- Triggers feelings of safety, security, control, autonomy. People will fight to the death for the right to say no.
- Lets the other side feel protected, so they relax and listen.
- Surfaces real concerns instead of cheap agreement.
- "No" is rarely literal rejection. It typically means one of:
  - I am not yet ready to agree.
  - You are making me feel uncomfortable.
  - I do not understand.
  - I don't think I can afford it.
  - I want something else.
  - I need more information.
  - I want to talk it over with someone else.

**The Three Yeses (must learn to distinguish):**

| Type | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Counterfeit Yes** | They plan to say no but find "yes" the easier exit, or want to keep the conversation going to extract more info. |
| **Confirmation Yes** | Reflexive affirmation to a black/white question. No commitment to action. |
| **Commitment Yes** | True agreement, leads to action. The signature on the contract. |

The three sound nearly identical. Most counterparts have become masters at the counterfeit yes because pushy salespeople have hammered them into it.

**Inviting "No" — phrasing flips:**
- ❌ "Do you have a few minutes to talk?" → ✅ **"Is now a bad time to talk?"**
- ❌ "Do you like to drink water?" → ✅ Whatever doesn't trigger the telemarketer reflex.
- ❌ "Can we agree on this?" → ✅ "Have you given up on this project?"
- "Let's talk about what you would say 'No' to."
- Forcing a corrective "No" via mislabeling: "It seems like you want this project to fail." → "No, that's not it. This is it."

**Reaffirm autonomy:** Give them "the right to veto" (Jim Camp).

After hearing "no," ask:
- "What about this doesn't work for you?"
- "What would you need to make it work?"
- "It seems like there's something here that bothers you."

**No "No" means no go.** If the counterpart cannot or will not say no, they are indecisive, confused, or hiding an agenda. Walk away.

## 9. Calibrated Questions

Open-ended questions that begin with **"What"** or **"How"** (rarely "Why"). They give your counterpart the illusion of control while you frame the conversation. Listener's judo.

**Why they work:**
- Acknowledge the other side without resistance.
- Force them to use *their* mental and emotional energy to solve *your* problem.
- Carry no target for attack — unlike statements.
- Educate the counterpart on the problem instead of telling them.
- Implicitly ask for help, which appeals to ego and triggers reciprocity.
- Suspend "unbelief" (Kevin Dutton) — you don't need to convince, just stop them disbelieving.

**Rules:**
- Only use **What**, **How**, and rarely **Why**.
- "Who," "When," "Where" elicit one-word facts. Avoid.
- "Why" is accusatory in every language. Treat like a hot stove. Only use when the defensiveness it triggers makes them defend a goal that serves *you*: "Why would you ever change from your existing supplier?"
- Avoid verbs that produce yes/no answers ("can," "is," "are," "do," "does").
- Pair with empathy and tone — these can land as accusation if you're not careful.

**Voss's go-to calibrated questions:**
- **"How am I supposed to do that?"** (the most powerful — gentle "no," forces empathy)
- **"What about this is important to you?"**
- **"How can I help to make this better for us?"**
- **"How would you like me to proceed?"**
- **"What is it that brought us into this situation?"**
- **"How can we solve this problem?"**
- **"What's the objective?"** / **"What are we trying to accomplish here?"**
- **"What is the biggest challenge you face?"**
- **"How does that affect things?"**
- **"What's the core issue here?"**
- **"How is that worthwhile?"**
- **"How does this fit into what the objective is?"**
- **"What would you be able to offer to make that a good price for me?"**
- **"How does this look to you?"** (instead of "Does this work?")
- **"What about this doesn't work for you?"**
- **"What caused you to do it?"** (instead of "Why did you do it?")
- **"How will we know we're on track?"**
- **"How will we address things if we find we're off track?"**

**Behind-the-table calibrated questions** (find the deal-killers):
- "How does this affect the rest of your team?"
- "How on board are the people not on this call?"
- "What do your colleagues see as their main challenges in this area?"

**Deal-killer questions:**
- "What are we up against here?"
- "What happens if you do nothing?"
- "What does doing nothing cost you?"
- "How does making this deal resonate with what your company prides itself on?"

## 10. The Ackerman Bargaining Model

Voss's go-to system for hard haggling. Named after Mike Ackerman, ex-CIA. Endorsed by Howard Raiffa. Originally for kidnap-for-ransom; works in business.

**The model:**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Set your **target** price (your goal). |
| 2 | First offer = **65%** of target. |
| 3 | Calculate three increasing offers: **85%, 95%, 100%**. |
| 4 | Use empathy + many ways of saying "No" between offers, to make them counter before you raise. |
| 5 | Final number: a **precise non-round** number ($37,893 not $38,000). Conveys deep calculation. |
| 6 | On the final offer, throw in a **non-monetary item** (something they don't really want) to signal you're at your limit. |

**Why it works psychologically:**
- 65% sets an extreme anchor → fight-or-flight reaction → bends their reality.
- Decreasing concessions (each is half the previous) → makes them feel they're squeezing every drop. People feel *better* after extracting concessions than after a single fair offer, even when they end up paying more.
- Reciprocity → your concessions trigger theirs.
- Odd numbers → sound thoughtful, immovable. ($1.99 vs $2.00 — same logic.)
- Non-monetary gift → reciprocity multiplier; makes "100%" feel like the ceiling.

**Voss's $4,751 ransom:** ($150,000 demand reduced via this exact pattern, plus a gifted "portable CD stereo" at the end.)

**Mishary's rent example:** Landlord asked $2,100. Mishary's target: $1,830. Bid sequence: $1,730 (anchor) → $1,790 → $1,810 → $1,829 — accepted.

## 11. Black Swans

**Definition:** Unknown unknowns — pieces of information neither side imagined would matter, but which change everything once revealed. In every negotiation Voss assumes there are 3–5 Black Swans on the table.

**The three types of leverage:**

| Type | Definition | How to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Positive** | Your ability to give or withhold something they want. | "Whenever they say 'I want…' you have leverage." Delay your offers. |
| **Negative** | Your ability to make them suffer (loss aversion). | Use sparingly. Threats are nuclear. Prefer to *label* the negative ("It seems you don't care what position you're leaving me in") rather than threaten. |
| **Normative** | Using their own stated norms/values/religion against their inconsistencies. | Speak their language back to them. No one wants to look like a hypocrite. |

**"Religion":** A counterpart's worldview — what the market, experts, God, or society have determined to be fair. People defer to authority. Speak the dialect of their religion and you gain influence (e.g., "stewardship" with the born-again CEO; "Dawn of the Third Day" with the Christian veteran Dwight Watson).

**How to find Black Swans:**
- **Get face time.** Especially the unguarded moments — first few minutes, last few minutes, interruptions, meals before/after the formal meeting. Reporters never turn off their recorders.
- **Listen, listen again, listen more.** Use a backup listener whose only job is to listen between the lines.
- **The Similarity Principle.** Find shared identity to gain trust.
- **Hopes and dreams.** Express passion for what they want and the path to it; people will follow you.
- **"Because" reasons.** Even idiotic ones increase compliance (Langer copy-machine study: 60% → 90%).
- **When they seem crazy, they aren't.** Three explanations:
  - **Ill-informed** — give them the missing data.
  - **Constrained** — legal/political/promised constraints they can't reveal.
  - **They have other interests** — hidden agendas (Griffin wanted suicide-by-cop, not money).
- **Mislabel intentionally** to provoke them to correct you and reveal data ("It seems the seller doubts the market" → "Actually, the seller has tougher properties in Atlanta and Savannah and needs to pay back mortgages" — Black Swan revealed).

## 12. Bend Their Reality

People are irrational. Use predictable irrationality patterns, especially **Prospect Theory** (Kahneman/Tversky):
- **Certainty Effect** — people prefer sure things over higher-EV probabilities.
- **Loss Aversion** — losses loom roughly 2x larger than equivalent gains.

Six tactics:

1. **Anchor their emotions first.** Run an accusation audit ("you're going to think I'm a lousy businessman…"), then pitch. The bad scenario you painted didn't happen, so your offer feels like relief. Voss cut $2,000/day contractors to $500 with no complaints by anchoring low first.

2. **Let the other guy go first on numbers — most of the time.** You may learn their first offer is better than your hoped close. Exception: if you know the market and they're a rookie, anchor extreme.

3. **Establish a range** (bolstering range). "At top places like X, this role pays between $130K and $170K." Columbia study: applicants who name a range get higher offers. Expect them to land at the low end.

4. **Pivot to non-monetary terms.** "Let's put price aside for a moment and talk about what would make this a good deal." "What else could you offer to make that price work for me?" Non-monetary items often cost the counterpart nothing but are valuable to you (the magazine cover story for the bar association training).

5. **Use odd, non-round numbers.** Round numbers feel like placeholders. $37,263 feels like the result of analysis.

6. **Surprise with a gift.** After staking an extreme anchor and getting the inevitable rejection, offer an unrelated, unexpected concession. Triggers reciprocity. (Sadat addressing the Knesset, 1977.)

**On compromise:** **Never split the difference.** Wearing one black shoe and one brown shoe is worse than either. Compromise is what we do to feel safe and save face — driven by fear, not goals.

**On deadlines:** Almost always self-inflicted, almost always flexible. Engage in the process; don't let the clock force a bad deal. Counter-intuitively, *revealing* your deadline often gets you a better deal (Don Moore research): hidden deadlines push you to concede while the other side waits. **No deal is better than a bad deal.**

**On "Fair" (the F-word):** Three uses:
- **Defensive judo:** "We just want what's fair." Triggers irrational concession from a guilt-stricken counterpart.
- **Manipulative attack:** "We've given you a fair offer." Distracts from substance. Defuse by mirroring: "Fair?" + label: "It seems like you're ready to provide the evidence that supports that."
- **Constructive (Voss's recommended):** Open with "I want you to feel like you are being treated fairly at all times. So please stop me at any time if you feel I'm being unfair, and we'll address it."

## 13. The 7-38-55 Rule

Albert Mehrabian (UCLA): when forming an attitude about someone, only **7%** of the message is the words. **38%** is tone of voice. **55%** is body language and face.

**Implication for negotiation:** Body language and tone are your most powerful assessment tools. Fly to meet in person when stakes matter. Distrust pure email/text.

**Use it to spot incongruence:**
- You: "So we're agreed?"
- Them: "Yes…"
- You: "I heard you say 'Yes,' but it seemed like there was hesitation in your voice."
- Them: "Oh, it's nothing really."
- You: "No, this is important. Let's make sure we get this right."

Surface and dissolve incongruence with labels — don't ignore it.

## 14. Body Language and Verbal Tells

- Words, music (tone), and dance (body) must align. When they don't, label the gap.
- Watch for changes in expression/posture/voice when external events (your statements) hit them.
- Look for fidgeting, downturned mouth corners, voice flattening on specific topics.
- **Most lying tells are verbal.** Liars use more words, more complex sentences, and more third-person pronouns. They work too hard at being believed.

---

## 15. Pinocchio Effect, Rule of Three, Pronouns

**Pinocchio Effect (Malhotra et al.):** Liars use *more* words, more *complex sentences*, and more *third-person pronouns* (he, she, they, it, one) to distance themselves from the lie.

**Rule of Three:** Get the same agreement three times in three different ways. It's hard to lie or fake conviction repeatedly.
1. First commitment.
2. Label or summarize the commitment so they answer "that's right."
3. Calibrated How/What about implementation: "What do we do if we get off track?"

Or: ask the same calibrated question three different ways:
- "What's the biggest challenge you faced?"
- "What are we up against here?"
- "What do you see as being the most difficult thing to get around?"

**Pronouns reveal authority and lying:**
- Heavy "I/me/my" → counterpart is *less* important; they're a front. (Or hiding nothing — reveals an inflated self-importance from low-power people.)
- Heavy "we/they/them" → counterpart is *more* important; deferring to people behind the table; or hiding behind a fictitious group (the Brooklyn bank robber Chris Watts blamed nonexistent "co-conspirators").

**The Chris Discount (using your own name):** Humanizes you. "My name is Chris. What's the Chris discount?" Forced empathy. Also defused the angry vet in the Kansas bar.

## 16. Yes-Ladder Dangers

The classic telemarketer "Yes pattern" script (Do you enjoy clean water? Yes. Do you want crisp water?... etc.) **does not work.** The buyer feels prey, defensive, weasels out later, gives a counterfeit yes.

Ben Ottenhoff's GOP fundraising study: replacing the "Yes pattern" script with a "No"-oriented script produced **23% higher** donation rate.

The early "Yes" is often:
- Counterfeit (escape route).
- Confirmation (reflex, no commitment).
- A signal that defensiveness has set in.

**Don't aim for "Yes" too quickly.** Start with "No"-oriented questions ("Is now a bad time?"). Drive toward "That's right" — not "yes." End with the commitment "Yes" backed by a "How."

## 17. Email and Text Negotiation Tactics

**The non-response email** (the magic one-sentence email):

> **Have you given up on this project?**

Why it works:
- "No"-oriented question → safe answer is "No," which forces engagement.
- Implicit threat to walk away, triggers loss aversion.
- Forces them to define their position and explain.
- Natural inclination: "No, our priorities haven't changed. We've just been bogged down…"

Voss has used this successfully in cultures (Arabic, Chinese) famous for never saying "No." It is direct but cloaked in safety. It is *not* rude — being ignored is rude.

**Other email/text rules:**
- Email gives the counterpart too much time to compose. Black Swans rarely emerge by email.
- For tough/tense subjects, push to a phone call or face time.
- When negotiating by email, scripted multi-step "No" responses (see Joaquin/Bruno €23,000 example below) work well because there is no real-time pressure.

## 19. Stories and Lessons

**1. Brooklyn Bank Robbery (Chase Manhattan, 1993, Chris Watts)**
First in-the-field hostage job. Lessons: Slow down. Use the late-night DJ voice. Mirror to make Watts vomit information ("We chased your driver away?" → admission of an unknown accomplice). Watch out for pronoun games — Watts pretended to have many co-conspirators when he had one. Don't step on your own mirror with a follow-up question.

**2. Harlem Apartment Fugitives (1998)**
Three fugitives barricaded behind a door, no phone, six hours of one-way labels: "It looks like you don't want to come out. It seems like you worry that if you open the door, we'll come in with guns blazing. It looks like you don't want to go back to jail." All three came out peacefully. Their explanation: "You calmed us down." Lesson: **Label the underlying fears (amygdala), not the surface** — it's not "you want us to let you go," it's "you don't want to die or go back to jail."

**3. Haiti Aunt Kidnapping ($150K → $4,751)**
Lessons: Compromise is a trap. Slow play to the kidnappers' Friday party deadline. Bend reality with extreme anchoring ($3,000 first offer), then odd numbers ($4,751), then a non-monetary gift (CD stereo). Aunt released safely.

**4. Schilling Kidnapping in the Philippines (Abu Sabaya)**
Sabaya demanded $10M in "war damages" citing 500 years of oppression. Voss had Benjie summarize Sabaya's worldview completely until Sabaya said "That's right." After that moment, Sabaya never mentioned money again. Lesson: A complete summary of "the world according to your counterpart" produces "That's right" — and that breaks deadlocks.

**5. Salary Negotiation (Angel Prado, MBA student)**
Lessons in one negotiation: Anchor non-specifically months ahead ($31K MBA → "becomes my raise"). Pivot to non-monetary first (new title, success metrics). Range with odd numbers ($134.5k–$143k). Let the boss negotiate against himself. Frame the boss as a *mentor* with skin in your success. Use "Fair" constructively. Result: $134,500 + boss saying "I'll fight to get you this salary."

**6. Voss's Toyota 4Runner ($36K → $30K)**
"How am I supposed to do that?" + "I'm sorry, this is so embarrassing, I just can't" + silence + repeat. Each time the salesman returned with a lower price. Any response that's not an outright rejection means you have the edge.

**7. Coca-Cola Stalled Deal**
Email "Have you given up on this project?" failed because the contact had an undisclosed constraint (he'd lost internal influence). Voss: "Get him to dinner — 'Would it be a bad idea for me to take you to your favorite steakhouse?'" Face time produced the Black Swan: the contact admitted he was the wrong guy and handed off to the right one. Took a year, but deal closed.

**8. Dwight Watson Tractor Standoff (Washington DC, 2003)**
The Black Swan was Watson's Christianity. Agent Winnie Miller, listening between the lines, suggested using "Dawn of the Third Day" (Christ's resurrection) as the surrender frame. Watson: "I'll come out." Lesson: **Know their religion** — find the worldview, the language, the norms that have authority over them.

**9. Joaquin/Bruno €23,000 Buyout**
Multi-step "No" via email. Each step: empathy + soft No + non-counter. Each round, Bruno's broker dropped the price (€30,812 → €28,346 → €25,000 → accepted €23,567 with split payments). Lesson: When the other side is desperate (revealed Black Swan: a bank loan recall), use the "No" series to get them bidding against themselves.

**10. Pittsburgh Drug Dealer Proof-of-Life Question**
Untrained drug dealer instinctively asked "Hey, dog, how do I know she's all right?" The kidnapper was so caught off-guard he volunteered to put the hostage on the phone. Lesson: **Calibrated questions get information without owing reciprocity.** "How" engages because "how" asks for help.

## 20. The One-Sheet Negotiation Prep Template

From the Appendix. The single document Voss and his clients prepare before any meaningful negotiation. **"You don't rise to the occasion — you fall to your highest level of preparation."** Five sections:

### Section I: The Goal
- Think through best/worst-case extremes.
- Write down only the **best-case, optimistic-but-reasonable** target. (Do *not* anchor your one-sheet on BATNA — it caps your aim.)
- Make it specific. Write it down. Discuss with a colleague (harder to wimp out). Bring the written goal into the room.

### Section II: Summary
- 1–2 sentences capturing the known facts that led to this negotiation.
- Must be a summary the counterpart will respond to with **"That's right."**
- Why are you here? What do you want? What do they want? Why?

### Section III: Labels / Accusation Audit
- Anticipate everything they could throw at you — fair or not.
- Distill to **3–5 labels** that pre-empt those accusations.
- Role-play. Plan pacing. It is theater.
- Use the fill-in-the-blank label templates (see §4).

### Section IV: Calibrated Questions
- **3–5 questions** to (a) reveal value to both sides, (b) identify and overcome deal-killers, (c) influence behind-the-table players.
- Categories to include:
  - **Discovery questions** (what motivates them — beneath stated positions).
  - **Behind-the-table questions** ("How does this affect the rest of your team?" / "How on board are the people not on this call?" / "What do your colleagues see as their main challenges?").
  - **Deal-killer questions** ("What are we up against here?" / "What happens if you do nothing?" / "What does doing nothing cost you?" / "How does making this deal resonate with what your company prides itself on?").
- Pair each question with a follow-up label ready to fire.
- Remember: a high % of negotiations hinge on **non-financial** drivers — self-esteem, status, autonomy, perceived loss. Find ways to make your counterpart look good in the face of change.

### Section V: Non-Cash Offers
- Inventory non-monetary items the counterpart has that would be valuable to you.
- Ask: **"What could they give that would almost get us to do it for free?"**
- Examples: introductions, exclusivity, magazine cover stories, references, longer terms, faster timelines, branding, mentorship, milestones, vacation days, titles, content rights.
- Build these into your final-stage Ackerman offer (the "non-monetary gift" at 100%).

## Key Principles to Internalize

1. **Negotiation = communication with results, not a battle.** Goal is discovery.
2. **Slow it down.** Speed kills rapport and trust.
3. **Subsume your ego.** The other side must feel the solution is theirs.
4. **Listening is the cheapest concession.** And the most powerful.
5. **The adversary is the situation, not the person.** Never make an enemy.
6. **No deal is better than a bad deal.** Be willing to walk. Don't be needy.
7. **The Golden Rule is wrong.** Treat them how *they* need to be treated, not how you'd want to be treated. There are 3 negotiator types (Analyst, Accommodator, Assertive) — 66% chance theirs ≠ yours.
8. **Yes is nothing without How.** Drive past agreement to implementation.
9. **There is always a team on the other side.** Influence the deal-killers behind the table.
10. **Embrace honest, regular conflict.** It's the only path to truth, creativity, and resolution. Avoidance kills marriages and deals alike.
