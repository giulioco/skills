# How to Win Friends & Influence People — Carnegie Extract

> Source: Dale Carnegie, *How to Win Friends and Influence People* (1936, rev. 1981). Extract for sales coaching use.

---

## 1. Core Thesis — Why Carnegie Still Works

Carnegie's psychology is older than psychology. The book sells because the underlying claims about human beings have not been falsified in ninety years.

- **Humans are not creatures of logic.** "When dealing with people, let us remember we are not dealing with creatures of logic. We are dealing with creatures of emotion, creatures bristling with prejudices and motivated by pride and vanity."
- **The deepest human craving is to feel important.** Freud called it "the desire to be great." Dewey called it "the desire to be important." William James upgraded it to a *craving* — "The deepest principle in human nature is the craving to be appreciated."
- **No one criticizes themselves.** Even Al Capone, "Two Gun" Crowley, and Dutch Schultz believed they were misunderstood public benefactors. If hardened killers do not blame themselves, your prospect won't either.
- **People are starved for sincere appreciation.** Most people never receive it. The salesperson who delivers it has access to a need as deep as hunger.
- **You cannot make anyone do anything.** "The only way I can get you to do anything is by giving you what you want." Persuasion is bait selection — strawberries-and-cream is what *you* like; the fish wants worms.
- **Carnegie's measure of success: 85% of financial success is "human engineering," 15% technical knowledge** (per the Carnegie Foundation studies he cites). For salespeople the ratio is even more lopsided.

The book is structured around four laws of human gravity — Handle, Like, Persuade, Lead. Every principle is a corollary of "people want to feel important and they decide with emotion."

## 2. Part 1 — Fundamental Techniques in Handling People

### Principle 1 — Don't criticize, condemn, or complain.

- **Why it works:** Criticism puts the other person on the defensive and makes them strive to justify themselves. It wounds pride, hurts the sense of importance, arouses resentment. B. F. Skinner showed reinforcement learning beats punishment learning. Hans Selye: "As much as we thirst for approval, we dread condemnation."
- **Carnegie's example:** Lincoln, after Meade let Lee escape over the Potomac, wrote a furious rebuke and *never sent it.* Lincoln learned from the Shields duel never to attack openly. "With malice toward none, with charity for all."
- **Sales application:**
  - Never tell a prospect they're wrong about a competitor, their current vendor, or their internal process. Even if true, you trigger justification mode and lose the deal.
  - When a prospect "ghosts" you, do not send the passive-aggressive breakup email. Criticism in writing rankles "across the decades."
  - When a deal slips, do not blame the champion. "I don't see how I could have done any differently from what I have" is what they will say to themselves.
  - Internal teams: never bash the CSM/SE/AE in the deal review. The Hoover-mechanic story — "I want you to service my F-51 tomorrow" — builds the loyalty that wins next quarter's deals.

### Principle 2 — Give honest and sincere appreciation.

- **Why it works:** Appreciation feeds the deepest craving. People will work, buy, marry, or follow whoever satisfies the hunger to feel important.
- **Carnegie's example:** Charles Schwab earned a million-dollar salary not because he knew steel — he had men under him who knew more — but because of his ability to arouse enthusiasm. "I am hearty in my approbation and lavish in my praise." Schwab's other line: "I have yet to find the person, however great or exalted his station, who did not do better work and put forth greater effort under a spirit of approval than he would ever do under a spirit of criticism."
- **Flattery vs. appreciation:** "One is sincere and the other insincere. One comes from the heart out; the other from the teeth out." Flattery is "telling the other person precisely what he thinks about himself." Sincere appreciation requires you to think about the other person's actual good points — which is what 95% of people don't do because they're thinking about themselves.
- **Sales application:**
  - Open meetings by recognizing something specific the buyer's team built or shipped — not the company's marketing, but a thing *they* personally drove. Specificity is the proof of sincerity.
  - When a prospect makes a sharp observation about their own market, name it: "That's the cleanest framing of that problem I've heard from a CFO this quarter." Only say it if you mean it.
  - For champions: praise them by name to their boss in writing. Most champions never get this from a vendor and will fight harder for the deal.
  - The Stevie Wonder origin story — a teacher told a blind boy he had remarkable ears — is what you want to *be* for your buyer's career. Recognize the talent they don't yet know they have.

### Principle 3 — Arouse in the other person an eager want.

- **Why it works:** "There is only one way under high heaven to get anybody to do anything... by making the other person want to do it." Henry Ford: "If there is any one secret of success, it lies in the ability to get the other person's point of view and see things from that person's angle as well as from your own."
- **Carnegie's example:** Carnegie's hotel raised his rent 300%. Instead of arguing, he listed the manager's "Advantages" and "Disadvantages" of the increase on a sheet of paper — emptier ballroom, lost cultured-audience advertising, no income at all if Carnegie left. Got the increase reduced to 50%. "Mind you, I got this reduction without saying a word about what I wanted."
- **Carnegie's other example:** Barbara Anderson, the bank manager moving to Phoenix, wrote 12 banks not about herself but about how her 10 years of experience could help *their* growth. 11 of 12 invited her to interview.
- **Sales application:**
  - Never open a cold email with what your company *desires.* The "Mr. Blank" radio-agency letter Carnegie shreds is every modern outbound email. "Who cares what your company desires?"
  - Reframe every feature into a buyer-side outcome. "We have an API" is your wants. "Your ops team stops the 4 PM data-entry shift" is theirs.
  - For deals stuck in committee, build the buyer-side advantages/disadvantages list *for them*. They will use your framing internally because it sounds like their own thinking.
  - The Mike Whidden / Shell Oil story — instead of telling a station owner to clean up, drive him to the new station, *let him want it himself.* Modern equivalent: customer reference visits, not pitch decks.
  - Salespeople fail because "they are always thinking only of what they want. They don't realize that neither you nor I want to buy anything... we are eternally interested in solving our problems."

## 3. Part 2 — Six Ways to Make People Like You

### Principle 1 — Become genuinely interested in other people.

- **Why it works:** "You can make more friends in two months by becoming interested in other people than you can in two years by trying to get other people interested in you." Adler: the person not interested in fellow men has the greatest difficulties in life.
- **Carnegie's example:** Theodore Roosevelt knew every White House servant by name and asked the kitchen maid Alice if she still made cornbread. Howard Thurston the magician whispered "I love my audience" before every show — over 60M tickets sold. Charles Walters got the bank interview by bringing stamps for the executive's 12-year-old son.
- **Sales application:**
  - Research before every call: not just LinkedIn title, but recent posts, podcast appearances, the kid's soccer team mentioned in a tweet. Reference specifically.
  - The "stamps for the son" move = sending a relevant book, a thoughtful Loom about something *they* posted, a referral that helps *their* career.
  - Remember birthdays, work anniversaries, kid names. "I was frequently the only person on earth who remembered."
  - Talk to the gatekeeper, the SDR, the procurement clerk like humans. Edward Sykes kept a J&J account because the soda clerk vouched for him to the owner.
  - The Knaphle / chain-store debate maneuver: instead of pitching the prospect, ask them for help on something *they* are an expert on. He went in to lose a debate, came out with a fuel order. Modern equivalent: "I'm working on a piece about how CFOs in your sector are thinking about [their problem]. Could you spare 20 minutes?"

### Principle 2 — Smile.

- **Why it works:** Actions speak louder than words. A smile says "I like you. You make me happy. I am glad to see you." James V. McConnell: "People who smile tend to manage, teach, and sell more effectively." William James: "Action seems to follow feeling, but really action and feeling go together; and by regulating the action... we can indirectly regulate the feeling." Smiling first creates the warmth that produces real warmth.
- **Carnegie's example:** Schwab told Carnegie his smile had been worth a million dollars. The NY stockbroker William Steinhardt — chronic grouch — committed to smiling for one week and reported "smiles are bringing me dollars, many dollars every day." Phone-power studies: your smile carries through the phone in your voice. Robert Cryer recruited a Purdue PhD over bigger competitors because his voice "sounded as if you were glad to hear from me."
- **Sales application:**
  - Smile before you dial. The voice carries it.
  - On video, the camera amplifies flat affect into hostility. Default state should be slight smile.
  - In-person: warmth on entry sets the price ceiling for the rest of the meeting. A buyer who feels grim during minute one will negotiate harder in minute fifty.
  - Internal: gatekeepers and assistants route calls based on whether they like talking to you. The "Eunice the cafeteria worker" story — she piled on the ham once he smiled and used her name — generalizes to every operations function in your customer's company.

### Principle 3 — Remember that a person's name is to that person the sweetest and most important sound in any language.

- **Why it works:** A name is the only word that belongs solely to that human. Using it is "a subtle and very effective compliment." Forgetting or misspelling places you "at a sharp disadvantage." Politicians know it: "To recall a voter's name is statesmanship. To forget it is oblivion."
- **Carnegie's example:** Jim Farley could call 50,000 people by their first names and put FDR in the White House. Andrew Carnegie named his Pittsburgh steel mill "Edgar Thomson Steel Works" — Thomson was president of Pennsylvania Railroad. Guess who bought rails? When fighting Pullman in sleeper cars, Carnegie suggested merging and naming it "Pullman Palace Car Company." Pullman: "Come into my room, let's talk it over."
- **Napoleon III's technique:** If he didn't catch a name: "So sorry, I didn't get the name clearly." Then "How is it spelled?" Repeated it through the conversation, wrote it down after, looked at it, tore up the paper.
- **Sales application:**
  - Use the prospect's name 2-3 times in the opening minutes — and never again in a way that sounds salesy. ("Sarah, the question I keep coming back to is...")
  - Name the deal after the customer. The "Pullman Palace Car" trick: name the internal Slack channel, the deal-room, the success plan after them. Build their company logo into your demo data.
  - For procurement / legal / IT / SE — the "support cast" — addressing them by name in a multi-stakeholder call signals you see them as people. They control the deal more than the C-suite admits.
  - The Sid Levy / Nicodemus Papadoulos story: pronounce hard names correctly. Most reps don't bother. Tears-in-the-eyes loyalty is the dividend.
  - Roosevelt with the Chrysler mechanic: thank the *junior* people on the customer team. They tell their boss who the good vendor is.

### Principle 4 — Be a good listener. Encourage others to talk about themselves.

- **Why it works:** "Few human beings are proof against the implied flattery of rapt attention." Charles W. Eliot of Harvard: "Exclusive attention to the person who is speaking to you is very important. Nothing else is so flattering as that." People talking are dropping problems, urgencies, and budget hints — but only if you stop talking long enough to hear it.
- **Carnegie's example:** Carnegie was called a "most interesting conversationalist" by a botanist at a dinner he barely spoke at. He just listened. The angry NY Telephone customer ranted for three hours over four interviews; the rep just listened and "yes"-ed; customer paid in full and withdrew his complaints. Detmer (Detmer Woolen) listened to a customer who had come to fire him, agreed with him, recommended competitors — got the largest order ever and a lifelong friend who named his son "Detmer."
- **Sales application:**
  - The discovery call must be 70%+ buyer talk-time. Reps who talk more than 50% close less. (Modern Gong/Chorus data confirms this.)
  - When a prospect gets emotional about an internal frustration — let them vent. Do not jump to solutioning. The vent is the buy signal. "He raved on and I listened for nearly three hours."
  - When a customer escalates an issue, the Detmer move: thank them for telling you, agree it's serious, side with them against your own org if needed. They will buy more, not less.
  - Lincoln invited his Springfield friend to DC to "discuss" the Emancipation Proclamation — talked at him for hours and never asked for advice. Sometimes your champion just needs to think out loud at you. Don't fill silence with your pitch.
  - Isaac Marcosson: "Many people fail to make a favorable impression because they don't listen attentively. They have been so much concerned with what they are going to say next that they do not keep their ears open."

### Principle 5 — Talk in terms of the other person's interests.

- **Why it works:** "The royal road to a person's heart is to talk about the things he or she treasures most." Roosevelt prepped the night before each visitor by reading on their hobbies. The point is not manipulation — the point is to give the *gift* of being seen.
- **Carnegie's example:** Edward Chalif wanted a corporate exec to fund a Boy Scout trip. He'd heard the exec had framed a million-dollar canceled check. Chalif asked to see it first thing — got six boys funded, $1,000 letter of credit, seven weeks in Europe, and ongoing job placements. Henry Duvernoy spent four years pitching bread to a hotel manager and failed; then he learned the manager was president of the Hotel Greeters association. One conversation about Greeters = the order.
- **Sales application:**
  - Pre-call research is not optional. Pull their LinkedIn posts, podcast appearances, recent quotes, board memberships. Open on *their* topic, not yours.
  - The "framed million-dollar check" equivalent today: the personal LinkedIn post that got 400 reactions, the talk they gave at a conference, the side project. Reference it specifically — not "I saw your post" but "the framing in slide 12 about agentic workflow ownership stuck with me because..."
  - For champions: their currency is internal political capital. Frame your product as something that elevates *them* in their org, not just solves their problem.
  - Harriman won a 20-year career by studying R.J. Funkhouser's gatekeeper *first*, learning her goals, helping her, and only then using her to access him.

### Principle 6 — Make the other person feel important — and do it sincerely.

- **Why it works:** "The unvarnished truth is that almost all the people you meet feel themselves superior to you in some way, and a sure way to their hearts is to let them realize in some subtle way that you recognize their importance." Disraeli: "Talk to people about themselves and they will listen for hours."
- **Carnegie's example:** Donald McMahon, the landscaper, complimented the attorney on the dogs that won blue ribbons at Madison Square Garden. The judge gave him a $400 puppy and an hour of his time. James Adamson got a $90,000 chair order from George Eastman by admiring the English oak panels in his office and asking about his early struggles — was supposed to take 5 minutes, took most of the day. Claude Marais kept a key employee from quitting by telling her in front of the entire staff: "you are as important to the success of this restaurant as I am."
- **Sales application:**
  - For senior buyers: ask about origin stories. "How did you end up running this org?" Most have never been asked. They will reveal motivations, fears, and timelines no discovery question would surface.
  - "Little phrases such as 'I'm sorry to trouble you,' 'Would you be so kind as to —?' 'Would you mind?' 'Thank you' — little courtesies oil the cogs of the monotonous grind of everyday life." Procurement remembers reps who said "thank you for your time" and meant it.
  - The David Smith / charity-booth move: when two people both think they're in charge, give one the cash box (symbol of authority) and the other a clear domain. In a deal with two competing internal stakeholders, give each ownership over a different success metric.
  - The Hall Caine origin: a son-of-a-blacksmith published an essay praising Rossetti's poetry and ended up Rossetti's secretary, then a multimillion-dollar literary career. Lesson: write a thoughtful piece about a future buyer's work *before* you ever try to sell to them.

## 4. Part 3 — How to Win People to Your Way of Thinking

### Principle 1 — The only way to get the best of an argument is to avoid it.

- **Why it works:** "Nine times out of ten, an argument ends with each of the contestants more firmly convinced than ever that he is absolutely right." Even if you win, you "make him feel inferior. You have hurt his pride. He will resent your triumph." Ben Franklin: "If you argue and rankle and contradict, you may achieve a victory sometimes; but it will be an empty victory because you will never get your opponent's good will."
- **Carnegie's example:** Patrick O'Haire, the truck salesman, was scrapping with prospects who insulted his trucks. Carnegie taught him to *agree* with prospects who praised competitors: "The Whose-It is a good truck. If you buy the Whose-It, you'll never make a mistake." It killed the argument and opened space to talk about White trucks. He became White Motors' star.
- **Frederick Parsons** had been arguing with a tax inspector for an hour over $9,000. He switched to "I suppose this is a very petty matter compared with the really important and difficult decisions you make. I've made a study of taxation myself, but I've had to get my knowledge from books. You are getting yours from the firing line of experience." Inspector talked about himself for hours, called three days later, and dropped the issue.
- **Sales application:**
  - When a prospect bashes your product or praises a competitor, agree first. "Sounds like Whose-It would be a fine choice for that use case." Removes the wall, then you can position differently.
  - In RFP debrief calls when you lost: do not argue the decision. "Makes sense. Thank you for explaining how you weighed it." You will be invited back for the next cycle. Reps who argue are blacklisted.
  - When a buyer's BATNA / current vendor / internal solution is mentioned, agree it has merits. Modern Challenger framing layers on top — but the *manner* must be Carnegie's.
  - "When two yells, there is no communication, just noise and bad vibrations." Tactical empathy in modern terms (Voss).

### Principle 2 — Show respect for the other person's opinions. Never say, "You're wrong."

- **Why it works:** "If you tell them they are wrong... you have struck a direct blow at their intelligence, judgment, pride and self-respect." James Harvey Robinson: it's not the ideas we cling to — it's our self-esteem. "The little word 'my' is the most important one in human affairs." We say *my* watch, *my* dinner, *my* God; doubt cast on any of these is felt as a personal threat.
- **Carnegie's example:** Ben Franklin, after an old Quaker scolded him, banned the words "certainly," "undoubtedly," etc. from his vocabulary. Adopted "I conceive," "I apprehend," "it appears to me at present." For 50 years no one heard a "dogmatical expression" from him — became one of the most influential men in America.
- **Katherine Allred:** failed to push a new yarn-incentive system by telling management they were wrong. Re-ran the meeting asking *them* where the problems were, used "low-keyed suggestions at proper intervals," let them develop her system themselves. Adopted enthusiastically.
- **R.V. Crowley:** stopped arguing with a lumber inspector who was incorrectly grading white pine. Instead asked questions, let the inspector slowly realize his own mistake. Saved the carload and got paid in full.
- **Sales application:**
  - "I may be wrong. I frequently am. Let's examine the facts." Magic phrase for objection-handling. Leaves the buyer's pride intact.
  - When a buyer makes a factually incorrect claim about your product, *never* "actually..." them. Instead: "That's a really common reading of how it works — let me share what I've seen and you can tell me if it lands."
  - In CFO/finance pushback on ROI math, don't argue the math. Walk through the assumptions and ask which one they'd flex.
  - Carl Rogers: "Our first reaction... is an evaluation or judgment, rather than an understanding." Listen *to understand* the prospect's frame before "correcting" it. You usually don't need to.

### Principle 3 — If you are wrong, admit it quickly and emphatically.

- **Why it works:** "Isn't it much easier to listen to self-criticism than to bear condemnation from alien lips?" Beat them to the punch. People want to play magnanimous when given the chance.
- **Carnegie's example:** The mounted policeman who'd already warned Carnegie about the unleashed dog caught him again. Carnegie blurted "I'm guilty. I have no alibis, no excuses." Cop went soft: "I think you are taking this a bit too seriously. Just let him run over the hill where I can't see him." Ferdinand Warren — a commercial artist — preempted an editor's criticism with "I'm ashamed of myself. I should have been more careful." Editor flipped to defending him; gave him another commission.
- **Robert E. Lee** after Pickett's Charge: "All this has been my fault. I and I alone have lost this battle." Could have blamed three division commanders. Didn't.
- **Sales application:**
  - When you missed a deadline, broke a SLA, or sent a wrong proposal: own it before the call starts. "I dropped the ball on the timeline. I should have caught it. Here's what I'm doing to fix it." Most reps over-explain or deflect — this destroys trust faster than the original error.
  - In QBRs where churn risk is the topic: don't defend. "You're right that the Q3 implementation didn't deliver what we promised. Here's what I think happened, and here's the plan to make Q4 different." Customers buy more from vendors who admit honestly.
  - Internal: when you blow a forecast, lead with self-criticism. Managers who hear "we missed because procurement, legal, security, etc." trust the rep less than one who says "I miscalled the timeline; here's the gap in my discovery."
  - "By fighting you never get enough, but by yielding you get more than you expected."

### Principle 4 — Begin in a friendly way.

- **Why it works:** Lincoln: "A drop of honey catches more flies than a gallon of gall." Woodrow Wilson: "If you come at me with your fists doubled, I think I can promise you that mine will double as fast as yours." Aesop's sun-and-wind: gentleness gets the coat off; force just makes him grip tighter.
- **Carnegie's example:** John D. Rockefeller Jr., after the Colorado Fuel & Iron strike (men killed, troops called out), opened a speech to strikers with: "This is a red-letter day in my life... we meet here not as strangers, but as friends." Strikers went back to work without their wage demand. Daniel Webster prefaced arguments with "It will be for the jury to consider," "This may perhaps be worth thinking of" — never bulldozed.
- **O.L. Straub** got rent reduced where every other tenant failed: opened with how much he loved the building, complimented the landlord, then said he couldn't afford the rent. Landlord *offered* the reduction unprompted.
- **Sales application:**
  - Never open a price negotiation by attacking the price. Open with what you appreciate about the relationship. *Then* introduce the constraint.
  - In escalations, the first 30 seconds set the ceiling for the resolution. Warmth in those seconds is worth thousands of dollars in concessions.
  - When walking into hostile rooms (deal-review-from-hell, lost-customer save calls), spend the first 5 minutes on common ground and shared goals, not on the issue. The "spirit of mutual friendship" framing.

### Principle 5 — Get the other person saying "yes, yes" immediately. (The Secret of Socrates.)

- **Why it works:** Once a person says "No" their entire neuromuscular system "sets itself on guard." Pride demands consistency. A "Yes" puts them in "forward-moving, accepting, open" posture. Socrates' method was a string of small agreements until the opponent embraced a conclusion they would have denied at the start.
- **Carnegie's example:** James Eberson, bank teller, almost lost a depositor who refused to fill out forms. Switched to "Suppose you have money in this bank at your death — wouldn't you like the bank to transfer it to your next of kin?" "Yes." Got every form filled out plus a trust account.
- **Joseph Allison** at Westinghouse: motors "too hot." Didn't argue. Asked: "If motors run too hot you shouldn't buy them, right?" Yes. "NEMA standard is 72°F above room temp, right?" Yes. "Mill room is 75°F?" Yes. "75 + 72 = 147°. Would you scald your hand at 147°?" Yes. "Better keep your hands off the motors then." $35,000 order.
- **Sales application:**
  - Discovery questions designed to elicit "yes": "Is operational efficiency a top priority this year?" "Is your team measured on X?" "Would solving X by Q2 be a win?" Each yes builds momentum.
  - In objection handling: never confront the No directly. Find the underlying premise that the buyer *will* say yes to, and build back up.
  - Modern label: "calibrated questions" (Voss). The lineage runs straight from Socrates through Carnegie to NSTD.

### Principle 6 — Let the other person do a great deal of the talking.

- **Why it works:** "They know more about their business and problems than you do." La Rochefoucauld: "If you want enemies, excel your friends; but if you want friends, let your friends excel you."
- **Carnegie's example:** G.B.R. lost his voice the day of a $1.6M upholstery contract pitch. Wrote "Gentlemen, I have lost my voice" on a pad. The president of the customer company *did the pitch for him*, advocating his samples. Won the contract. "I would have lost the contract if I hadn't lost my voice." Charles Cubellis got the job by asking the founder to tell his origin story; founder talked for an hour, then hired him.
- **Roy Bradley** hired Richard Pryor by *letting Pryor talk himself into the job* — Bradley nearly interrupted multiple times but didn't.
- **Sales application:**
  - On every discovery call, target ≤30% talk-time for yourself. Top performers cluster at 43% (Gong data).
  - When a buyer is venting, do not redirect. Let it flow. The 7th frustration they mention is the real budget driver.
  - When negotiating, silence after stating your position is your strongest tool. Most reps fill the silence with concessions.
  - Henrietta the recruiter: stopped boasting about her placements, started asking colleagues about theirs — became the most-liked person in the agency. Same dynamic with internal teams that close deals with you.

### Principle 7 — Let the other person feel that the idea is his or hers.

- **Why it works:** "We have much more faith in ideas that we discover for ourselves than in ideas that are handed to us on a silver platter." Emerson: "In every work of genius we recognize our own rejected thoughts; they come back to us with a certain alienated majesty."
- **Carnegie's example:** Eugene Wesson failed for years selling sketches by pushing what *he* thought a buyer should want. Switched to bringing *unfinished* sketches and asking "Won't you tell me how we could finish them up so you could use them?" Bought every one after that. The X-ray manufacturer wrote a hospital director: "They are not perfect... we'd be obligated if you could give us your ideas about how they can be made more serviceable." Director felt the buy-decision was his own.
- **Colonel House** influenced Wilson by planting ideas casually so Wilson would later trot them out as his own — and House gave Wilson public credit for them.
- **Sales application:**
  - In late-stage deals, hand champions a draft business case with deliberately-incomplete sections. "What numbers would your CFO want here?" They co-author and then defend it internally as theirs.
  - Pricing: present 2-3 options and let the buyer pick the structure. Their own choice will be defended internally; your "recommendation" forced on them will be re-litigated.
  - Adolph Seltz turned around a discouraged sales team by writing on the blackboard *what they expected from him*, then asking what he had a right to expect from them. Co-created accountability. Same move with a champion's stakeholder map.
  - Lao Tzu: "wishing to be above men, putteth himself below them." Reps who win big want credit; reps who win biggest let their champion take credit.

### Principle 8 — Try honestly to see things from the other person's point of view.

- **Why it works:** "There is a reason why the other man thinks and acts as he does. Ferret out that reason — and you have the key to his actions." Goode: "Realize that everybody else in the world feels exactly the same way" about their own concerns as you do about yours.
- **Carnegie's example:** The park-fires kids — Carnegie first ordered them to put fires out (sullen compliance, fires reignited later). Switched to: "Having a good time, boys? What are you cooking? I loved to build fires when I was a boy. You boys don't mean any harm, but other boys aren't so careful..." Got compliance and goodwill.
- **Sales application:**
  - Before every cold outreach, write 2 sentences answering: "Why has this person ignored 50 outreach emails this week?" That answer is your opener.
  - Before every QBR, write down the customer's top 3 internal pressures. Address those, not your roadmap.
  - In multi-threaded deals: each stakeholder sees a different problem. The CFO's view is not the CRO's view is not the IT director's view. Map them separately.

### Principle 9 — Be sympathetic with the other person's ideas and desires.

- **Why it works:** "Three-fourths of the people you will ever meet are hungering and thirsting for sympathy. Give it to them, and they will love you." Magic phrase: "I don't blame you one iota for feeling as you do. If I were you I would undoubtedly feel just as you do."
- **Carnegie's example:** A Colonial Dame in Philadelphia sent Carnegie scorching letters about a geographical error in his radio broadcast. He called her, apologized, sympathized — she ended up apologizing to him and asking to know him better. Sol Hurok handled Chaliapin (the temperamental basso) by rushing to him "dripping with sympathy" when he refused to sing — got him on stage every time. Jay Mangum got an 8-hour escalator shutdown approved by *first* sympathizing with the hotel manager's desire to keep guests happy.
- **Sales application:**
  - When a buyer says "we don't have budget" — never argue. "Of course. With everything else on your plate this quarter, that makes sense. If I were you I'd say the same thing." Then explore.
  - "Self-pity for misfortunes real or imaginary is in some measure practically a universal practice." (Dr. Arthur Gates) Every buyer has a story of being burned by a vendor. Ask about it. Sympathize *first*. Differentiate *second*.
  - The "I would feel exactly as you do" phrase is precisely Chris Voss's "tactical empathy" — labeled 80 years before Voss codified it.

### Principle 10 — Appeal to the nobler motives.

- **Why it works:** J. Pierpont Morgan: "A person usually has two reasons for doing a thing: one that sounds good and a real one." All people have a high regard for themselves and like to be fine and unselfish in their own estimation. Appeal to the noble version of themselves.
- **Carnegie's example:** Hamilton Farrell told a tenant breaking his lease: "I sized you up as a man of your word... we are either men or monkeys." Tenant came back and paid his rent. Northcliffe asked a paper not to publish a photo by saying "My mother doesn't like it." Cyrus Curtis got Louisa May Alcott to write for low pay by routing payment to her favorite charity.
- **James Thomas** at the auto company: 6 customers refused to pay disputed bills. He approached each saying "you know more about your car than anyone — I'm leaving the bill in your hands. Whatever you say goes." 5 of 6 paid in full. All 6 bought new cars within two years.
- **Sales application:**
  - Closing-line move: "I trust you'll make the right call for your team." Hands the ethical/professional standard to the buyer to live up to.
  - When negotiating concessions, frame your concession as evidence of trust ("I'm doing this because I believe you'll be a great long-term partner") not as bargaining weakness.
  - Late-payment collection: assume the customer is honest. Most people, given the chance to be the noble version of themselves, will be.

### Principle 11 — Dramatize your ideas.

- **Why it works:** "Merely stating a truth isn't enough. The truth has to be made vivid, interesting, dramatic." Movies do it. TV does it. Window displays do it.
- **Carnegie's example:** The Philadelphia Bulletin clipped one day's editorial content into a 307-page book to dramatize how much news it printed — killed the rumor it was all ads. Jim Yeamans (NCR) walked into a grocery, said "you're literally throwing pennies away," and threw a handful of pennies on the floor. Got the order. James Boynton dumped 32 jars of cold cream on a cosmetics exec's desk, each tagged with research findings — replaced an argument with an hour-long conversation.
- **Sales application:**
  - In demos, show, don't tell. The screenshot of *their* data running through your product beats slides about your platform.
  - In ROI conversations, calculate the cost of doing nothing and write it on a napkin. "$2.4M / quarter sitting on the table."
  - For competitive deals, bring physical artifacts. A printed customer reference quote on real paper held in their hand has more weight than a logo wall on a slide.
  - Mary Catherine Wolf got a meeting with her boss by sending him a self-addressed reply form with checkboxes. Modern equivalent: 1-click meeting links framed as a pre-decided menu, not an open-ended ask.

### Principle 12 — Throw down a challenge.

- **Why it works:** Charles Schwab: "The way to get things done is to stimulate competition... in the desire to excel." Frederic Herzberg's research: the #1 motivator is the work itself — the game, the chance to prove worth, to win. Money was *not* it.
- **Carnegie's example:** Schwab walked into a struggling steel mill, asked the day shift their heat count (six), chalked a "6" on the floor. Night shift saw it, replaced with "7." Day shift came back with "10." The mill jumped from worst to best in the plant. Al Smith got Lewis Lawes to take the Sing Sing wardenship by saying "It'll take a big person to go up there and stay" — Lawes ran it for decades.
- **Sales application:**
  - For champions: frame the deal as their proving moment internally. "If you ship this by EOY, you'll be the team that drove the biggest infrastructure modernization in your division's history."
  - In multi-region rollouts, expose the competition between regions transparently. "Region A is at 80% adoption — what would it take for Region B to beat them by Q4?"
  - For executives sitting on the fence: appeal to their legacy/peer-comparison instinct. Not "you should do this" but "the leaders ahead of the curve on this are X, Y, Z."

## 5. Part 4 — Be a Leader: How to Change People Without Giving Offense

### Principle 1 — Begin with praise and honest appreciation.

- **Why it works:** "It is always easier to listen to unpleasant things after we have heard some praise of our good points." A dentist applies Novocain before drilling.
- **Carnegie's example:** Coolidge praised his secretary's dress before mentioning her punctuation. McKinley stopped a Republican from giving a bad speech by calling it "splendid" — then rewrote it together. Lincoln's letter to General Hooker — "I believe you to be a brave and skillful soldier... and yet there are some things in regard to which I am not quite satisfied with you." W.P. Gaw won over a delayed bronze subcontractor by complimenting his unique surname before ever raising the schedule issue.
- **Sales application:** When delivering bad news to a customer (price increase, service degradation, missed deadline), open with sincere appreciation. The pre-delivery praise must be specific and true.

### Principle 2 — Call attention to people's mistakes indirectly.

- **Why it works:** "But" negates the praise. Replace "but" with "and." Subtle suggestion beats direct correction.
- **Carnegie's example:** Schwab didn't tell smoking employees to read the no-smoking sign. He handed each a cigar and said "I'll appreciate it if you smoke these on the outside." Wanamaker, finding salespeople ignoring a customer, silently served her himself — they got the message. Marge Jacob cleaned up the construction debris herself, then thanked the foreman the next morning for a clean lawn.
- **Sales application:** For internal teams (SE, CSM) underperforming on a deal: don't say "you missed X *but* did Y well." Say "great work on Y *and* if we add X next time...". For customers misusing the product: instead of "you're using it wrong," show the cleaner workflow with a praise hook on what they're already doing right.

### Principle 3 — Talk about your own mistakes before criticizing the other person.

- **Why it works:** "It isn't nearly so difficult to listen to a recital of your faults if the person criticizing begins by humbly admitting that he, too, is far from impeccable."
- **Carnegie's example:** Carnegie corrected his niece Josephine's mistakes by recalling his own at her age. E.G. Dillistone showed his secretary his own personal spelling-difficulty notebook before pointing out her errors. Clarence Zerhusen told his teenage son about being hooked on cigarettes himself — son never started smoking.
- **Sales application:** When coaching reps or critiquing a champion's deck: open with "I made this exact mistake when I was running my own deal — let me show you." When customers escalate, share a comparable failure your own team had and what you learned. Vulnerability earns trust.

### Principle 4 — Ask questions instead of giving direct orders.

- **Why it works:** "A technique like that saves a person's pride and gives him or her a feeling of importance. It encourages cooperation instead of rebellion."
- **Carnegie's example:** Owen D. Young never gave direct orders in three years of being observed. "You might consider this," "Do you think that would work?", "Maybe if we phrased it this way it would be better." Ian Macdonald asked his team how they could make an impossible delivery date — they came up with the ideas and made it happen.
- **Sales application:** Forecast calls: instead of telling reps what to do, ask "If you were the buyer, what's the next email you'd want to receive?" Champion enablement: "What slide would you want to walk into your CFO meeting with?" The asking *is* the coaching.

### Principle 5 — Let the other person save face.

- **Why it works:** Saint-Exupéry: "I have no right to say or do anything that diminishes a man in his own eyes... Hurting a man in his dignity is a crime."
- **Carnegie's example:** GE made Steinmetz "Consulting Engineer" — a new title for work he was already doing — to remove him from running a department where he'd failed, without humiliating him. Marshall Granger fired seasonal accountants by praising specific assignments they'd done well, then explaining the seasonal nature. Anna Mazzone made a major test-marketing error; her boss in front of all colleagues said "your lack of experience, not lack of ability" caused this.
- **Sales application:** When closing a deal where a champion got a feature request rejected by your product team: never say "your priority wasn't strategic enough." Frame it as "your timing on this is ahead of the roadmap — when it ships in '26, you'll be the first to validate." When a customer cancels: thank them for what they tried, never publicly blame them for poor adoption.

### Principle 6 — Praise the slightest improvement and praise every improvement.

- **Why it works:** Jess Lair: "Praise is like sunlight to the warm human spirit; we cannot flower and grow without it." Animal trainers use meat, not whips. Skinner: minimize criticism, emphasize praise — the good things get reinforced and the poor ones atrophy.
- **Carnegie's example:** Caruso (told he had no voice — his mother insisted he could sing). Dickens (one editor's praise of one accepted story changed his whole life). H.G. Wells (a schoolmaster's praise saved him from a lifetime in a dry goods store). Keith Roper turned around a struggling young printer with one specific compliment about a single job.
- **Sales application:** Specific praise > general flattery. "Your slide 4 framing of the cost-of-delay is the best I've seen all year" beats "great deck." For new reps: praise the first sign of improvement publicly, in a deal review or Slack channel — not just one-on-one. Buyers too: "you're the first prospect this week who pushed back on slide 7 — that's exactly the right question."

### Principle 7 — Give the other person a fine reputation to live up to.

- **Why it works:** Shakespeare: "Assume a virtue, if you have it not." Samuel Vauclain: "The average person can be led readily if you have his or her respect and if you show that you respect that person for some kind of ability."
- **Carnegie's example:** Henry Henke turned around a slipping mechanic by reminding him of his past excellence. Georgette Leblanc told "Marie the Dishwasher" she had "treasures within her" — Marie cleaned herself up and married into respect. Mrs. Hopkins, fourth-grade teacher, told "bad boy" Tommy: "I understand you're a natural leader. I'm depending on you." Tommy became the model student.
- **Sales application:** Tell your champion "you're the most strategic thinker on the buying committee" — they will rise to it. Tell a stalled procurement officer "I've worked with a lot of buyers, and you're one of the most thorough I've seen on actually reading the contract" — they'll move faster, not slower. Bill Parker re-approached a rejecting buyer with: "You're always willing to listen and big enough to change your mind when facts warrant." Buyer reopened the meeting.

### Principle 8 — Use encouragement. Make the fault seem easy to correct.

- **Why it works:** "Be liberal with your encouragement, make the thing seem easy to do, let the other person know you have faith in his ability... and he will practice until the dawn comes in the window in order to excel."
- **Carnegie's example:** The dance teacher who told Carnegie's friend his fundamentals were fine, just needed a few new steps — kept him learning. Ely Culbertson became the world's bridge expert because Josephine Dillon told him he was a potential genius at the table. Clarence Jones used flash cards and stopwatch celebrations to turn a son labeled "brain damaged" into a National Honor Society student.
- **Sales application:** When a champion says "I can't get my CFO to sign off in 30 days," don't agree it's hard. Say "Three of our last five deals had the exact same constraint — here's the one-pager that got it done." For new logos worried about implementation: "Your team is more technical than the average customer who got this live in 6 weeks. You'll be ahead of them."

### Principle 9 — Make the other person happy about doing the thing you suggest.

- **Why it works:** Napoleon: "Men are ruled by toys." Titles, recognition, framing the request so the other person sees their own benefit creates compliance without resentment.
- **Carnegie's example:** Wilson appointed William Gibbs McAdoo to Treasury by framing it as McAdoo doing *him* a favor. Gunter Schmidt fixed his sloppy-pricing employee by making her "Supervisor of Price Tag Posting." Mrs. Gent stopped boys cutting through her lawn by deputizing the worst offender as her "detective."
- **Sales application:**
  - Be sincere. Don't promise what you can't deliver.
  - Know exactly what you want them to do.
  - Be empathetic — what does *the other person* really want?
  - Match the benefit to their want.
  - Frame the ask so they see their own benefit.
  - The Ben Franklin "ask a small favor" move (Part 5): asking your prospect for advice on something they're an expert on disarms them and makes you a friend. Albert Amsel split open a hostile plumber's account by asking him for advice on a Queens Village location. The plumber lectured, became friends, started buying.

## 7. The Dark Side — Where Carnegie Misleads

Carnegie is necessary but not sufficient for modern complex sales. Pure relationship-building falls short in three predictable ways.

### 7.1 The Challenger Critique

Dixon & Adamson's CEB study (*The Challenger Sale*, 2011) found that the lowest-performing sales archetype in complex B2B is the "Relationship Builder" — the Carnegie disciple who is liked by everyone, never argues, and never pushes. The highest performer is the "Challenger" who *teaches the customer something they didn't know about their own business*, *tailors* the message to stakeholder economics, and *takes control* of the conversation — including respectful tension and reframe.

Carnegie says: never tell anyone they are wrong. The Challenger says: your job is to tell sophisticated buyers their assumptions are incomplete — that's the value they pay for. Both are right; the resolution is *manner*. You can challenge the *idea* while honoring the *person*.

### 7.2 The "Tell Me About Yourself" Trap

Carnegie elevates listening to the highest discipline. Modern data (Gong) shows top reps still talk 43% of the time — not 30%. Why? Because in complex deals the buyer often does not know what they don't know. Pure listening leaves them stuck in their own frame. Carnegie underweights the salesperson's role as *educator* and *frame-setter*.

### 7.3 The Sympathy Loop

"Be sympathetic with the other person's ideas and desires" can become enabling. If a CFO says "we have no budget," sympathizing forever produces no deal. Voss's tactical empathy is specifically designed to *use* empathy as a lever to surface the deeper objection — not to validate the surface one. Carnegie's framing risks producing reps who are universally liked and universally unable to close.

### 7.4 The Sincerity Loophole

Carnegie repeats throughout that flattery is poison and only sincere appreciation works. Modern outbound at scale has, predictably, weaponized "sincerity" — every cold email opens with "I loved your post on..." Buyers' BS detectors have evolved. Specificity, brevity, and a willingness to be less liked are the modern moat. Carnegie's tools work; their *ubiquity* has raised the bar on what counts as sincere.

### 7.5 Nothing About Pipeline Math

Carnegie says nothing about prospecting volume, activity discipline, pipeline coverage, or the structural reality of B2B sales: most deals don't close, and reps win by getting at-bats. Jeb Blount's *Fanatical Prospecting* is the antidote. Carnegie is the *quality* book; you still need a *quantity* book.

### Both/And

The complete sales operator runs Carnegie at the *human* layer (warmth, listening, importance, sincerity) and Challenger/Voss/SPIN/Gap at the *deal* layer (insight, tension, calibrated questions, problem-centric discovery). Reps who choose only one fail predictably: pure-Carnegie reps are loved and never close; pure-Challenger reps win with sophisticated buyers and burn warm relationships; pure-Voss reps tactical-empathy themselves into manipulation accusations.

## 9. Retellable Stories for Coaches

### Story 1 — Lincoln's Unsent Letter to Meade

After Gettysburg, Meade let Lee escape over the swollen Potomac. Lincoln, furious, wrote a blistering rebuke. Then he set it aside and never sent it. He realized that if Meade had felt the screams of the wounded, he might have done the same. Found unsent in his papers after his death.
**Coaching point:** Write the angry email. Save it as a draft. Send it 24 hours later, or better, never. Most "necessary" criticism is necessary only to your ego.

### Story 2 — The Mechanic and Bob Hoover's F-51

Test pilot Bob Hoover's plane lost both engines at 300 feet because a mechanic had filled it with jet fuel instead of gasoline. Hoover landed it, walked back, found the mechanic in tears. Instead of yelling, he put his arm around him: "To show you I'm sure you'll never do this again, I want you to service my F-51 tomorrow."
**Coaching point:** When someone screws up a deal, an implementation, a forecast — they already know. Punishing them adds nothing and costs loyalty. Trust them with the next one.

### Story 3 — Schwab Chalking "6" on the Floor

Charles Schwab walked through a struggling steel mill, asked the day shift how many heats they'd run that day. "Six." Without a word he chalked a "6" on the floor. The night shift saw it, made it "7." Day shift came back and made it "10." Mill went from worst to best in the company.
**Coaching point:** Throw down a visible, peer-comparable challenge. The work itself motivates more than money. Make the scoreboard visible and let pride do the work.

### Story 4 — Andrew Carnegie's Edgar Thomson Steel Works

Carnegie wanted to sell rails to the Pennsylvania Railroad, run by J. Edgar Thomson. He named his Pittsburgh mill "Edgar Thomson Steel Works." Guess where Pennsylvania Railroad bought rails? Same trick — when battling George Pullman for sleeper-car business, Carnegie proposed a merger named "Pullman Palace Car Company." Pullman: "Come into my room. Let's talk."
**Coaching point:** A person's name is the sweetest sound. Name the deal-room, the channel, the success plan, the case study after them. Architectural recognition is more powerful than verbal flattery.

### Story 5 — The Stamps for the Executive's Son

Charles Walters needed information from a tight-lipped exec. Saw a secretary mention the exec's 12-year-old was collecting stamps. Walters returned the next day with foreign stamps from his bank's mail. "He couldn't have shaken my hand with more enthusiasm if he had been running for Congress." Got the report, the data, the answers — without asking again.
**Coaching point:** The thing they care about most is rarely the thing on their LinkedIn. Find the personal angle and lead with it — but only if you genuinely care, not as a hack.

### Story 6 — The Westinghouse Motors Too Hot

Joseph Allison spent 13 years failing to sell Westinghouse motors. Finally got an order. Three weeks later: "I can't buy any more — they run too hot." Instead of arguing, Allison agreed: "If they run too hot, you shouldn't buy them." Then walked through NEMA standards: "Mill room is 75°F, allowed temp rise is 72°, total 147°. Would you scald your hand at 147°?" Yes. "Then keep your hands off the motors." $35,000 order.
**Coaching point:** Never argue an objection head-on. Build a string of small Yes's that lead the buyer to the conclusion. Socratic, not adversarial.

### Story 7 — G.B.R.'s Lost Voice and the $1.6M Contract

A sales rep, G.B.R., showed up to pitch a $1.6M upholstery contract with laryngitis. Wrote on a pad: "Gentlemen, I have lost my voice." The president of the customer company offered to do the pitch *for him* — and won the contract. "I would have lost the contract if I hadn't lost my voice — because I had the wrong idea about the whole proposition."
**Coaching point:** When you can't talk, the buyer must. When the buyer talks, they sell themselves. Plan deliberate silence into every discovery call.

### Story 8 — Eugene Wesson's Unfinished Sketches

Eugene Wesson failed for years selling sketches by pushing his finished work. Switched to bringing *unfinished* sketches: "Won't you tell me how we could finish them so you could use them?" Buyer: "Leave these for a few days." Came back, took the suggestions, finished the sketches accordingly. All accepted. Years of repeat business.
**Coaching point:** Give the buyer authorship of the solution. A draft business case with deliberate gaps invites co-creation. The version they help build is the version they defend internally.

### Story 9 — Detmer Woolen and the Customer Who Came to Quit

Julian Detmer's credit team had been hounding a customer over a disputed bill. Customer traveled to Chicago specifically to fire Detmer in person. Detmer listened patiently, agreed credit had annoyed him, said the charge would be wiped, recommended *competing* woolen houses. Took him to lunch. Customer placed his largest order ever, found a misfiled bill at home, sent payment with apology, named his son "Detmer," remained loyal for 22 years.
**Coaching point:** The customer who comes to fire you is the customer most loyal to you if you exceed their expectation in the next 5 minutes. Disarm with agreement. Recommend a competitor. Their pride won't let them leave.
