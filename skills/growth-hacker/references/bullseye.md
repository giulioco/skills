# Bullseye Framework (Level 3: Channel Selection)

The single most important framework in *Traction*. Solves the founder's hardest growth question: **which one channel will move the needle next?**

> *"At any stage in a startup's lifecycle, one traction channel dominates in terms of customer acquisition."* — Weinberg & Mares

> *"Poor distribution — not product — is the number one cause of failure. If you can get even a single distribution channel to work, you have a great business. If you try for several but don't nail one, you're finished."* — Peter Thiel

> *"More wood behind fewer arrows."* — Larry Page

The metaphor: aim for the bullseye — the one channel that unlocks your next growth stage. The framework is iterative: rinse, repeat after each phase.

## The 5 steps

### Step 1 — Brainstorm
Come up with at least one reasonable idea for **every one of the 19 channels**. The point is to systematically counteract your channel biases. **Do not dismiss any channel.**

Research what worked for similar companies in your industry. Equally valuable: research what *unsuccessful* companies wasted marketing dollars on — that tells you which channels are saturated or wrong-fit.

Format the brainstorm as a spreadsheet:

| Channel | Idea | Probability it works (1–5) | Expected CAC | Customers available before saturation | Time to test |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

The 19 channels — see `channels/index.md` for the full list with one-line summaries.

### Step 2 — Rank into 3 columns

- **Column A (Inner Circle):** channels that seem most promising right now. Usually 3–5.
- **Column B (Potential):** channels that could plausibly work. Usually 5–8.
- **Column C (Long-shot):** channels that seem like a stretch right now (but may matter at a later stage).

The drop-off in conviction usually happens around the third channel. If you have 10 in column A, you haven't critiqued hard enough.

### Step 3 — Prioritize: pick three from column A
Run them in **parallel**, not sequentially. Tests take time after setup, so parallel is faster overall. **More than three causes errors from lack of focus; one is too slow** to find the winner.

### Step 4 — Test cheaply

Each test answers three questions:
1. Roughly what does it cost to acquire customers through this channel?
2. How many customers are available?
3. Are these the customers you want *right now*?

**Critically: you are NOT trying to get a lot of traction yet. You are determining if it's a channel that *could* work.** Speed > perfection.

- Run **4 Facebook ads, not 40**.
- $250 on AdWords gives a rough SEM read.
- *"You should be able to get a rough idea of a channel's effectiveness with just a few hundred dollars."*
- Limited resources mean you can't optimize ten ads at once — *that is optimization, not testing*.

You need a tracking/reporting system in place before testing (Sean Ellis: don't start until it exists). Otherwise you can't read the results.

### Step 5 — Focus

Direct **all** resources toward the single channel that produced promising results. Continually experiment to wring every bit of traction out of it until saturation/rising costs flatten the curve. Then re-run Bullseye.

This is where founders fail most often after the first round — they keep dabbling in column B/C channels instead of going all-in on the winner. Don't.

## Bullseye in the wild — Mint

Goal: 100k users in 6 months.

- **Inner circle:** Targeting Blogs, PR, SEM.
- **Step 4 tests:** sponsored a small newsletter, reached out to financial celebrities like Suze Orman, ran some Google ads.
- **Step 5: Targeting Blogs won.** Sponsoring mid-level financial bloggers + guest posting got the first **40,000 customers**.
- When that maxed, they re-ran Bullseye and found PR.
- **Result: 1M users within 6 months of launch. Acquired by Intuit for $170M.**

## Why founders fail at Bullseye (the comfort trap)

Founders consider only channels they're already familiar with or that match their product type:

- **Engineers** default to SEO/content (don't understand distribution, spray "everything but the kitchen sink").
- **Ex-marketers** default to Facebook/Google ads (the most crowded, lowest-ROI channels for early stage).
- **Ex-salespeople** default to cold outbound (often not the channel a self-serve product needs).

Three bias modes block channels:
1. **Out-of-sight/out-of-mind** (e.g., speaking engagements, trade shows — invisible if you don't attend them).
2. **Refusal to consider channels viewed negatively** (sales, affiliates — felt as cheesy).
3. **Bias against schlep** — channels that seem annoying/time-consuming (BD, trade shows, customer appreciation programs).

The Bullseye brainstorm step exists specifically to surface these. **Force yourself to write one credible idea per channel before ranking.**

Closing repeated theme of the book: **untapped beats comfortable.** The channels your competitors aren't using are usually the ones with the best ROI per hour of work.

## The 50% Rule

Spend **50% of time on product, 50% on traction, in parallel.** Andreessen: *"The number one reason that we pass on entrepreneurs we'd otherwise like to back is their focusing on product to the exclusion of everything else."*

The split is hard because the pull to spend everything on product is strong, and splitting will slow product development *in the short run*. It will not slow time-to-market. Benefits:

1. **Better product.** Traction work surfaces messaging that resonates, niches to target first, easiest customer types, distribution roadblocks. Dropbox tested SEM during product development, found it cost $230 to acquire on a $99 product, pivoted to viral and built the referral program *into the product*. That referral program has been their biggest growth driver since.
2. **Tested channels at launch day.** Marketo had SEO and a blog running before product was built — they "beta tested an idea" instead of beta testing a product, and had **14,000 interested buyers** when product shipped.
3. **Addresses both major risks at once:** market risk (can you reach customers sustainably) and product risk (do they want it).

**The Product Trap:** the fallacy that the best use of your time is always improving your product. *"If you build it, they will come" is wrong.* Bullseye is to traction what Lean is to product — they work hand-in-hand.

## The Critical Path / Single Traction Goal

> *"You should always have a traction goal you're working towards."*

One number — e.g., 1,000 paying customers, 100 new daily users, 10% of your market. It must be **particularly significant** to your company: would reaching it make you profitable, raise more easily, become market leader?

DuckDuckGo's progression:
1. Get to product/market fit
2. → 100M searches/month (break-even)
3. → 1% of search market (entrenched player)

Each took roughly two years.

**The Critical Path is the path to that goal with the fewest number of steps.** Literally draw it out, enumerate intermediate milestones (need not be traction-related, but must be **absolutely necessary**). Order them by dependencies and find the shortest path. Then: *"work on the first step(s) and **nothing else**."*

Re-assess after each milestone using market knowledge gained — your original plan is often wrong.

**The decision rule:** *"Everything you do should be assessed against your Critical Path. Every activity is either on path or not. **If it is not on the path, don't do it!**"*

DuckDuckGo's iOS team's path: ship iOS 7 update for desktop parity. Once their mobile team realized "get featured in the App Store" was the next milestone, they **promptly stopped activity on press, ads, and other app traction strategies because they were no longer on path.**

### Moving the Needle

Focus only on activities with *measurable, significant impact* — not blips. Early on, small things move the needle (one tweet from a respected person, a meetup speech to a few hundred). At 10,000 visitors/day, a tweet sending 200 visitors is invisible. To add 100k customers at 1–5% conversion you need to reach **2–10 million people** — that's why community building and viral scale better in late stages.

> *"What worked before usually will not get you to the next level."*

Channels rejected as long-shots earlier may be worth re-examining each Bullseye cycle. Quantify: include CAC and LTV columns. Ask "does this channel have enough users to be meaningful?" — back-of-envelope math before exploring.

## Channel choice by phase

| Phase | What it means | Channels that work | Channels that don't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Phase I — making something people want** | Pre-PMF; first few customers | Unscalable hustle: hand-recruiting, manual outreach, founder pitches, small communities, targeted blogs | PR (no story yet), large paid (no LTV data), trade shows (low ROI before scale) |
| **Phase II — marketing something people want** | Post-PMF; ramping to sustainability | SEM, content marketing, viral loops, social ads, community, email, BD, SEO compounds | Sometimes outgrown: Phase I unscalable hustle |
| **Phase III — scaling** | Established model and market position | PR, large paid, BD, community building, trade shows, offline events, speaking | Phase I/II low-volume tactics |

**Some channels work early but fail later** (small-volume tactics).
**Some are hard in Phase I but become major in later phases** — PR is the canonical example.
**Community building and viral scale with userbase size** — they get more powerful as you grow.

## The Law of Shitty Click-Throughs (Andrew Chen)

> *"Over time, all marketing strategies result in shitty click-through rates."*

Banner ads launched at 75% CTR. All channels saturate as competitors pile in. Defense:
1. Constantly run small experiments to find the next untapped strategy.
2. Be **early to a new platform** (Zynga on Facebook before competition arrived). Chris Dixon: *"Some of the most successful startups grew by making bets on emerging platforms that were not yet saturated and where barriers to discovery were low. Betting on new platforms means you'll likely fail if the platform fails, but it also dramatically lowers the distribution risks."*
3. Re-run Bullseye when your current channel flattens.

## When to switch / when to double down

The closing chapters reinforce two heuristics:

1. **Run cheap parallel tests, then double down on the one that works.** Trade-show example: visit as a guest *before* exhibiting. Mini-conference: $500 university lecture hall before committing to a flagship. AppSumo bundle: Lifehacker test before building a sales motion. The pattern: smallest credible test → measure → escalate.
2. **Switch channels when the channel saturates.** Stick to a working channel until the unit economics break, then move. Don't switch out of boredom; switch when the math forces you.

## Diagnostic questions for the founder

1. "Have you written down one credible idea for each of the 19 channels? If not, that's step 1."
2. "Which 3 channels are in your column A right now — and which would your competitors say are theirs?"
3. "What channel produced your last 100 paying customers, and is it still producing them?"
4. "What's the smallest test you can run on each of your 3 column-A channels for under $1k each?"
5. "Are you violating Thiel's rule — trying for several channels but not nailing one?"

The answer "we're growing through a mix of channels" is almost always wrong. Almost always one is doing the work; identify which and double down.
