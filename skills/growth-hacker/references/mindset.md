# Mindset (Holiday's closing argument)

This file collects the mindset and quotes that distinguish a growth hacker from a marketer. Use it when the user is in a "what should I really be doing differently" mood, or when a specific recommendation needs a quote that lands.

## What differentiates a growth hacker from a marketer

Holiday's most-quoted formulation:

> *"A growth hacker is someone who has thrown out the playbook of traditional marketing and replaced it with only what is testable, trackable, and scalable. Their tools are e-mails, pay-per-click ads, blogs, and platform APIs instead of commercials, publicity, and money. While their marketing brethren chase vague notions like 'branding' and 'mind share,' growth hackers relentlessly pursue users and growth — and when they do it right, those users beget more users, who beget more users."*

Other framings worth preserving verbatim:

> *"Marketing has always been about the same thing — who your customers are and where they are."* — Noah Kagan

> *"A growth hacker doesn't see marketing as something one does, but rather as something one builds into the product itself."*

> *"Marketing, too many people forget, is not an end unto itself. It is simply getting customers. And by the transitive property, anything that gets customers is marketing."*

> *"Growth hacking fundamentally reduces the costs of being wrong, giving us freedom to experiment and try new things."*

> *"Once you break out of the shackles of antiquated notions of what is or isn't marketing, the whole field becomes cheaper, easier, and much more scalable. The game changes forever."*

## The mindset is the differentiator (not technical chops)

A growth hacker mindset has five marks:

1. **Treats the product itself as a marketing surface** (you can change it).
2. **Optimizes for measurable signups/conversions**, not impressions or "buzz."
3. **Refuses to pay full price** for what can be earned with cleverness.
4. **Iterates endlessly** through the four steps (PMF → growth hack → virality → retention) rather than running discrete campaigns.
5. **Owns the full funnel** — acquisition, activation, virality, retention — instead of handing the customer off after lead-gen.

## On product-market fit

> *"The race has changed. The prize and spoils no longer go to the person who makes it to market first. They go to the person who makes it to Product Market Fit first. Because once you get there, your marketing efforts become like a spark applied to a bed of kindling soaked in kerosene. The old way? It's striking a match . . . and hoping it starts a fire somewhere."* — Holiday

> *"Today, it is the marketer's job as much as anyone else's to make sure Product Market Fit happens."* — Holiday

> *"Do whatever is required to get to product/market fit. Including changing out people, rewriting your product, moving into a different market, telling customers no when you don't want to, telling customers yes when you don't want to, raising that fourth round of highly dilutive venture capital — whatever is required."* — Marc Andreessen

> *"People [who are] thinking about things other than making the best product, never make the best product."* — Phil Libin (Evernote)

## On finding the growth hack

> *"It doesn't matter how many people know about you or how they find out about you. It matters how many sign up. If handing out flyers on the street corner accomplishes that, then consider it growth hacking."* — Holiday

> *"They got to mass market by ignoring the urge to appeal to the mass market, at least to start with."* — Holiday

> *"Let's be honest, a traditional marketer would not even be close to imagining the integration above [Airbnb-Craigslist]. There's too many technical details needed for it to happen. As a result, it could only have come out of the mind of an engineer tasked with the problem of acquiring more users from Craigslist."* — Andrew Chen

> *"The more innovative your product is, the more likely you will have to find new and novel ways to get at your customers."* — Patrick Vlaskovits

## On virality

> *"Virality isn't luck. It's not magic. And it's not random. There's a science behind why people talk and share. A recipe. A formula, even."* — Jonah Berger

> *"Virality is not an accident. It is engineered."* — Holiday

> *"Virality at its core is asking someone to spend their social capital recommending or linking or posting about you for free. The best way to get people to do this enormous favor for you? Make it seem like it isn't a favor."* — Holiday

> *"Making things more observable makes them easier to imitate, which makes them more likely to become popular. We need to design products and initiatives that advertise themselves and create behavioral residue that sticks around even after people have bought the product or espoused the idea."* — Jonah Berger

## On retention

> *"Retention trumps acquisition."* — Bronson Taylor

> *"What's the point of driving a bunch of new customers through marketing channels if they immediately leak out through a hole in the bottom?"* — Holiday

> *"The focus needs to be on improving customer retention. If a company lacks growth, it should invest in refining and improving the service itself until users are so happy that they can't stop using the service."* — Eric Ries

> *"You're better off rolling out new features that get more out of your customer base, that turn potential users into active users, than going out and pounding the pavement for more potentials."* — Holiday

> *"This doesn't seem like marketing at all. How could a feature inside the service — the Twitter suggested user list — be considered marketing? But if it drives better user adoption it is."* — Holiday

> *"Whatever your current state is, it can be better."* — Sean Beausoleil (Mailbox)

## On distribution being the #1 cause of failure

> *"Poor distribution — not product — is the number one cause of failure. If you can get even a single distribution channel to work, you have a great business. If you try for several but don't nail one, you're finished."* — Peter Thiel (via Ellis)

> *"More wood behind fewer arrows."* — Larry Page (via Ellis)

> *"I rarely see startups fail because they didn't have a good idea or build a decent product. 90% fail because they can't reach their customers."* — Rand Fishkin (via Weinberg)

## On testing tempo

> *"Tempo of testing is itself the strategy."* — Sean Ellis

> *"Over time, all marketing strategies result in shitty click-through rates."* — Andrew Chen (the Law of Shitty Click-Throughs)

## On staying humble about prediction

> *"I have zero ability to predict what's going to work."* — Jason Cohen (Smart Bear / WPEngine, on offline ads)

> *"If you build it, they will come" is wrong.* — Weinberg paraphrasing the universal traction-truth.

## The Ogilvy lens (Holiday's closing analogy)

Before David Ogilvy was a famous adman, he sold ovens door-to-door. Holiday says he *"never forgot that advertising is just a slightly more scalable form of creating demand than door-to-door sales."*

This is the deep mindset of growth hacking: **the function under the form**. Anything that gets you a customer is marketing. Everything else is theater.

When you're stuck on a tactic, ask: *"What is the door-to-door version of this?"* That usually reveals the actual mechanism. A flyer, a personal email, a manual outreach. Then ask: *"How can I make that scalable, testable, trackable?"* That's the growth hack.

## When to pull this file

Use these quotes when:
- The user is rationalizing a comfortable channel choice → Page, Thiel, Fishkin.
- The user wants to skip PMF and "just market it" → Holiday's spark/kerosene line.
- The user is overinvesting in acquisition while retention leaks → Taylor, Ries, Holiday.
- The user thinks virality means a share button → Berger, Chen.
- The user is tired of the cadence and wants a magic bullet → Ellis on tempo, Cohen on humility.

A well-placed quote does the work of a paragraph of argument. Use sparingly but deliberately.
