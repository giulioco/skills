# Trade Shows

## Definition
Industry events where vendors show off products in person — usually exclusive to industry insiders, designed to foster vendor-prospect interactions.

## When it works
- **Every phase**: pre-product (learn from prospects), launch (announcements), scale (close big customers, secure partnerships).
- **Tight industry community + high AOV** justifies travel + booth costs.
- Brad Feld's worked example: $5k product, $10k show, 30% of 10k attendees = profitable around the third sale.

## When it doesn't
- Consumer products with no industry-specific gathering.
- Low AOV (<$500/yr) where booth ROI math fails.
- B2C self-serve.

## Tactics

### Pick events (Brad Feld 5 steps)
1. Set goals.
2. List every event.
3. Evaluate each vs. goals.
4. Annual budget by quarter.
5. Work backward from quarterly budget per event.

### Visit as a guest first
The year before whenever possible. Lets you scout, learn the rhythm, and evaluate without committing booth budget.

### Pre-event meetings (Jason Cohen, Smart Bear / WPEngine)
*"Set up meetings. Yes, meetings!"* Editors of online + offline magazines, bloggers, customers, prospects in trial, vendors, competition, partners.

Email subject: *"At [Trade Show X]: Can we chat for 5 minutes?"* **Aim for 5/day.**

### Inbound + outbound booth strategy
RJ Metrics: only **28% of conversations were walk-ups**; outbound walking the floor was **3–4× more productive**.

### Pre-mailed discount cards (Jason Cohen)
Send before the show, redeemable at booth.

### Memorable creative giveaways
Yo-yos, coconuts, cigar lighters > generic mugs/pens. The cost is the same; the memorability is 10×.

### CTA on every handout
Custom URL like yoursite.com/tradeshow, mobile-optimized, lets you measure show traffic + builds email list.

### Compelling demo at the booth
SlidePad's bike-brake before/after video is the canonical example — visceral, specific, shows the problem and the solution in 30 seconds.

### Dinners (Mark Suster trick)
Anchor with one **brand-name guest**, name them on invites, fill from there. Or invite **3–4 customers + 3–4 prospects + 2–3 employees** — prospects love talking to existing customers.

### Co-host events with 2 other startups to split costs.

## $1k / 1-week test
1. Attend the year's marquee show as a **guest, not exhibitor**.
2. Schedule **5 meetings/day** with editors + prospects + partners.
3. Send pre-mailed discount cards if you can.
4. Use CTA tracking on any materials.
5. Decide whether to exhibit next year based on this data.

## Key metrics
- Booth conversations (split inbound/outbound)
- CTA URL hits
- Meetings booked
- Deals progressed
- Press coverage from event presence

## Pitfalls
- **Spending on a booth without a demo or outbound strategy** — you'll get walk-ups but no real engagement.
- **Chasing walk-up traffic** when target deals would close better through 1-on-1 dinners.
- **Skipping the visit-as-a-guest year** and committing booth $$ blind.

## Companies + their plays
- **SlidePad** — early prototype show → spec feedback → manufacturing partnership with Jamis (biggest source of traction).
- **Smart Bear** — Jason Cohen pre-mailed discount cards.
- **WPEngine** — pre-event meetings as primary tactic.
- **RJ Metrics** — outbound booth strategy (3–4× more productive than inbound).
- **Grasshopper** — David Hauser hosts customer dinners at every event.
