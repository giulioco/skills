# Community Building

## Definition
Investing in connections among your users, fostering them and helping them bring others in. The output: **evangelists**.

## When it works
- The underlying value of your business is "**bringing people together**" or where users have shared interests/needs.
- **Phase I seeding** (manual, hand-curated) → **Phase III mature** (community runs itself, generates assets).
- Examples: Yelp, Wikipedia, reddit, Stack Overflow, GitHub, Codecademy, Startup Digest, Quibb.

## When it doesn't
- Chris McCann: *"If I were doing something with advertising and retargeting, it might be hard to build a community around that."*
- Products that don't naturally connect users (most ad-tech, most B2B middleware).

## Tactics

### Build initial audience first
- **Wikipedia** ← Nupedia user group.
- **Stack Overflow** ← Joel Spolsky + Jeff Atwood's blogs (**7,000 votes on the *name* before launch**).
- **Startup Digest** ← Chris McCann emailing 22 friends, then 20-second pitches at every event he attended → **250k members today**.

### Establish a mission
*"We had a manifesto, and an idea of what we wanted to accomplish. People bought into the vision because it was about *them* being awesome."* — Atwood.

### Foster cross-connections
Atwood's biggest mistake fix: *"I didn't see the need for a Meta… I was telling the people who loved Stack Overflow the most to f**k off and go away."* **Every Stack Exchange site now launches with a Meta.** Build forums, events, user groups so your evangelists can find each other.

### Communicate personally
Alexis Ohanian emailed every blogger who wrote about reddit, mailed shirts/stickers, ran an open-bar tour for redditors.

### Be transparent
Stack Overflow built the site users wanted by soliciting feedback at every step.

### Ensure quality
- **Strict launch guidelines** (Stack Overflow)
- **Karma/voting** (reddit)
- **Invite-only curation** (Quibb)
- **City-curator content** (Startup Digest)
- **Editorial standards** (Wikipedia)

*"Focus on quality early on and set standards that can be maintained as the community grows."* Communities decay when quality dilutes.

### Build assets
**Wikipedia's articles, Yelp's reviews, Codecademy's lessons** — all user-generated; the community *is* the product.

### Open source as community magnet (Tom Preston-Werner, GitHub)
Free for open-source projects → goodwill + free advertising → developers default to GitHub on side projects.

### Inbound hiring (DuckDuckGo)
Hire from your community — they already buy in. Cheaper, better-fit, and pre-loyal.

## $1k / 1-week test

1. Email **22 friends**, get the first cohort.
2. Set up a forum + a Meta-equivalent (where users can talk *about* the community).
3. Define **explicit mission + 5 quality rules**.
4. **Personally email every active user** thanking them.
5. Track participation + cross-connections (DMs/replies between members).

## Key metrics
- Active contributors
- Evangelist count
- **Content/asset created per active user**
- Retention of top 1%
- Cross-connection density (DMs/replies)
- Community-sourced hires (a Phase III metric)

## Pitfalls
- **Letting quality dilute as you grow** → downward spiral.
- **Not having a Meta** — losing your most engaged users (Atwood's "biggest mistake").
- **Trying to build community around products that don't naturally connect people** (advertising tech).
- **Treating the community as a marketing channel only** — it's a product.

## Companies + their plays
- **Wikipedia** — Nupedia seed, strict editorial.
- **reddit** — karma, Ohanian's personal touch, open-bar tour.
- **Stack Exchange** — manifesto + Meta + strict launch rules.
- **Startup Digest** — 22-friend email seed → 250k.
- **Quibb** — invite-only curation.
- **GitHub** — free for OSS.
- **DuckDuckGo** — inbound hiring from community.
- **Yelp** — reviews-as-asset.
- **Codecademy** — lessons-as-asset.
